## Identify all 206 individual bones and their locations within the body

# The 206 Bones of the Body

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Identify all 206 individual bones and their locations within the body.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The adult skeleton has 206 named bones, and although the number seems daunting, organising them by region turns a long list into a navigable map. Learning them by region lets you locate and name any bone you assess. Today we work through the 206 bones and their locations, region by region.

- By the end of this session you will be able to organise the 206 bones by region.
- You will be able to list the bones of the axial skeleton.
- You will be able to list the bones of the appendicular skeleton.
- You will be able to connect the skeletal map to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: A Map Learned by Region, Not by Rote

The organising idea is that the 206 bones are best learned as a map organised into the axial and appendicular divisions and their regions, so that each bone has a place rather than being an isolated fact. Grouping by region tames the number. This idea of a regional map frames the whole objective.

- The 206 bones divide into the axial and appendicular skeletons.
- Each division breaks into regions that group the bones.
- Learning the bones by region makes the number manageable.
- Each bone has a location within this regional map.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Filing System With Labelled Drawers

To keep the map concrete, picture a filing cabinet with two main sections, each holding several labelled drawers, and each drawer holding a manageable set of files. The two sections are the axial and appendicular skeletons, the drawers are the regions, and the files are the individual bones. Learning the drawers before the files keeps the 206 organised, and the image keeps the regional structure in view.

- The two main sections correspond to the axial and appendicular skeletons.
- The labelled drawers correspond to the regions such as the skull or the hand.
- The files in each drawer correspond to the individual bones.
- The analogy breaks down in that files are independent while bones are connected into one working framework.

---

## The Axial Skeleton: Skull and Associated Bones

Begin with the axial skeleton, starting at the head. The skull dominates this region.

- The skull comprises eight cranial bones, including the frontal, two parietal, two temporal, occipital, sphenoid, and ethmoid bones.
- The face comprises fourteen bones, including the maxillae, mandible, zygomatic, nasal, and other bones.
- Six auditory ossicles, three in each middle ear, and the single hyoid bone in the neck complete this region.
- Together the skull and associated bones protect the brain and form the face.

---

## The Axial Skeleton: Vertebral Column and Thoracic Cage

Next the rest of the axial skeleton, the spine and the chest. These support and protect the trunk.

- The vertebral column has twenty-six bones in the adult, comprising the cervical, thoracic, and lumbar vertebrae, the sacrum, and the coccyx.
- There are seven cervical, twelve thoracic, and five lumbar vertebrae.
- The thoracic cage comprises twelve pairs of ribs and the single sternum.
- Together the vertebral column and thoracic cage support the trunk and protect the spinal cord, heart, and lungs.

---

## The Appendicular Skeleton: Upper Limb and Girdle

Now the appendicular skeleton, beginning with the upper limb. It hangs from the pectoral girdle.

- The pectoral girdle comprises the clavicle and scapula on each side.
- The arm and forearm comprise the humerus, radius, and ulna on each side.
- The hand comprises eight carpals, five metacarpals, and fourteen phalanges on each side.
- Together the upper limb and its girdle provide reach and manipulation.

---

## The Appendicular Skeleton: Lower Limb and Girdle

Finally the lower limb, which bears weight and moves the body. It attaches through the pelvic girdle.

- The pelvic girdle comprises the two hip bones, each formed from the fused ilium, ischium, and pubis.
- The thigh and leg comprise the femur, patella, tibia, and fibula on each side.
- The foot comprises seven tarsals, five metatarsals, and fourteen phalanges on each side.
- Together the lower limb and its girdle bear weight and produce locomotion.

---

## Why the Skeletal Map Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

Knowing the bones and their locations is fundamental to musculoskeletal assessment. Each point below connects the map to practice.

- Naming bones precisely lets you document and communicate the site of injury or pain unambiguously.
- Palpable bones are the landmarks for your assessment, measurement, and treatment.
- The limb bones are the ones you most often assess and rehabilitate after injury.
- The vertebral column is central to posture, back pain, and spinal rehabilitation.
- A confident skeletal map underpins accurate assessment of any region.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the skeletal count, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to the filing system with labelled drawers.

- It is a mistake to forget the small bones, since the ossicles, carpals, and tarsals make up much of the count.
- It is a mistake to count the sacrum and coccyx as many separate bones in the adult, since they are fused.
- It is a mistake to place the girdles in the axial skeleton, because they are appendicular.
- It is a mistake to treat the list as unlearnable, because grouping by region makes it manageable.

---

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Listing the bones region by region aloud is the strongest test.

- Organise the 206 bones into the axial and appendicular divisions from memory.
- List the bones of the skull and vertebral column.
- List the bones of the upper and lower limbs and their girdles.
- State how many vertebrae there are in each region of the spine.
- Relate the skeletal map to a physiotherapy-relevant region.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have mapped the 206 bones by region. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The 206 bones divide into the axial and appendicular skeletons.
- The axial skeleton is the skull and associated bones, the vertebral column, and the thoracic cage.
- The appendicular skeleton is the pectoral girdle and upper limb and the pelvic girdle and lower limb.
- The spine has seven cervical, twelve thoracic, and five lumbar vertebrae plus the sacrum and coccyx.
- Learning the bones by region makes the number manageable and underpins assessment.
