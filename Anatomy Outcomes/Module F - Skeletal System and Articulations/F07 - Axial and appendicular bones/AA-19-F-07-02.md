# Major Bone Markings on Individual Bones

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Identify major bone markings (e.g., spines, processes, foramina) on individual bones.

## Where We Are Going Today

Having learned the vocabulary of bone markings, we now locate the major named markings on real bones, especially the palpable landmarks you use in assessment. Knowing where key markings sit turns the skeleton into a set of usable reference points. Today we identify major bone markings on individual bones.

- By the end of this session you will be able to locate major markings on the bones of the limbs.
- You will be able to identify key palpable landmarks used in assessment.
- You will be able to relate markings to the muscles and structures they serve.
- You will be able to connect the markings to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Named Landmarks That Guide Assessment

The organising idea is that specific bones carry named markings that serve as reliable landmarks for muscle attachment, palpation, and measurement, so that identifying them turns bones into a map of clinical reference points. The markings are where soft tissues attach and where you place your hands. This idea of landmarks guiding assessment frames the topic.

- Individual bones carry specific named markings.
- Many markings are palpable and serve as clinical landmarks.
- Markings indicate where muscles attach or where structures pass.
- Identifying them turns the skeleton into a map of reference points.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Landmarks on a Familiar Route

To keep the markings concrete, picture navigating a city by its landmarks, a particular tower, a bridge, a well-known corner, each a fixed point you use to orient and give directions. The bones' markings are anatomical landmarks used the same way for orientation and reference. The image keeps the idea of fixed reference points in view.

- A prominent tower corresponds to a large palpable process like the greater trochanter.
- A well-known corner corresponds to a landmark like the lateral epicondyle.
- Using landmarks to give directions corresponds to using bony points to localise findings.
- The analogy breaks down in that city landmarks are fixed structures, whereas bony markings develop in response to the muscles and forces acting on the bone.

---

## Markings of the Upper Limb Bones

Begin with the upper limb, which carries many palpable landmarks. These guide shoulder, elbow, and wrist assessment.

- The scapula carries the spine, the acromion, and the coracoid process, key attachment and landmark points.
- The humerus carries the greater and lesser tubercles, the deltoid tuberosity, and the medial and lateral epicondyles.
- The epicondyles of the humerus are palpable landmarks and common sites of tendon problems at the elbow.
- The radius and ulna carry the olecranon at the elbow and the styloid processes at the wrist.

---

## Markings of the Lower Limb Bones

Next the lower limb, which carries the landmarks of hip, knee, and ankle assessment. Several are key palpable points.

- The femur carries the head, neck, greater and lesser trochanters, and the condyles at the knee.
- The greater trochanter is a major palpable landmark for hip assessment.
- The tibia carries the tibial tuberosity, the medial malleolus, and the condyles at the knee.
- The fibula carries the lateral malleolus at the ankle, a key palpable landmark.

---

## Markings of the Axial Bones

Finally some key markings of the axial skeleton, used in spinal and trunk assessment. These orient you on the trunk.

- The vertebrae carry spinous processes, transverse processes, and the vertebral foramen for the spinal cord.
- The spinous processes are palpable down the midline of the back and used as landmarks.
- Foramina in the skull and between vertebrae transmit nerves and vessels.
- The intervertebral foramina transmit the spinal nerves emerging from the cord.

---

## Why Bone Markings Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

Locating markings underlies palpation, muscle attachment reasoning, and localisation of findings. Each point below connects the markings to practice.

- Palpable landmarks such as the greater trochanter and malleoli are reference points for your assessment and measurement.
- The epicondyles and tuberosities mark tendon attachments that are common sites of injury.
- Spinous processes guide your localisation and manual assessment of the spine.
- Foramina transmit nerves whose compression causes clinical problems you assess.
- Identifying markings lets you locate structures and findings precisely.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors weaken landmark knowledge, and naming them keeps it reliable. When unsure, return to landmarks on a familiar route.

- It is a mistake to confuse the medial and lateral epicondyles, since they mark different attachments and problems.
- It is a mistake to think markings are the same on every bone, because each bone has its own named markings.
- It is a mistake to overlook that foramina transmit nerves and vessels, not just being holes.
- It is a mistake to treat markings as unimportant, because they are your clinical landmarks.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Locating markings on named bones aloud is the strongest test.

- Locate the major markings of the humerus from memory.
- Locate the major palpable landmarks of the femur and tibia.
- Name the palpable landmarks at the wrist and ankle.
- Identify the markings of a typical vertebra.
- Relate one marking to a physiotherapy-relevant structure or injury.

We have located major markings on individual bones. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The scapula, humerus, radius, and ulna carry key upper-limb landmarks such as the acromion and epicondyles.
