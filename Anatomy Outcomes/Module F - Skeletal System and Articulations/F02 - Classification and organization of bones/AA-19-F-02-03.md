## Define the two major divisions of the skeletal system (axial and appendicular) and list the bones contained in each

# The Axial and Appendicular Skeletons

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Define the two major divisions of the skeletal system (axial and appendicular) and list the bones contained in each.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The skeleton's 206 bones are organised into two divisions, one forming the central axis of the body and one forming the limbs and their attachments. Learning this division gives you a framework for locating any bone. Today we define the axial and appendicular skeletons and list the bones of each.

- By the end of this session you will be able to define the axial and appendicular skeletons.
- You will be able to list the bones of the axial skeleton.
- You will be able to list the bones of the appendicular skeleton.
- You will be able to connect the division to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: A Central Axis and the Limbs That Hang From It

The organising idea is that the skeleton divides into the axial skeleton, forming the central supporting axis of the head and trunk, and the appendicular skeleton, forming the limbs and the girdles that attach them to the axis. One holds the body up; the other moves it through the world. This axis-and-appendages division frames the whole skeleton.

- The skeleton divides into the axial and appendicular skeletons.
- The axial skeleton forms the central axis of the head and trunk.
- The appendicular skeleton forms the limbs and their attaching girdles.
- Together they make up the 206 bones of the adult skeleton.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Ship's Keel and Its Rigging

To keep the division concrete, picture a ship with a central keel and mast forming its supporting axis, and the movable booms and arms rigged out from that axis to work the sails. The axial skeleton is the keel and mast, and the appendicular skeleton is the rigged-out movable parts, and the image keeps the central support and movable appendages together.

- The keel and mast correspond to the axial skeleton supporting the body.
- The movable booms and arms correspond to the appendicular skeleton of the limbs.
- The fittings attaching the booms to the mast correspond to the girdles attaching the limbs to the axis.
- The analogy breaks down in that a ship's parts are built and fixed, whereas the skeleton's bones are living and grow and remodel.

---

## The Axial Skeleton

Begin with the axial skeleton, the central supporting core. It comprises three main regions.

- The axial skeleton consists of the skull, the vertebral column, and the thoracic cage.
- The skull includes the cranial bones protecting the brain and the facial bones.
- The vertebral column consists of the vertebrae and the sacrum and coccyx.
- The thoracic cage consists of the ribs and the sternum, protecting the heart and lungs.

---

## The Appendicular Skeleton: The Girdles

Next the girdles of the appendicular skeleton, which attach the limbs to the axis. There are two.

- The pectoral girdle attaches the upper limbs to the axial skeleton and consists of the clavicle and scapula.
- The pelvic girdle attaches the lower limbs to the axial skeleton and consists of the hip bones.
- The pelvic girdle is joined firmly to the sacrum, providing stable weight transfer.
- The girdles are the bridges between the axial core and the limbs.

---

## The Appendicular Skeleton: The Limbs

Finally the limb bones of the appendicular skeleton. Each limb follows a similar pattern.

- The upper limb consists of the humerus, the radius and ulna, and the carpals, metacarpals, and phalanges.
- The lower limb consists of the femur, the tibia and fibula, and the tarsals, metatarsals, and phalanges.
- The patella is a sesamoid bone at the knee within the lower limb.
- The limbs are the bones of movement and manipulation, hung from the girdles.

---

## Why This Division Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The axial and appendicular division organises how you locate and reason about the bones you assess. Each point below connects the division to care.

- The axial skeleton includes the spine, central to posture, back pain, and spinal rehabilitation.
- The appendicular skeleton includes the limb bones you assess and rehabilitate in most musculoskeletal cases.
- The girdles transfer force between limbs and axis, relevant to shoulder and hip mechanics.
- The pelvic girdle's stable attachment underlies weight transfer in standing and walking.
- Knowing the division helps you organise assessment of the whole skeleton.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the division, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to the keel and rigging.

- It is a mistake to place the girdles in the axial skeleton, because they belong to the appendicular skeleton.
- It is a mistake to forget the sacrum and coccyx are part of the vertebral column in the axial skeleton.
- It is a mistake to overlook the patella when listing the lower limb bones.
- It is a mistake to think the two divisions work independently, since the girdles connect them.

---

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Listing the bones of each division aloud is the strongest test.

- Define the axial and appendicular skeletons from memory.
- List the regions and bones of the axial skeleton.
- List the girdle and limb bones of the appendicular skeleton.
- State which division the clavicle belongs to.
- Relate one division to a physiotherapy-relevant region.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have divided the skeleton into its two major parts. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The skeleton divides into the axial and appendicular skeletons.
- The axial skeleton is the skull, vertebral column, and thoracic cage.
- The appendicular skeleton is the pectoral and pelvic girdles and the limb bones.
- The girdles attach the limbs to the axial skeleton.
- The division organises how you locate and reason about the bones you assess.
