# The Structural Components of the Skeletal System

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: List and describe the major structural components (e.g., bones, ligaments, tendons, articulations) of the skeletal system.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The skeletal system is more than bones alone, because bones must be connected, joined, and linked to muscle to produce movement. Listing its structural components gives you the parts you assess and treat across musculoskeletal practice. Today we list and describe the major structural components of the skeletal system.

- By the end of this session you will be able to list the major structural components of the skeletal system.
- You will be able to describe the role of bones, cartilage, ligaments, and tendons.
- You will be able to explain how articulations link bones together.
- You will be able to connect the components to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: A Framework of Rigid Parts Joined by Connective Tissues

The organising idea is that the skeletal system combines rigid bones with the connective tissues that join them to each other and to muscle, so that the framework can both bear load and move. The components divide into the rigid struts and the flexible connectors. This division into struts and connectors frames the list.

- The skeletal system consists of rigid bones joined by connective tissues.
- Cartilage cushions and connects bones at certain sites.
- Ligaments connect bone to bone, and tendons connect muscle to bone.
- Articulations, or joints, are where bones meet and movement is permitted.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Framework Tent of Poles and Guy-Ropes

To keep the components concrete, picture a large framework tent: rigid poles form the structure, flexible joints let the poles angle against one another, guy-ropes tie poles together for stability, and lines link the poles to the fabric that pulls the shape into place. The skeleton is built this way, and the image keeps rigid struts and flexible connectors together.

- The rigid poles correspond to the bones.
- The flexible joints between poles correspond to the articulations.
- The guy-ropes tying poles together correspond to the ligaments.
- The lines linking poles to the pulling fabric correspond to the tendons linking bone to muscle, though the analogy breaks down in that tent parts are assembled and inert while the skeleton's tissues are living and remodel.

---

## Bones and Cartilage

Begin with the rigid and semi-rigid components, the bones and cartilage. These form and cushion the framework.

- Bones are the rigid organs that form the framework, providing support, leverage, and protection.
- They are living organs containing bone tissue, blood vessels, nerves, and marrow.
- Cartilage is a firm but flexible connective tissue that cushions and connects at certain sites.
- Cartilage covers the ends of bones at joints and forms structures such as the costal cartilages.

---

## Ligaments and Tendons

Next the connective tissue cords, ligaments and tendons. These bind the framework and link it to muscle.

- Ligaments are bands of dense connective tissue that connect bone to bone.
- They stabilise joints and limit excessive or abnormal movement.
- Tendons are bands of dense connective tissue that connect muscle to bone.
- They transmit the pull of muscles to the bones, producing movement.

---

## Articulations

Finally the articulations, where bones meet and movement is allowed or prevented. These are the joints.

- An articulation, or joint, is any place where two bones meet.
- Joints hold bones together while permitting varying amounts of movement.
- Some joints are freely movable, some slightly movable, and some immovable.
- Joints are where the skeleton's rigid parts are connected into a movable whole.

---

## Why the Components Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The structural components are the very tissues you assess and rehabilitate in musculoskeletal practice. Each point below connects a component to care.

- Bones are the tissues you load to promote strength and manage after fracture.
- Ligaments are the structures you assess and rehabilitate after sprains.
- Tendons transmit muscle force and are the site of strains and tendinopathy you treat.
- Joints are where you assess range of movement and treat stiffness and instability.
- Knowing the components ensures you consider all the tissues involved in a movement problem.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors narrow the list, and naming them keeps it complete. When unsure, return to the framework tent of poles and guy-ropes.

- It is a mistake to equate the skeletal system with bones alone, because it includes joints and connective tissues.
- It is a mistake to confuse ligaments and tendons, because one joins bone to bone and the other muscle to bone.
- It is a mistake to overlook cartilage, which cushions and connects at joints.
- It is a mistake to forget that joints are structural components, not just spaces.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Listing and describing the components aloud is the strongest test.

- List the major structural components of the skeletal system from memory.
- Describe the roles of bones and cartilage.
- Distinguish ligaments from tendons.
- Explain what an articulation is.
- Relate one component to a physiotherapy-relevant concern.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have listed the parts that make up the skeletal system. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The skeletal system consists of bones joined by connective tissues at articulations.
- Bones form the rigid framework, and cartilage cushions and connects at certain sites.
- Ligaments connect bone to bone, and tendons connect muscle to bone.
- Articulations are the joints where bones meet and movement is permitted.
- These components are the tissues you assess and rehabilitate in musculoskeletal practice.
