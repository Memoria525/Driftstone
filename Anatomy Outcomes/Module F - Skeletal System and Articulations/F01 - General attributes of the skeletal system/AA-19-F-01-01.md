## Describe the major functions of the skeletal system

# The Major Functions of the Skeletal System

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Describe the major functions of the skeletal system.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The skeleton is so obviously about support and movement that its other functions are easily forgotten, yet it also protects, stores minerals, and makes blood. Understanding all its functions frames why bone health matters far beyond fractures. Today we describe the major functions of the skeletal system.

- By the end of this session you will be able to list the major functions of the skeletal system.
- You will be able to describe the mechanical functions of support, protection, and movement.
- You will be able to describe the metabolic functions of mineral storage and blood formation.
- You will be able to connect these functions to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: A Living Framework That Also Stores and Produces

The organising idea is that the skeleton is not an inert scaffold but a living organ system with mechanical functions of support, protection, and movement and metabolic functions of mineral storage and blood cell production. It is a framework that is also a store and a factory. This dual mechanical and metabolic role frames the functions.

- The skeletal system performs both mechanical and metabolic functions.
- Its mechanical functions are support, protection, and movement.
- Its metabolic functions are mineral storage and blood cell formation.
- It is a living, active organ system rather than a dead scaffold.

---

## Our Master Analogy: The Steel Frame of a Working Factory

To keep the functions concrete, picture the steel frame of a working factory. It holds the building up, shields the machinery inside, provides anchor points for moving cranes, stores a reserve of raw metal, and houses a workshop that manufactures parts. The skeleton does all these, and the image keeps its mechanical and productive roles together.

- Holding the building up corresponds to the skeleton's support.
- Shielding the machinery corresponds to the protection of organs.
- Anchoring the moving cranes corresponds to providing attachment for muscles to produce movement.
- Storing metal and housing a workshop correspond to mineral storage and blood formation, though the analogy breaks down in that a steel frame is inert while bone is living and continually remodels itself.

---

## The Mechanical Functions

Begin with the mechanical functions, the skeleton's most familiar roles. Three work together for posture and movement.

- The skeleton supports the body, providing a rigid framework that bears weight and maintains shape.
- It protects delicate organs, as the skull shields the brain and the rib cage shields the heart and lungs.
- It enables movement by providing attachment points and levers for the muscles.
- Muscles pull on bones across joints, so the skeleton is essential to producing movement.

---

## The Metabolic Functions

Next the metabolic functions, which are easily overlooked but vital. Bone is a store and a factory.

- Bone stores minerals, chiefly calcium and phosphorus, releasing them into the blood as needed.
- This mineral store helps maintain the blood calcium level essential for nerve and muscle function.
- Red bone marrow within certain bones produces blood cells in a process called haematopoiesis.
- Bone also stores energy as fat in yellow marrow.

---

## How the Functions Interrelate

Finally it is worth seeing how the functions connect, since they are not independent. Mechanical loading drives metabolic change.

- The mechanical loading of bone stimulates it to remodel and strengthen, linking movement to bone maintenance.
- The mineral store both supports metabolism and contributes to bone's mechanical strength.
- Blood formation in the marrow depends on the protected internal space the bone provides.
- The skeleton's mechanical and metabolic roles thus support one another.

---

## Why the Skeletal Functions Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The skeleton's functions underlie the loading, movement, and bone health central to musculoskeletal practice. Each point below connects a function to care.

- The support and lever functions underlie all the movement you assess and rehabilitate.
- Mechanical loading stimulates bone strengthening, which is why weight-bearing exercise helps prevent bone loss.
- The mineral store links bone to the calcium balance relevant to muscle and nerve function.
- Reduced loading, as in immobility, weakens bone, informing your reasoning about disuse and recovery.
- Understanding the functions grounds exercise prescription for bone health.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors narrow the skeleton's roles, and naming them keeps them complete. When unsure, return to the steel frame of a working factory.

- It is a mistake to see the skeleton only as support, because it also protects, moves, stores, and produces blood.
- It is a mistake to treat bone as inert, because it is living and continually remodels.
- It is a mistake to overlook blood formation, which occurs in the marrow.
- It is a mistake to forget that loading strengthens bone, linking movement to bone health.

## Key Takeaways

We have described the many functions of the skeleton. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The skeletal system performs mechanical and metabolic functions.
- Its mechanical functions are support, protection of organs, and enabling movement through levers.
- Its metabolic functions are storing minerals such as calcium and producing blood cells in marrow.
- Mechanical loading stimulates bone to remodel and strengthen.
- These functions underlie loading, movement, and bone health central to practice.
