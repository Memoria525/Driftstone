# The Movements That Occur at Joints

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Define the movements that typically occur at a joint (e.g., flexion, extension, abduction, adduction, rotation, circumduction, inversion, eversion, protraction, retraction).

---

## Where We Are Going Today

Every movement you assess and prescribe has a precise name, and these movement terms are the shared language of rehabilitation. Learning to define them exactly lets you describe and record movement unambiguously. Today we define the movements that typically occur at joints.

- By the end of this session you will be able to define the main joint movements.
- You will be able to group movements into opposing pairs.
- You will be able to describe the special movements of particular regions.
- You will be able to connect movement terms to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Named Movements Defined Against the Anatomical Position

The organising idea is that joint movements are defined as changes from the anatomical position, described in pairs of opposites, so that each term names a precise direction of movement about a joint. The reference pose anchors every definition. This idea of named movements from a reference frames the terms.

- Joint movements are defined as changes from the anatomical position.
- Most movements come in pairs of opposites.
- Each term names a precise direction of movement about a joint.
- The anatomical position is the reference against which movements are defined.

---

## Our Master Analogy: The Named Moves of a Dance Notation

To keep the terms concrete, picture a dance notation in which every step has a precise name, defined relative to a starting stance, so any choreographer can record and reproduce a sequence exactly. Movement terms are anatomy's dance notation, defined from the anatomical position, and the image keeps the idea of precise named moves in view.

- The precise named steps correspond to the named joint movements.
- The starting stance corresponds to the anatomical position.
- Recording and reproducing a sequence corresponds to documenting movement unambiguously.
- The analogy breaks down in that dance steps are invented conventions, whereas joint movements are constrained by the real anatomy of each joint.

## The Movements in the Sagittal Plane

Begin with the front-to-back movements, which are among the most common. These are flexion and extension.

- Flexion is a movement that decreases the angle at a joint, as in bending the elbow.
- Extension is a movement that increases the angle at a joint, as in straightening the elbow.
- Hyperextension is extension beyond the normal anatomical position.
- These movements occur at many joints, including the elbow, knee, and spine.

---

## The Movements Toward and Away From the Midline

Next the side-to-side movements relative to the midline. These are abduction and adduction.

- Abduction is movement of a limb away from the midline of the body, as in raising the arm sideways.
- Adduction is movement of a limb toward the midline, as in lowering the raised arm.
- Circumduction is a circular movement combining flexion, abduction, extension, and adduction, as in circling the arm.
- These movements occur at joints such as the shoulder and hip.

---

## The Rotational and Special Movements

Finally rotation and the special movements of particular regions. These have specific names.

- Rotation is turning a bone around its own long axis, as in turning the head or the whole limb.
- Inversion turns the sole of the foot inward and eversion turns it outward, at the ankle and foot.
- Protraction moves a part forward and retraction moves it back, as with the jaw or shoulder blades.
- Other special movements include supination and pronation of the forearm and elevation and depression of the shoulders.

---

## Why Movement Terms Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

Movement terms are the working language of assessment, documentation, and exercise prescription. Each point below connects the terms to practice.

- You measure and record range of movement using these terms, so precision keeps your notes clear.
- You prescribe exercises in these terms, so a shared vocabulary ensures they are performed correctly.
- Describing a movement deficit precisely lets colleagues understand and reproduce your findings.
- The special movements such as inversion and eversion are central to describing ankle injuries.
- Fluent movement vocabulary underpins all your communication about movement.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors blur the movement terms, and naming them keeps them precise. When unsure, return to the named moves of a dance notation.

- It is a mistake to confuse flexion and extension, since one decreases and the other increases the joint angle.
- It is a mistake to swap abduction and adduction, since one moves away from and the other toward the midline.
- It is a mistake to confuse inversion and eversion of the foot.
- It is a mistake to define movements without reference to the anatomical position.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Defining each movement and demonstrating it aloud is the strongest test.

- Define flexion, extension, abduction, and adduction from memory.
- Define rotation and circumduction.
- Define inversion, eversion, protraction, and retraction.
- Group the movements into opposing pairs.
- Relate one movement term to a physiotherapy-relevant assessment.

## Key Takeaways

We have defined the movements that occur at joints. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- Joint movements are defined as changes from the anatomical position, mostly in opposing pairs.
- Flexion and extension change the joint angle, and abduction and adduction move relative to the midline.
- Rotation turns a bone on its axis, and circumduction combines movements into a circle.
- Special movements include inversion and eversion, protraction and retraction, and supination and pronation.
- These terms are the working language of assessment, documentation, and exercise prescription.
