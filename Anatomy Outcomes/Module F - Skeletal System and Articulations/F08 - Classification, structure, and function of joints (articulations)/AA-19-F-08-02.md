# The Functional Classification of Joints

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Describe the functional classification of joints based on amount of movement permitted, and provide examples of each type.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

Alongside the structural classification, joints are classified functionally by how much they move, from immovable to freely movable. This functional scheme is the one most directly relevant to movement and rehabilitation. Today we describe the functional classification of joints and give examples of each type.

- By the end of this session you will be able to name the three functional classes of joint.
- You will be able to define each by the movement it permits.
- You will be able to give examples of each.
- You will be able to relate the functional classes to the structural ones.

---

## The Big Idea: Classifying Joints by How Much They Move

The organising idea is that the functional classification sorts joints purely by the amount of movement they allow, from none, through slight, to free, so that the class name tells you immediately how mobile a joint is. Movement is the sole criterion. This focus on amount of movement frames the classification.

- The functional classification sorts joints by how much movement they permit.
- Synarthroses are immovable joints.
- Amphiarthroses are slightly movable joints.
- Diarthroses are freely movable joints.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Doors That Do Not Open, Barely Open, or Swing Freely

To keep the classes concrete, picture three doors: one sealed shut and immovable, one that gives only a slight push, and one on free-swinging hinges. The functional joint classes are these three doors, sorted purely by how much they move, and the image keeps the amount of movement in view.

- The sealed immovable door corresponds to the synarthrosis.
- The barely-giving door corresponds to the amphiarthrosis.
- The free-swinging door corresponds to the diarthrosis.
- The analogy breaks down in that doors are designed to fixed specifications, whereas a joint's mobility arises from its living structure and can change with injury or stiffness.

---

## Synarthroses

Begin with the immovable joints, which sacrifice movement for stability and protection. These are the synarthroses.

- A synarthrosis is an immovable joint.
- It provides strong, stable connection and often protection.
- The sutures between the skull bones are synarthroses, protecting the brain.
- A gomphosis, anchoring a tooth, is also functionally a synarthrosis.

---

## Amphiarthroses

Next the slightly movable joints, which balance some movement with stability. These are the amphiarthroses.

- An amphiarthrosis is a slightly movable joint.
- It permits limited movement while maintaining stability.
- The pubic symphysis and the joints between vertebral bodies are amphiarthroses.
- The syndesmosis between the tibia and fibula is also slightly movable.

---

## Diarthroses

Finally the freely movable joints, which allow the wide movements of the limbs. These are the diarthroses.

- A diarthrosis is a freely movable joint.
- All diarthroses are synovial joints with a fluid-filled cavity.
- The shoulder, hip, elbow, and knee are diarthroses.
- These joints permit the wide range of movement used in daily activity.

---

## Relating the Functional and Structural Classes

It helps to connect the two classifications, since they largely correspond. Structure predicts function.

- Fibrous joints are usually synarthroses or amphiarthroses, moving little.
- Cartilaginous joints are usually synarthroses or amphiarthroses, moving slightly.
- Synovial joints are diarthroses, moving freely.
- The two classifications therefore describe the same joints from different angles.

---

## Why This Classification Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The functional classification maps directly onto the movement you assess and rehabilitate. Each point below connects the classes to practice.

- The diarthroses are the freely movable joints whose range you most often assess and restore.
- The amphiarthroses of the spine allow the summed movement of the vertebral column central to back care.
- Knowing a joint's functional class sets your expectation for its normal movement.
- Loss of movement at a normally free joint signals a problem to address.
- The classification grounds your reasoning about joint mobility.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors blur the functional classes, and naming them keeps them clear. When unsure, return to the three doors.

- It is a mistake to think all joints move freely, because synarthroses are immovable.
- It is a mistake to confuse amphiarthroses with diarthroses, since one moves slightly and the other freely.
- It is a mistake to believe the functional and structural classifications are unrelated, since they correspond closely.
- It is a mistake to assume immovable joints are unimportant, because they provide stability and protection.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Naming each class with an example aloud is the strongest test.

- Name the three functional classes of joint from memory.
- Define each by the movement it permits.
- Give an example of each class.
- Relate each functional class to its usual structural class.
- Explain why the diarthroses matter most in rehabilitation.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have classified joints by how much they move. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- Joints are functionally classified as synarthroses, amphiarthroses, and diarthroses.
- Synarthroses are immovable, as at the skull sutures.
- Amphiarthroses are slightly movable, as at the pubic symphysis and between vertebrae.
- Diarthroses are freely movable and are all synovial joints, as at the shoulder and hip.
- The functional and structural classifications describe the same joints from different angles.
