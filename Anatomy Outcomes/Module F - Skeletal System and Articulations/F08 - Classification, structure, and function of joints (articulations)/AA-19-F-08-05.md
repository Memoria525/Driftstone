# The Six Types of Synovial Joint

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: For each of the six structural types of synovial joints, describe its anatomic features, identify locations in the body, and predict the kinds of movement each joint allows.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The six types of synovial joint differ in the shape of their articulating surfaces, and that shape predicts exactly what movements each allows. Learning the shapes lets you predict a joint's movements rather than memorise them. Today we describe each of the six synovial joint types, locate them, and predict their movements.

- By the end of this session you will be able to name the six synovial joint types.
- You will be able to describe the articular shape of each.
- You will be able to locate each in the body.
- You will be able to predict the movements each allows from its shape.

---

## The Big Idea: Joint Shape Predicts Joint Movement

The organising idea is that the shape of the articulating surfaces of a synovial joint determines the axes about which it can move, so that knowing the shape lets you predict the movements. Flat surfaces slide, hinges bend in one plane, and spheres move in all directions. This link from shape to movement frames the six types.

- The six synovial joint types differ in the shape of their articulating surfaces.
- The shape determines the axes and range of movement.
- Simpler shapes allow movement in one plane, and rounder shapes allow more.
- Knowing the shape predicts the movement.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Set of Mechanical Linkages

To keep the types concrete, picture a set of mechanical linkages, each shaped for a particular motion: a flat slider, a door hinge, a rotating spindle, an egg-in-cup, a saddle fitting, and a ball in a socket. Each linkage moves in its own characteristic way. The six synovial joints are these linkages, and the image keeps shape matched to movement.

- The flat slider corresponds to the planar joint.
- The door hinge corresponds to the hinge joint, and the rotating spindle to the pivot joint.
- The egg-in-cup corresponds to the condylar joint, and the saddle fitting to the saddle joint.
- The ball in a socket corresponds to the ball-and-socket joint, though the analogy breaks down in that mechanical linkages are rigid while joints are cushioned, lubricated, and supported by soft tissue.

---

## Planar and Hinge Joints

Begin with the simplest joints, which allow sliding or bending in one plane. These are the planar and hinge joints.

- A planar, or gliding, joint has flat or slightly curved surfaces that slide across one another, as between the carpal bones and the tarsal bones.
- Planar joints allow short gliding movements in various directions but no rotation.
- A hinge joint has a convex surface fitting a concave one, as at the elbow, knee, and finger joints.
- Hinge joints allow bending and straightening, that is flexion and extension, in one plane.

---

## Pivot and Condylar Joints

Next joints allowing rotation or two-plane movement. These are the pivot and condylar joints.

- A pivot joint has a rounded bone rotating within a ring, as at the joint between the first two neck vertebrae and between the radius and ulna.
- Pivot joints allow rotation around a single axis, such as turning the head or the forearm.
- A condylar, or ellipsoid, joint has an oval surface fitting an oval socket, as at the knuckles and the wrist.
- Condylar joints allow movement in two planes, including flexion, extension, abduction, and adduction.

---

## Saddle and Ball-and-Socket Joints

Finally the most mobile joints, allowing wide movement. These are the saddle and ball-and-socket joints.

- A saddle joint has surfaces shaped like a saddle and rider, as at the base of the thumb.
- Saddle joints allow movement in two planes with greater freedom than condylar joints, giving the thumb its wide range.
- A ball-and-socket joint has a spherical head in a cup-shaped socket, as at the shoulder and hip.
- Ball-and-socket joints allow movement in all planes, including rotation, giving the greatest range of any joint.

---

## Why the Six Types Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

Knowing each joint type and its movements underlies your assessment of range and function. Each point below connects the types to practice.

- Predicting a joint's movements from its type sets your expectation for its normal range.
- The ball-and-socket shoulder's wide range explains both its function and its vulnerability to dislocation.
- The hinge knee's single-plane design explains why twisting forces injure it.
- The saddle joint of the thumb underlies the grip and pinch you assess in hand rehabilitation.
- Relating shape to movement grounds your assessment of any synovial joint.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors blur the types, and naming them keeps them distinct. When unsure, return to the set of mechanical linkages.

- It is a mistake to think hinge joints rotate, because they move in only one plane.
- It is a mistake to confuse condylar and ball-and-socket joints, since one moves in two planes and the other in all.
- It is a mistake to overlook that planar joints only glide short distances.
- It is a mistake to assume shape and movement are unrelated, since shape predicts movement.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Predicting movements from shape aloud is the strongest test.

- Name the six synovial joint types from memory.
- Describe the articular shape of each.
- Locate each type in the body.
- Predict the movements each allows from its shape.
- Relate one joint type to a physiotherapy-relevant function or injury.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have described the six synovial joint types and their movements. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- Planar joints glide, as between the carpals, and hinge joints flex and extend in one plane, as at the elbow.
- Pivot joints rotate, as at the neck, and condylar joints move in two planes, as at the knuckles.
- Saddle joints, as at the thumb, move freely in two planes, and ball-and-socket joints, as at the hip and shoulder, move in all planes.
- The shape of the articulating surfaces predicts the movements allowed.
- Relating shape to movement grounds your assessment of any synovial joint.
