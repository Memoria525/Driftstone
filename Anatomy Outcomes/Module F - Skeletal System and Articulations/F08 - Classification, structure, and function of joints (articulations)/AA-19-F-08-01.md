# The Structural Classification of Joints

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Describe the anatomical classification of joints based on structure: fibrous, cartilaginous, and synovial, and provide examples of each type.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

Joints are classified structurally by what holds the bones together, and this classification maps closely onto how much the joint can move. Learning the structural types and their examples lets you predict a joint's mobility from its construction. Today we describe the structural classification of joints and give examples of each type.

- By the end of this session you will be able to name the three structural classes of joint.
- You will be able to describe the fibrous and cartilaginous subtypes with examples.
- You will be able to describe the synovial subtypes with examples.
- You will be able to relate structure to the mobility of each joint.

---

## The Big Idea: What Holds the Bones Together Decides How They Move

The organising idea is that joints are classified by the material binding the bones, fibrous tissue, cartilage, or a fluid-filled capsule, and this binding material largely determines how freely the joint moves. Tighter binding means less movement. This link between binding and mobility frames the classification.

- Joints are classified structurally by what connects the bones.
- Fibrous joints are held by dense connective tissue and move little.
- Cartilaginous joints are held by cartilage and move slightly.
- Synovial joints have a fluid-filled cavity and move freely.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Three Ways of Joining Two Boards

To keep the classes concrete, picture three ways of joining two boards: gluing them with a stiff bond that barely flexes, joining them with a firm rubber pad that gives a little, or connecting them with an oiled hinge in a housing that moves freely. Fibrous, cartilaginous, and synovial joints are these three joins, and the image keeps binding matched to movement.

- The stiff glued bond corresponds to the fibrous joint with little movement.
- The firm rubber pad corresponds to the cartilaginous joint with slight movement.
- The oiled hinge in a housing corresponds to the freely moving synovial joint.
- The analogy breaks down in that joins are made by a builder, whereas joints develop as living tissues and their fluid is actively maintained.

---

## Fibrous Joints

Begin with fibrous joints, held by connective tissue with little or no movement. Three subtypes exist.

- Fibrous joints connect bones with dense connective tissue and generally permit little movement.
- A suture is an immovable fibrous joint between the flat bones of the skull.
- A gomphosis is the fibrous joint anchoring a tooth in its socket.
- A syndesmosis is a fibrous joint connecting two bones by a ligament, as between the tibia and fibula.

---

## Cartilaginous Joints

Next cartilaginous joints, held by cartilage with slight movement. Two subtypes exist.

- Cartilaginous joints connect bones with cartilage and permit little to moderate movement.
- A synchondrosis is joined by hyaline cartilage, as at the growth plate and the joint of the first rib with the sternum.
- A symphysis is joined by fibrocartilage, allowing slight movement, as at the pubic symphysis and between vertebral bodies.
- The intervertebral discs between the vertebral bodies are symphyses.

---

## Synovial Joints

Finally synovial joints, the freely movable joints with a fluid-filled cavity. Six subtypes are recognised by shape.

- Synovial joints have a fluid-filled joint cavity and permit free movement.
- Planar or gliding joints allow sliding, as between the carpal bones, while hinge joints allow bending, as at the elbow.
- Pivot joints allow rotation, as at the top of the neck, and condylar joints allow movement in two planes, as at the knuckles.
- Saddle joints, as at the base of the thumb, and ball-and-socket joints, as at the hip and shoulder, allow the freest movement.

---

## Why This Classification Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The structural classification underlies your reasoning about joint mobility, stability, and injury. Each point below connects the classification to practice.

- Knowing a joint's structure predicts its normal range and the movements you assess.
- The synovial joints are the freely movable joints you most often rehabilitate.
- Symphyses such as the intervertebral discs are central to spinal loading and injury.
- The syndesmosis between the tibia and fibula is injured in high ankle sprains you assess.
- Relating structure to mobility grounds your reasoning about any joint.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors blur the classification, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to three ways of joining two boards.

- It is a mistake to think all joints move freely, because fibrous and cartilaginous joints move little.
- It is a mistake to confuse a synchondrosis and a symphysis, since one uses hyaline cartilage and the other fibrocartilage.
- It is a mistake to overlook that only synovial joints have a fluid-filled cavity.
- It is a mistake to treat structure and mobility as unrelated, since binding determines movement.

## Key Takeaways

We have classified joints by their structure. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- Joints are fibrous, cartilaginous, or synovial by structure.
- Fibrous joints include sutures, gomphoses, and syndesmoses and move little.
- Cartilaginous joints include synchondroses and symphyses and move slightly.
- Synovial joints have a fluid-filled cavity and include planar, hinge, pivot, condylar, saddle, and ball-and-socket types.
- What binds the bones determines how freely the joint moves.
