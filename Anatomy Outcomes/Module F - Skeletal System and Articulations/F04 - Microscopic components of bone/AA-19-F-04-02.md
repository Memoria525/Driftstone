# The Microscopic Anatomy of Bone Tissue

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Describe the microscopic anatomy of bone tissue, including osteon composition.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

Under the microscope, compact bone reveals a beautifully ordered structure of repeating units built around blood vessels, and this microscopic architecture explains how bone is both strong and living. Understanding the osteon shows how bone nourishes its buried cells. Today we describe the microscopic anatomy of bone tissue, including the composition of the osteon.

- By the end of this session you will be able to describe the composition of bone matrix.
- You will be able to describe the structure of an osteon.
- You will be able to explain how buried bone cells are nourished.
- You will be able to connect bone microstructure to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Concentric Rings Around a Blood Supply

The organising idea is that compact bone is built from osteons, cylindrical units of bone laid down in concentric rings around a central blood vessel, so that living cells buried in the hard matrix can still be nourished. The blood supply sits at the centre of each unit. This idea of rings around a supply frames the microstructure.

- Compact bone is organised into repeating units called osteons.
- Each osteon is built around a central canal carrying blood vessels.
- Bone is deposited in concentric rings around this canal.
- This arrangement lets living cells in the hard matrix be nourished.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Tree Rings Around a Central Pipe

To keep the osteon concrete, picture a tree trunk with growth rings, but with a supply pipe running up its centre and tiny chambers between the rings connected by fine channels to the pipe. The concentric rings give strength, and the central pipe feeds the chambers. An osteon is built this way, and the image keeps the rings and central supply together.

- The concentric growth rings correspond to the layers of bone, the lamellae.
- The central supply pipe corresponds to the central canal with its blood vessels.
- The tiny chambers between rings correspond to the lacunae housing osteocytes.
- The fine channels to the pipe correspond to the canaliculi that nourish the cells, though the analogy breaks down in that a tree's rings are annual growth while bone lamellae are laid down by osteoblasts around the vessel.

---

## The Composition of Bone Matrix

Begin with what bone tissue is made of, since its matrix gives it its properties. The matrix combines two components.

- Bone matrix consists of an organic component and a mineral component.
- The organic component, mainly collagen, gives bone its flexibility and resistance to tension.
- The mineral component, mainly calcium phosphate, gives bone its hardness and resistance to compression.
- The combination makes bone both strong and slightly flexible, resisting fracture.

---

## The Structure of the Osteon

Next the osteon itself, the structural unit of compact bone. Its parts fit together around the central canal.

- An osteon is a cylindrical unit of compact bone aligned along the bone's lines of stress.
- At its centre is the central canal, containing blood vessels and nerves.
- Around the canal are concentric rings of bone matrix called lamellae.
- Between the lamellae are lacunae containing osteocytes, connected by tiny channels called canaliculi.

---

## How Buried Cells Are Nourished

Finally how the osteocytes buried in hard matrix survive, since this is what the structure solves. The canaliculi are the key.

- Osteocytes are trapped in lacunae within the hard matrix, away from direct blood supply.
- The canaliculi are tiny channels linking lacunae to one another and to the central canal.
- Nutrients pass from the central blood vessel through the canaliculi to reach each osteocyte.
- This network allows living cells to survive deep within the solid bone.

---

## Why Bone Microstructure Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The microstructure underlies bone strength, its living nature, and its response to load. Each point below connects the structure to practice.

- The combination of collagen and mineral is why bone resists both tension and compression during activity.
- The alignment of osteons along stress lines is why bone is strong in the directions it is habitually loaded.
- The blood supply within osteons is what nourishes bone and supports its healing.
- Loss of mineral weakens bone, while loss of collagen makes it brittle, informing understanding of bone conditions.
- Understanding the microstructure grounds reasoning about bone strength and fracture.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture of bone tissue, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to tree rings around a central pipe.

- It is a mistake to think bone is non-living, because osteocytes live within it, nourished through canaliculi.
- It is a mistake to believe bone is only mineral, because collagen gives it flexibility.
- It is a mistake to overlook the central canal's blood supply within each osteon.
- It is a mistake to treat osteons as randomly arranged, because they align along lines of stress.

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Describing the osteon aloud is the strongest test.

- Describe the composition of bone matrix from memory.
- Describe the structure of an osteon and its parts.
- Explain how osteocytes buried in matrix are nourished.
- Explain how the matrix gives bone both hardness and flexibility.
- Relate bone microstructure to bone strength or a bone condition.

## Key Takeaways

We have described bone tissue under the microscope. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- Bone matrix combines collagen for flexibility with calcium phosphate for hardness.
- Compact bone is built from osteons, cylindrical units aligned along lines of stress.
- Each osteon has a central canal of blood vessels surrounded by concentric lamellae.
- Osteocytes in lacunae are nourished through tiny canaliculi linking them to the central canal.
- The microstructure underlies bone's strength, its living nature, and its response to load.
