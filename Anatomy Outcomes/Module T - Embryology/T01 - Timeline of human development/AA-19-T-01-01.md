## Define the pre-embryonic period, embryonic period, and fetal period, and describe the main events that occur in each period

# The Three Periods of Human Development

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Define the pre-embryonic period, embryonic period, and fetal period, and describe the main events that occur in each period.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

Development before birth is divided into three periods, each with its own defining events, from the first divisions through the forming of organs to growth and maturation. Understanding this timeline frames the whole of embryology. Today we define the three periods of development and describe the main events of each.

- By the end of this session you will be able to name the three periods of development.
- You will be able to describe the main events of each.
- You will be able to explain why the periods are divided as they are.
- You will be able to connect the timeline to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: First the Basic Layout, Then the Organs, Then Growth

The organising idea is that development proceeds through three periods, the pre-embryonic period establishing the basic structures, the embryonic period forming the organs, and the fetal period growing and maturing the body. Layout, organs, then growth. This idea of a three-stage timeline frames the topic.

- Development before birth is divided into three periods.
- The pre-embryonic period establishes the basic structures.
- The embryonic period forms the organs.
- The fetal period grows and matures the body.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Laying the Foundation, Building the Structure, Then Fitting It Out

To keep the periods concrete, picture building a house: first laying the foundation, then building the structure, then fitting it out and finishing. Development lays the basic structures, forms the organs, then grows and matures, and the image keeps the three stages in view.

- Laying the foundation corresponds to the pre-embryonic period.
- Building the structure corresponds to the embryonic period forming organs.
- Fitting out and finishing corresponds to the fetal period growing and maturing.
- The analogy breaks down in that a house is built by workers, whereas development unfolds through the embryo's own genetic programme.

---

## The Pre-Embryonic Period

Begin with the pre-embryonic period, the first stage. It establishes the basic structures.

- The pre-embryonic period covers the first two weeks after fertilisation.
- The fertilised egg divides and forms a ball of cells.
- It implants in the wall of the uterus.
- It forms the early structures on which the embryo will develop.

---

## The Embryonic Period

Next the embryonic period, when the organs form. This is a critical stage.

- The embryonic period covers roughly the third to the eighth week.
- The three basic tissue layers form and give rise to the organs.
- All the major organ systems begin to form during this period.
- Because the organs are forming, this is when development is most vulnerable to harm.

---

## The Fetal Period

Finally the fetal period, when the body grows and matures. This is the longest period.

- The fetal period covers from the ninth week until birth.
- The organs that formed in the embryonic period grow and mature.
- The body grows greatly in size.
- The fetus becomes ready for life outside the womb.

---

## Why This Timeline Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The timeline underlies development relevant to paediatric and obstetric practice. Each point below connects it to practice.

- The embryonic period is when the organs form, and harm then can cause birth defects.
- Understanding the timeline grounds the study of development and congenital conditions.
- The fetal period's growth is relevant to the newborn's readiness at birth.
- Congenital conditions affect children you may treat.
- Understanding the timeline grounds your reasoning about development.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the timeline, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to laying the foundation, building the structure, then fitting it out.

- It is a mistake to muddle the order of the periods.
- It is a mistake to think the organs form in the fetal period, since they form in the embryonic period.
- It is a mistake to overlook that the embryonic period is the most vulnerable.
- It is a mistake to forget that the fetal period is mainly growth and maturation.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Naming the periods and their events aloud is the strongest test.

- Name the three periods of development from memory.
- Describe the main events of each.
- Explain why the periods are divided as they are.
- Explain why the embryonic period is the most vulnerable.
- Relate the timeline to a physiotherapy-relevant concern.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have described the three periods of development. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- Development is divided into the pre-embryonic, embryonic, and fetal periods.
- The pre-embryonic period covers the first two weeks and establishes the basic structures.
- The embryonic period covers weeks three to eight and forms the organs.
- The fetal period covers week nine to birth and grows and matures the body.
- The embryonic period, when the organs form, is the most vulnerable to harm.
