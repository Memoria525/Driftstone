## List the maternal and fetal components of the placenta

# The Components of the Placenta

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: List the maternal and fetal components of the placenta.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The placenta is formed from both the fetus and the mother, joining their blood supplies closely so that substances can be exchanged. Understanding its components frames how the fetus is supported. Today we list the maternal and fetal components of the placenta.

- By the end of this session you will be able to name the fetal component of the placenta.
- You will be able to name the maternal component.
- You will be able to explain how they work together.
- You will be able to connect the placenta to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: A Joint Organ Formed From Both Fetal and Maternal Tissue

The organising idea is that the placenta is a joint organ formed from a fetal part, derived from the chorion, and a maternal part, derived from the uterine lining, joined so substances can be exchanged between the two blood supplies. A joint fetal-maternal organ. This idea of a joint organ frames the topic.

- The placenta is formed from both fetal and maternal tissue.
- The fetal component is derived from the chorion.
- The maternal component is derived from the uterine lining.
- The two join so substances can be exchanged between the blood supplies.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Shared Exchange Point Built From Both Sides

To keep the components concrete, picture an exchange point built where two supply networks meet, one side contributed by each network, so goods can pass between them without the networks merging. The placenta is built from a fetal side and a maternal side that exchange substances, and the image keeps the joint exchange point in view.

- The side contributed by one network corresponds to the fetal component.
- The side contributed by the other network corresponds to the maternal component.
- Goods passing between them corresponds to substances being exchanged.
- The analogy breaks down in that an exchange point is built by choice, whereas the placenta forms through development.

---

## The Fetal Component

Begin with the fetal component, derived from the chorion. It carries the fetal blood.

- The fetal component of the placenta is derived from the chorion.
- It forms finger-like projections that reach into the maternal tissue.
- These projections carry the fetal blood vessels.
- They provide a large surface for exchange.

---

## The Maternal Component

Next the maternal component, derived from the uterine lining. It carries the maternal blood.

- The maternal component of the placenta is derived from the uterine lining.
- It surrounds the fetal projections.
- The maternal blood bathes the fetal projections.
- This brings the two blood supplies close together.

---

## How the Two Work Together

Finally how the two work together, since exchange occurs between them. They stay separate but close.

- The fetal and maternal blood do not normally mix.
- They are separated by a thin barrier.
- Across this barrier, oxygen and nutrients pass from mother to fetus and wastes pass the other way.
- The placenta thereby supports the fetus by exchange between the two blood supplies.

## Why the Placenta Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The placenta underlies the support of the fetus relevant to obstetric practice. Each point below connects it to practice.

- The placenta supplies the fetus with oxygen and nutrients, essential to its development.
- Its function is relevant to the health of the pregnancy, relevant to obstetric care.
- Understanding the placenta grounds the study of development.
- Pregnancy is relevant to obstetric physiotherapy.
- Understanding it grounds your reasoning about development.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to a shared exchange point built from both sides.

- It is a mistake to think the placenta is only fetal or only maternal, since it is both.
- It is a mistake to believe the fetal and maternal blood mix, since they are separated by a thin barrier.
- It is a mistake to overlook that the fetal component derives from the chorion.
- It is a mistake to forget that the maternal component derives from the uterine lining.

## Key Takeaways

We have described the components of the placenta. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The placenta is formed from both fetal and maternal tissue.
- The fetal component, derived from the chorion, carries the fetal blood in finger-like projections.
- The maternal component, derived from the uterine lining, surrounds the projections with maternal blood.
- The two blood supplies are brought close but do not mix, separated by a thin barrier.
- Across the barrier, oxygen and nutrients pass to the fetus and wastes pass the other way.
