## Compare and contrast the different mitotic phases

# Comparing the Phases of Mitosis

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Compare and contrast the different mitotic phases.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

Mitosis is the process that shares a cell's duplicated chromosomes equally between two new cells, and it is the mechanism behind growth and most tissue repair. Rather than memorising four names, we will follow the chromosomes through the process and see why each phase is a necessary step. Today we compare the phases by what happens to the chromosomes in each.

- By the end of this session you will be able to name the phases of mitosis in order.
- You will be able to describe the key event of each phase.
- You will be able to contrast the phases by the state and position of the chromosomes.
- You will be able to connect mitosis to tissue growth and repair in physiotherapy.

---

## The Big Idea: Equal Sharing of Duplicated Chromosomes

The organising idea is that mitosis exists to distribute one complete, identical set of chromosomes to each of two daughter cells, and every phase is a step in lining them up and pulling them apart accurately. The whole process is about equal, error-free sharing. Keeping that goal in mind makes each phase's purpose clear.

- Mitosis divides a duplicated nucleus so that each daughter cell receives an identical full set of chromosomes.
- Each phase is a necessary step in condensing, aligning, separating, and repackaging the chromosomes.
- The process ensures the two daughter cells are genetically identical to each other and the parent.
- Understanding the goal of equal sharing explains why the phases occur in their fixed order.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Sorting Duplicated Documents Into Two Identical Folders

To keep the phases concrete, picture a clerk who must place one copy of every duplicated document into each of two folders, with no mistakes. First the loose papers are bundled into labelled files, then the old filing cabinet is cleared away, then every duplicated file is lined up along a central table, then the pairs are pulled to opposite ends, and finally each set is tidied into its own new folder. Mitosis follows exactly this order, and the sorting image keeps the purpose visible.

- Bundling loose papers into files corresponds to prophase, where chromatin condenses into chromosomes.
- Lining files along the central table corresponds to metaphase, where chromosomes align at the centre.
- Pulling pairs to opposite ends corresponds to anaphase, where sister chromatids separate.
- The analogy breaks down in that a clerk sorts by hand, whereas the cell uses a self-assembling spindle of microtubules to move the chromosomes.

---

## Prophase: Condensing and Preparing

Begin with the first phase, in which the chromosomes become visible and the machinery to move them assembles. The cell readies its cargo.

- In prophase, the loosely coiled chromatin condenses into distinct, visible chromosomes.
- Each chromosome is already duplicated, consisting of two sister chromatids joined at the centromere.
- The spindle, made of microtubules, begins to form and extend across the cell.
- The nuclear envelope breaks down, allowing the spindle to reach the chromosomes.

---

## Metaphase: Aligning at the Centre

Next the phase in which the chromosomes are lined up, the arrangement that guarantees equal separation. Alignment is the key event.

- In metaphase, the chromosomes are moved by the spindle to the centre of the cell.
- They line up along an imaginary central plane called the metaphase plate.
- Each chromosome is attached to spindle fibres from both poles of the cell.
- This precise central alignment ensures each daughter cell will receive exactly one copy of each chromosome.

---

## Anaphase and Telophase: Separating and Repackaging

Finally the phases in which the chromatids are pulled apart and each set is enclosed in a new nucleus. These complete the nuclear division.

- In anaphase, the sister chromatids separate and are pulled to opposite poles of the cell.
- Once separated, each chromatid is considered an independent chromosome.
- In telophase, a nuclear envelope reforms around each set of chromosomes at the two poles.
- The chromosomes decondense back into chromatin, and the cell now has two distinct nuclei.

---

## Comparing the Phases

Holding the phases together lets us contrast them by the state and position of the chromosomes, which is the objective's aim. Each phase is distinguished by one key event.

- Prophase is distinguished by condensation and spindle formation, with chromosomes scattered.
- Metaphase is distinguished by alignment, with chromosomes ordered at the centre.
- Anaphase is distinguished by separation, with chromatids moving to the poles.
- Telophase is distinguished by repackaging, with two new nuclei forming and chromosomes decondensing.

---

## Why Mitosis Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

Mitosis is the cellular basis of the growth and repair you support, so its accuracy and pace shape recovery. Each point below links the process to practice.

- The proliferation phase of tissue healing depends on mitosis producing new cells to rebuild damaged tissue.
- Accurate chromosome sharing keeps new cells healthy, whereas errors can contribute to disease.
- Tissues with cells that divide readily, such as skin, heal faster than those whose cells rarely divide.
- Understanding that division follows preparation explains why repair cannot outpace the cell cycle.
- The process grounds realistic expectations about the timeline of tissue regeneration.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture of mitosis, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to sorting duplicated documents.

- It is a mistake to think DNA is copied during mitosis, because replication happened earlier in the S phase.
- It is a mistake to confuse metaphase alignment with separation, because separation occurs in anaphase.
- It is a mistake to believe mitosis produces genetically varied cells, because the daughter cells are identical.
- It is a mistake to treat mitosis as the whole of cell division, because dividing the cytoplasm is a separate step.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Naming each phase with its key chromosome event is the strongest test.

- Name the four phases of mitosis in order from memory.
- State the key event of each phase.
- Contrast metaphase and anaphase by what happens to the chromosomes.
- Explain how metaphase alignment ensures equal sharing.
- Explain why mitosis matters for tissue healing.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have followed the chromosomes through mitosis and contrasted its phases. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- Mitosis distributes one identical set of chromosomes to each of two daughter cells.
- In prophase the chromosomes condense and the spindle forms as the nuclear envelope breaks down.
- In metaphase the chromosomes align at the centre, and in anaphase the sister chromatids separate to the poles.
- In telophase two new nuclei form and the chromosomes decondense.
- Mitosis is the basis of growth and the proliferation phase of tissue repair.
