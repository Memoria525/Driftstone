## Where We Are Going Today

The larynx is built from several cartilages that hold the airway open and support the vocal folds. Understanding its structure frames how it protects the airway and produces the voice. Today we describe the anatomical features of the larynx, including its cartilages.

- By the end of this session you will be able to describe the framework of the larynx.
- You will be able to name the major laryngeal cartilages.
- You will be able to describe the epiglottis.
- You will be able to connect the larynx's structure to physiotherapy practice.

## The Big Idea: A Cartilage Framework That Holds the Airway Open and Supports the Vocal Folds

The organising idea is that the larynx is a framework of cartilages that keeps the airway open and provides attachment for the vocal folds, with a flap that covers the airway during swallowing. A cartilage framework with a cover. This idea of a supportive framework frames the topic.

- The larynx is built from several cartilages.
- The cartilages keep the airway open.
- They support the vocal folds.
- A flap, the epiglottis, covers the airway during swallowing.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Rigid Box Frame Holding a Passage Open

To keep the structure concrete, picture a rigid frame of struts that holds a passage open and provides anchor points for internal parts, with a hinged lid that can cover the opening. The laryngeal cartilages hold the airway open and support the vocal folds, and the image keeps the supportive framework in view.

- The rigid frame of struts corresponds to the laryngeal cartilages.
- Holding the passage open corresponds to keeping the airway open.
- The hinged lid corresponds to the epiglottis covering the airway.
- The analogy breaks down in that a frame is fixed, whereas the laryngeal cartilages move to change the tension of the vocal folds.

---

## The Framework of Cartilages

Begin with the overall framework, since the cartilages give the larynx its shape. Several cartilages combine.

- The larynx is made of several cartilages connected by muscles and ligaments.
- Together they form a rigid but movable framework.
- The framework keeps the airway open at all times.
- The muscles move the cartilages to adjust the vocal folds.

---

## The Major Cartilages

Next the major cartilages, which you should be able to name. Two are especially prominent.

- The thyroid cartilage is the largest, forming the prominence known as the Adam's apple.
- The cricoid cartilage is a ring-shaped cartilage below the thyroid cartilage.
- The paired arytenoid cartilages sit above the cricoid and anchor the vocal folds.
- These cartilages together support the vocal folds and keep the airway open.

---

## The Epiglottis

Finally the epiglottis, the flap that protects the airway. It folds down during swallowing.

- The epiglottis is a leaf-shaped cartilage at the top of the larynx.
- During swallowing, it folds down over the opening of the larynx.
- This directs food and drink into the oesophagus rather than the airway.
- It springs back up after swallowing to reopen the airway.

---

## Why the Larynx's Structure Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The larynx's structure underlies airway protection and voice relevant to your patients. Each point below connects it to practice.

- The cartilages keep the airway open, relevant to breathing.
- The epiglottis protects the airway during swallowing, relevant to the risk of food entering the airway.
- The vocal folds, supported by the cartilages, produce the voice used in communication.
- Understanding the structure clarifies how the larynx works.
- Understanding it grounds your reasoning about the airway and voice.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to a rigid frame holding a passage open.

- It is a mistake to think the larynx is a single structure, since it is built from several cartilages.
- It is a mistake to overlook the epiglottis's role in swallowing.
- It is a mistake to forget that the cartilages support the vocal folds.
- It is a mistake to treat the cartilages as immovable, since they move to adjust the voice.
