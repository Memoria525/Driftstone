# Phonation: How the Larynx Produces the Voice

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Briefly explain how the vocal folds and the larynx function with phonation.

## Where We Are Going Today

Phonation is the production of the voice, and it depends on air passing between the vocal folds to make them vibrate. Understanding it explains how we speak and how pitch and volume are controlled. Today we briefly explain how the vocal folds and the larynx function with phonation.

- By the end of this session you will be able to explain how the vocal folds produce sound.
- You will be able to explain how pitch is controlled.
- You will be able to explain how volume is controlled.
- You will be able to connect phonation to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Air Vibrating the Vocal Folds Produces the Voice

The organising idea is that phonation occurs when exhaled air passes between the vocal folds and makes them vibrate, with the tension and position of the folds controlling pitch and the force of the air controlling volume. Air vibrates the folds. This idea of vibration-produced sound frames the topic.

- Phonation is the production of the voice.
- It occurs when exhaled air passes between the vocal folds.
- The air makes the vocal folds vibrate, producing sound.
- The tension of the folds and the force of the air shape the sound.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Air Blown Across a Reed

To keep phonation concrete, picture blowing air across a reed to make it vibrate and produce a note, tightening the reed for a higher note and blowing harder for a louder one. The vocal folds vibrate as air passes, and their tension and the air's force shape the sound, and the image keeps the vibration in view.

- The air blown across the reed corresponds to the exhaled air passing the vocal folds.
- The reed vibrating corresponds to the vocal folds vibrating.
- Tightening the reed for a higher note corresponds to tensing the folds to raise pitch.
- The analogy breaks down in that a reed is a simple fixed part, whereas the vocal folds are finely controlled living tissue.

---

## How Sound Is Produced

Begin with the basic mechanism, since it is the core of phonation. Air vibrates the folds.

- During speech, the vocal folds are brought close together.
- Exhaled air is forced between them.
- The air makes the folds vibrate.
- The vibration produces the sound of the voice.

---

## How Pitch Is Controlled

Next how pitch is controlled, since this shapes the voice. Fold tension sets the pitch.

- The pitch of the voice depends on the tension of the vocal folds.
- Tenser, more tightly stretched folds vibrate faster and produce a higher pitch.
- Looser folds vibrate slower and produce a lower pitch.
- The muscles of the larynx adjust the tension to change pitch.

---

## How Volume Is Controlled

Finally how volume is controlled, since this too shapes the voice. Air force sets the volume.

- The volume of the voice depends on the force of the air passing the folds.
- More forceful air makes the folds vibrate more strongly, producing a louder sound.
- Gentler air produces a quieter sound.
- The sound is further shaped into speech by the mouth, tongue, and lips.

---

## Why Phonation Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

Phonation underlies the voice and communication relevant to your patients. Each point below connects it to practice.

- Voice production is relevant to communication and to voice problems in your patients.
- Breathing supplies the air for phonation, linking voice to respiratory function.
- Conditions affecting the vocal folds impair the voice.
- Understanding phonation clarifies how the voice is produced.
- Understanding it grounds your reasoning about voice and breathing.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to air blown across a reed.

- It is a mistake to think the vocal folds make sound without air passing them, since air drives the vibration.
- It is a mistake to confuse the control of pitch and volume, since one depends on fold tension and the other on air force.
- It is a mistake to overlook that the mouth and lips shape the sound into speech.
- It is a mistake to separate phonation from breathing, since breathing supplies the air.
