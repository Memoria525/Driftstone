## List, in order, the respiratory structures that air passes through during inhalation and exhalation

# The Path of Air Through the Respiratory Structures

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: List, in order, the respiratory structures that air passes through during inhalation and exhalation.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

Air travels a fixed route from the nose to the air sacs and back, passing through each part of the respiratory tract in order. Learning this sequence ties the respiratory structures into one path. Today we list, in order, the respiratory structures that air passes through during inhalation and exhalation.

- By the end of this session you will be able to list the respiratory structures in order.
- You will be able to trace air from the nose to the air sacs.
- You will be able to explain that exhalation reverses the path.
- You will be able to connect the path to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: One Fixed Route In, and the Same Route Back Out

The organising idea is that air passes through the respiratory structures in a fixed order from the nose to the air sacs on inhalation, and reverses the same route on exhalation. One route in and out. This idea of a fixed reversible path frames the topic.

- Air passes through the respiratory structures in a fixed order.
- On inhalation, it travels from the nose down to the air sacs.
- On exhalation, it travels back out along the same route.
- Each structure lies in sequence along the path.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Journey Down a Branching Road to a Destination and Back

To keep the path concrete, picture a journey down a main road that branches into ever-smaller lanes ending at a destination, then the same journey back. Air travels down the branching airways to the air sacs and back, and the image keeps the fixed reversible path in view.

- The main road corresponds to the trachea.
- The branching lanes correspond to the bronchi and bronchioles.
- The destination corresponds to the air sacs.
- The analogy breaks down in that a journey is chosen, whereas air follows the airways passively driven by breathing.

---

## The Upper Part of the Path

Begin with the upper part of the path, from the nose to the larynx. Air is conditioned here.

- Air enters through the nose into the nasal cavity.
- It passes through the pharynx, the throat.
- It passes through the larynx, the voice box.
- It then enters the trachea.

---

## The Lower Part of the Path

Next the lower part of the path, from the trachea to the air sacs. The airways branch.

- From the trachea, air passes into the two main bronchi, one to each lung.
- The bronchi branch into smaller and smaller bronchi.
- These branch into bronchioles, ever smaller.
- The smallest bronchioles lead to the air sacs, the alveoli.

---

## The Reverse Path on Exhalation

Finally the reverse path, since exhalation retraces it. Air leaves the same way it entered.

- On exhalation, air leaves the air sacs.
- It travels back up through the bronchioles and bronchi.
- It passes up the trachea, larynx, and pharynx.
- It leaves through the nose or mouth, reversing the inward path.

---

## Why This Path Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The path underlies breathing and airway care relevant to your patients. Each point below connects it to practice.

- The path shows how air reaches the air sacs where gas exchange occurs, central to exercise tolerance.
- Airway clearance techniques act along this path to clear the airways.
- Blockage anywhere along the path impairs breathing.
- Understanding the path clarifies how air reaches the lungs.
- Understanding it grounds your reasoning about breathing.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the path, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to a journey down a branching road and back.

- It is a mistake to muddle the order of the structures.
- It is a mistake to think exhalation follows a different route, since it reverses the same path.
- It is a mistake to place the larynx after the trachea, since it comes before it.
- It is a mistake to forget that the air sacs are the end of the path.

---

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Listing the structures in order aloud is the strongest test.

- List the respiratory structures in order from memory.
- Trace air from the nose to the air sacs.
- Explain that exhalation reverses the path.
- Name the structure that comes before the trachea.
- Relate the path to a physiotherapy-relevant concern.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have listed the path of air through the respiratory structures. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- Air passes in a fixed order from the nose to the air sacs.
- It travels through the nasal cavity, pharynx, larynx, and trachea.
- From the trachea it passes through the bronchi and bronchioles to the air sacs.
- Exhalation reverses the same route.
- Air reaches the air sacs, where gas exchange occurs.
