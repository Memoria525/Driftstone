# The Upper and Lower Respiratory Tract

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Identify the anatomical division of the upper versus lower respiratory tract.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The respiratory tract is also divided into an upper and a lower part, split at the larynx. Understanding this division frames how respiratory infections and conditions are described. Today we identify the anatomical division of the upper versus lower respiratory tract.

- By the end of this session you will be able to state where the tract divides into upper and lower.
- You will be able to name the parts of the upper tract.
- You will be able to name the parts of the lower tract.
- You will be able to connect the division to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: A Division at the Larynx Into Upper and Lower Tracts

The organising idea is that the respiratory tract divides into an upper tract above the larynx and a lower tract from the larynx down, giving a simple way to locate structures and conditions. Above and below the larynx. This idea of a larynx-level division frames the topic.

- The respiratory tract divides into an upper and a lower part.
- The division is around the level of the larynx.
- The upper tract includes the structures above this level.
- The lower tract includes the structures from this level down into the lungs.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Building Split Into Upper and Lower Floors

To keep the division concrete, picture a building split into upper and lower floors at a certain level, so any room can be placed above or below the divide. The respiratory tract is split at the larynx this way, and the image keeps the upper and lower division in view.

- The upper floors correspond to the upper respiratory tract.
- The lower floors correspond to the lower respiratory tract.
- The dividing level corresponds to the larynx.
- The analogy breaks down in that a building's floors are fixed, whereas the exact dividing point of the tract is a convention.

---

## The Upper Respiratory Tract

Begin with the upper tract, above the larynx. It includes the nose and pharynx.

- The upper respiratory tract includes the nose and nasal cavity.
- It includes the paranasal sinuses.
- It includes the pharynx, the throat behind the nose and mouth.
- These structures lie above the larynx.

---

## The Lower Respiratory Tract

Next the lower tract, from the larynx down. It includes the airways and lungs.

- The lower respiratory tract includes the larynx.
- It includes the trachea, the main airway into the chest.
- It includes the bronchi and their branches within the lungs.
- It includes the lungs and their air sacs.

---

## Why the Division Is Useful

Finally why the division matters, since it organises the tract. It helps locate structures and conditions.

- The division gives a simple way to locate structures along the tract.
- Respiratory infections are often described as upper or lower.
- Upper tract conditions include the common cold and sinus infections.
- Lower tract conditions include bronchitis and pneumonia.

---

## Why This Division Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The division underlies how respiratory conditions are described relevant to your patients. Each point below connects it to practice.

- Lower respiratory tract conditions, such as pneumonia, are central to respiratory physiotherapy.
- The division helps you locate where a condition affects the tract.
- Airway clearance acts on the lower tract airways.
- Understanding the division clarifies how the tract is organised.
- Understanding it grounds your reasoning about respiratory conditions.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the division, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to a building split into upper and lower floors.

- It is a mistake to place the larynx in the upper tract, since it is part of the lower tract.
- It is a mistake to think the lungs are in the upper tract.
- It is a mistake to overlook the paranasal sinuses in the upper tract.
- It is a mistake to treat the division as a sharp physical boundary rather than a convention.

## Key Takeaways

We have identified the upper and lower respiratory tract. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The respiratory tract divides into an upper and a lower part around the larynx.
- The upper tract includes the nose, nasal cavity, sinuses, and pharynx.
