# The Process of Swallowing

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Describe the general process of swallowing (deglutition), and the role of the soft palate, uvula, and epiglottis.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

Swallowing is a carefully coordinated process that moves food from the mouth to the oesophagus while protecting the nose and airway. Understanding it explains how food is routed safely past the crossing of air and food. Today we describe the process of swallowing and the roles of the soft palate, uvula, and epiglottis.

- By the end of this session you will be able to describe the general process of swallowing.
- You will be able to explain the role of the soft palate and uvula.
- You will be able to explain the role of the epiglottis.
- You will be able to connect swallowing to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: A Coordinated Process That Routes Food Safely Past the Airway

The organising idea is that swallowing is a coordinated process that moves food to the oesophagus while the soft palate closes off the nose and the epiglottis closes off the airway, routing food safely. Route food, protect the nose and airway. This idea of protected routing frames the topic.

- Swallowing moves food from the mouth to the oesophagus.
- The soft palate and uvula close off the nose.
- The epiglottis closes off the airway.
- Together these protect the nose and airway as food passes.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Closing the Side Doors to Send a Package Down the Right Chute

To keep swallowing concrete, picture sending a package down the correct chute while side doors are closed so it cannot go the wrong way. During swallowing the nose and airway are closed off so food goes to the oesophagus, and the image keeps the protected routing in view.

- The side doors being closed correspond to the nose and airway being closed off.
- The package going down the right chute corresponds to food passing to the oesophagus.
- The closed nose door corresponds to the soft palate closing off the nose.
- The closed airway door corresponds to the epiglottis closing off the airway.
- The analogy breaks down in that doors are simple flaps, whereas swallowing is a finely coordinated sequence of muscle actions.

---

## The General Process

Begin with the general process, since it is a sequence of steps. It moves food from mouth to oesophagus.

- Swallowing begins voluntarily when the tongue pushes the food to the back of the mouth.
- This triggers an automatic swallowing reflex.
- The reflex moves the food through the pharynx to the oesophagus.
- Peristalsis then carries the food down the oesophagus to the stomach.

---

## The Role of the Soft Palate and Uvula

Next the role of the soft palate and uvula, which protect the nose. They close off the nasal route.

- As swallowing begins, the soft palate lifts.
- The soft palate and uvula close off the opening to the nasal cavity.
- This prevents food and drink from going up into the nose.
- The nose is sealed off while the food passes.

---

## The Role of the Epiglottis

Finally the role of the epiglottis, which protects the airway. It closes off the larynx.

- As the food passes, the larynx rises and the epiglottis folds down over it.
- This closes off the airway.
- The food is directed into the oesophagus rather than the airway.
- After the food passes, the airway reopens.

---

## Why Swallowing Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

Swallowing underlies safe eating relevant to your patients. Each point below connects it to practice.

- Safe swallowing protects the airway, and its failure can allow food to enter the lungs.
- Difficulty swallowing is common after stroke and other conditions you may treat.
- Food entering the airway can cause choking or chest infection, which you help prevent.
- Understanding swallowing clarifies how food is routed safely.
- Understanding it grounds your reasoning about swallowing safety.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to closing the side doors to send a package down the right chute.

- It is a mistake to think swallowing is entirely voluntary, since much of it is an automatic reflex.
- It is a mistake to overlook the soft palate's role in closing off the nose.
- It is a mistake to forget the epiglottis's role in closing off the airway.
- It is a mistake to think the airway stays open during swallowing.

## Key Takeaways

We have described the process of swallowing. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- Swallowing moves food from the mouth to the oesophagus, beginning voluntarily then becoming an automatic reflex.
- The soft palate and uvula close off the nose.
- The epiglottis folds down to close off the airway.
- Together these route the food safely to the oesophagus.
- Difficulty swallowing is common after stroke and can allow food to enter the airway.
