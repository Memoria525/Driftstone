# The Structure of the Liver

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Identify and describe the structure of the liver, including the individual lobes, ligaments (e.g., coronary ligament, falciform ligament, round ligament [ligamentum teres]), and the porta hepatis.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The liver is a large organ divided into lobes, anchored by ligaments, with a central doorway where its vessels and ducts enter and leave. Understanding its structure frames how it is organised and connected. Today we identify and describe the structure of the liver, its lobes, ligaments, and porta hepatis.

- By the end of this session you will be able to name the lobes of the liver.
- You will be able to name the major ligaments.
- You will be able to describe the porta hepatis.
- You will be able to connect the liver's structure to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: A Lobed Organ Anchored by Ligaments With a Central Doorway

The organising idea is that the liver is a large organ divided into lobes, held in place by ligaments, with a central doorway, the porta hepatis, where its vessels and ducts enter and leave. Lobes, anchoring ligaments, and a doorway. This idea of a lobed anchored organ frames the topic.

- The liver is divided into lobes.
- It is anchored by ligaments.
- Its vessels and ducts enter and leave at a central doorway, the porta hepatis.
- These features organise and connect the liver.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Large Structure Divided Into Wings, Anchored by Straps, With a Service Door

To keep the structure concrete, picture a large structure divided into wings, held in place by straps, with a service door where all its supply lines enter. The liver's lobes are the wings, its ligaments the straps, and the porta hepatis the service door, and the image keeps the lobed anchored organ in view.

- The wings correspond to the lobes of the liver.
- The straps correspond to the ligaments.
- The service door corresponds to the porta hepatis.
- The analogy breaks down in that a structure is built, whereas the liver develops as living tissue.

---

## The Lobes

Begin with the lobes, since they divide the liver. Two are large and two are small.

- The liver has two large lobes, a large right lobe and a smaller left lobe.
- It also has two smaller lobes on its underside.
- The lobes are divided by ligaments and grooves.
- The lobes together make up the whole liver.

---

## The Ligaments

Next the ligaments, which anchor the liver. Several hold it in place.

- The falciform ligament attaches the liver to the front of the abdominal wall and the diaphragm.
- It divides the right and left lobes on the front surface.
- The coronary ligament attaches the liver to the diaphragm above.
- The round ligament, a remnant of a fetal vessel, runs in the lower edge of the falciform ligament.

---

## The Porta Hepatis

Finally the porta hepatis, the doorway. Vessels and ducts enter and leave here.

- The porta hepatis is the central doorway on the underside of the liver.
- The hepatic portal vein and the hepatic artery enter here.
- The bile ducts leave here, carrying bile away.
- Nerves and lymphatic vessels also pass through the porta hepatis.

---

## Why the Liver's Structure Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The liver's structure underlies its connections relevant to your patients. Each point below connects it to practice.

- The porta hepatis is where the liver's blood supply and bile ducts connect, relevant to its function.
- The ligaments anchor the liver, relevant to its position in the upper right abdomen.
- Understanding the lobes helps localise liver conditions.
- The round ligament is a remnant of the fetal circulation, linking to development.
- Understanding the structure grounds your reasoning about the liver.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to a structure with wings, straps, and a service door.

- It is a mistake to think the liver has a single lobe, since it has several.
- It is a mistake to overlook the porta hepatis as the doorway for vessels and ducts.
- It is a mistake to forget that the round ligament is a fetal remnant.
- It is a mistake to confuse the ligaments that anchor the liver.

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Naming the lobes, ligaments, and porta hepatis aloud is the strongest test.

- Name the lobes of the liver from memory.
- Name the major ligaments.
- Describe the porta hepatis.

- Relate the liver's structure to a physiotherapy-relevant concern.

## Key Takeaways

We have described the structure of the liver. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The liver has a large right lobe, a smaller left lobe, and two smaller lobes on its underside.
- The falciform ligament attaches it to the front wall and diaphragm and divides the lobes.
- The coronary ligament attaches it to the diaphragm, and the round ligament is a fetal remnant.
- The porta hepatis is the doorway where the portal vein and hepatic artery enter and the bile ducts leave.
- These features organise and connect the liver.
