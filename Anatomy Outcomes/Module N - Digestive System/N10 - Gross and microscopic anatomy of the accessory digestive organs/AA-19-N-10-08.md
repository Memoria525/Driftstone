# The Location of the Pancreas

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Describe the location of the pancreas relative to other body structures.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The pancreas lies deep in the upper abdomen, behind the stomach, stretching from the duodenum to the spleen. Understanding its location frames its relationships and why its pain is felt in the back. Today we describe the location of the pancreas relative to other structures.

- By the end of this session you will be able to describe where the pancreas lies.
- You will be able to describe its relationship to the stomach and duodenum.
- You will be able to describe its position relative to the back.
- You will be able to connect its location to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: A Deep Retroperitoneal Gland Behind the Stomach From the Duodenum to the Spleen

The organising idea is that the pancreas lies deep in the upper abdomen behind the stomach, retroperitoneal against the back wall, stretching from the duodenum on the right to the spleen on the left. Deep, behind the stomach, across the abdomen. This idea of a deep transverse position frames the topic.

- The pancreas lies deep in the upper abdomen.
- It lies behind the stomach.
- It is retroperitoneal, against the back wall.
- It stretches from the duodenum on the right to the spleen on the left.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Shelf Fixed to the Back Wall Behind a Large Unit

To keep the location concrete, picture a shelf fixed to the back wall behind a large unit, stretching across the space. The pancreas is fixed against the back wall behind the stomach, stretching across the upper abdomen, and the image keeps its deep transverse position in view.

- The shelf fixed to the back wall corresponds to the retroperitoneal pancreas.
- Behind the large unit corresponds to behind the stomach.
- Stretching across the space corresponds to reaching from the duodenum to the spleen.
- The analogy breaks down in that a shelf is rigid, whereas the pancreas is a soft gland.

---

## The General Location

Begin with the general location, deep in the upper abdomen. It lies behind the stomach.

- The pancreas lies deep in the upper abdomen.
- It lies behind the stomach.
- It is retroperitoneal, lying against the back wall of the abdomen.
- Its deep position means it is not easily felt from the front.

---

## The Relationship to the Stomach and Duodenum

Next its relationship to the stomach and duodenum, since these matter. Its head is cradled by the duodenum.

- The stomach lies in front of the pancreas.
- The head of the pancreas is cradled by the C-shaped curve of the duodenum.
- The pancreas secretes its enzymes into the duodenum.
- The tail of the pancreas reaches toward the spleen.

---

## The Position Relative to the Back

Finally its position relative to the back, since this affects its pain. It lies against the back wall.

- The pancreas lies against the back wall of the abdomen.
- Because it is deep and near the back, its pain is often felt in the back.
- Its retroperitoneal position makes it more fixed than the mobile organs.
- Its deep position places it near the large vessels of the back.

---

## Why the Pancreas's Location Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The pancreas's location underlies its pain and relationships relevant to your patients. Each point below connects it to practice.

- Because the pancreas lies near the back, its pain is often felt in the back, relevant to interpreting a patient's pain.
- Its deep retroperitoneal position makes it hard to feel from the front.
- Its connection to the duodenum is where it delivers its enzymes.
- Understanding its location clarifies its relationships and its pain pattern.
- Understanding it grounds your reasoning about the upper abdomen.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to a shelf fixed to the back wall behind a large unit.

- It is a mistake to place the pancreas in front of the stomach, since it lies behind it.
- It is a mistake to think the pancreas is easily felt from the front, since it is deep and retroperitoneal.
- It is a mistake to overlook that its head is cradled by the duodenum.
- It is a mistake to forget that its pain is often felt in the back.
