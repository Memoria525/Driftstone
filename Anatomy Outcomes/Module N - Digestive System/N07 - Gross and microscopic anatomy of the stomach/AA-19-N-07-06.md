# The Regions of the Stomach

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Identify and describe the gross anatomic regions of the stomach (cardia, fundus, body, pylorus).

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The stomach is divided into named regions from where the oesophagus enters to where the small intestine leaves. Learning them lets you locate parts of the stomach and where its functions occur. Today we identify and describe the gross regions of the stomach.

- By the end of this session you will be able to name the regions of the stomach in order.
- You will be able to describe the location of each.
- You will be able to explain the role of each region.
- You will be able to connect the regions to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Named Regions From the Entrance to the Exit

The organising idea is that the stomach is divided into named regions in order from the entrance where the oesophagus joins to the exit where the small intestine leaves, each region a part of the one bag. Entrance to exit regions. This idea of ordered regions frames the topic.

- The stomach is divided into named regions.
- The cardia is the entrance region where the oesophagus joins.
- The fundus and body are the main storage regions.
- The pylorus is the exit region leading to the small intestine.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Tank Labelled From Inlet to Outlet

To keep the regions concrete, picture a tank labelled by region from its inlet to its outlet, each region doing part of the work. The stomach's regions run from the cardia entrance to the pyloric exit, and the image keeps the ordered regions in view.

- The inlet region corresponds to the cardia.
- The main body of the tank corresponds to the fundus and body.
- The outlet region corresponds to the pylorus.
- The analogy breaks down in that a tank is machinery, whereas the stomach is living muscle that churns and secretes.

---

## The Cardia and Fundus

Begin with the entrance and the top, the cardia and fundus. These lie near where the oesophagus joins.

- The cardia is the region where the oesophagus joins the stomach.
- It surrounds the opening from the oesophagus.
- The fundus is the dome-shaped upper region, above the level of the cardia.
- The fundus often holds swallowed air.

---

## The Body

Next the body, the main region. This is where most storage and churning occur.

- The body is the large central region of the stomach.
- It is the main storage area for a meal.
- Much of the churning and mixing occurs here.
- Its lining contains many of the gastric glands.

---

## The Pylorus

Finally the exit region, the pylorus. This leads to the small intestine.

- The pylorus is the lower region of the stomach, leading to the small intestine.
- It funnels the stomach contents toward the exit.
- It ends at a sphincter that controls emptying into the small intestine.
- It grinds the food to a fine consistency before release.

---

## Why the Regions Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The stomach's regions underlie its function and conditions relevant to your patients. Each point below connects a region to practice.

- The cardia is near where reflux occurs, relevant to interpreting reflux symptoms.
- The body and fundus store and churn the meal, relevant to digestion.
- The pylorus controls emptying, relevant to how meals are handled.
- Understanding the regions clarifies where the stomach's functions occur.
- Understanding them grounds your reasoning about the stomach.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to a tank labelled from inlet to outlet.

- It is a mistake to muddle the order of the regions.
- It is a mistake to place the cardia at the exit, since it is at the entrance.
- It is a mistake to overlook that the fundus often holds swallowed air.
- It is a mistake to forget that the pylorus ends at a sphincter.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Naming the regions in order aloud is the strongest test.

- Name the regions of the stomach in order from memory.
- Describe the location of each.
- Explain the role of each region.
- State which region the oesophagus joins.
- Relate one region to a physiotherapy-relevant concern.

## Key Takeaways

We have described the regions of the stomach. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The stomach is divided into named regions from entrance to exit.
- The cardia is the entrance region where the oesophagus joins.
- The fundus is the dome-shaped upper region, and the body is the main storage region.
- The pylorus is the exit region leading to the small intestine, ending at a sphincter.
- Each region is part of the one stomach, doing part of its work.
