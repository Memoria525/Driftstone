## Describe the location of the major salivary glands (i.e., parotid, submandibular, sublingual) and their respective ducts

# The Major Salivary Glands

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Describe the location of the major salivary glands (parotid, submandibular, sublingual) and their respective ducts.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

Three pairs of major salivary glands around the mouth produce most of the saliva, each draining through its own duct. Understanding their locations frames how saliva reaches the mouth. Today we describe the location of the major salivary glands and their ducts.

- By the end of this session you will be able to name the three pairs of major salivary glands.
- You will be able to state the location of each.
- You will be able to state where each duct opens.
- You will be able to connect the glands to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Three Pairs of Glands Around the Mouth, Each With Its Own Duct

The organising idea is that three pairs of major salivary glands sit around the mouth, the parotid near the ear, the submandibular under the jaw, and the sublingual under the tongue, each draining saliva into the mouth through its own duct. Three glands, three ducts. This idea of glands with their own ducts frames the topic.

- Three pairs of major salivary glands produce most of the saliva.
- The parotid glands lie near the ears.
- The submandibular glands lie under the jaw.
- The sublingual glands lie under the tongue, and each gland drains through its own duct.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Three Reservoirs Feeding a Room Through Pipes

To keep the glands concrete, picture three reservoirs positioned around a room, each feeding it through its own pipe. The salivary glands are these reservoirs around the mouth, each with its own duct, and the image keeps the glands and ducts in view.

- The three reservoirs correspond to the three pairs of salivary glands.
- The room they feed corresponds to the mouth.
- The pipes correspond to the ducts.
- The analogy breaks down in that reservoirs are static, whereas the glands actively produce saliva in response to food and other cues.

---

## The Parotid Glands

Begin with the parotid glands, the largest, near the ears. Their duct opens near the upper teeth.

- The parotid glands are the largest salivary glands.
- They lie in front of and below each ear.
- Their duct runs forward across the cheek.
- It opens into the mouth near the upper back teeth.

---

## The Submandibular Glands

Next the submandibular glands, under the jaw. Their duct opens under the tongue.

- The submandibular glands lie under the jaw, toward the back.
- They produce much of the saliva.
- Their duct runs forward under the floor of the mouth.
- It opens under the tongue, near the front.

---

## The Sublingual Glands

Finally the sublingual glands, under the tongue. Their ducts open in the floor of the mouth.

- The sublingual glands lie under the tongue, in the floor of the mouth.
- They are the smallest of the three pairs.
- They drain through several small ducts.
- These ducts open into the floor of the mouth under the tongue.

---

## Why the Salivary Glands Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The salivary glands underlie saliva and its role relevant to your patients. Each point below connects them to practice.

- Saliva moistens food and begins digestion, relevant to swallowing and nutrition.
- Reduced saliva, from some conditions or medications, causes a dry mouth that affects swallowing.
- The parotid gland can become inflamed, a condition you may encounter.
- Understanding the glands clarifies how saliva reaches the mouth.
- Understanding them grounds your reasoning about the mouth.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to three reservoirs feeding a room through pipes.

- It is a mistake to think one gland produces all the saliva, since three pairs contribute.
- It is a mistake to place the parotid duct anywhere but near the upper back teeth.
- It is a mistake to confuse the submandibular and sublingual glands.
- It is a mistake to overlook that each gland has its own duct.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Naming each gland and its duct aloud is the strongest test.

- Name the three pairs of major salivary glands from memory.
- State the location of each.
- State where each duct opens.
- Explain which gland is the largest.
- Relate the glands to a physiotherapy-relevant concern.

## Key Takeaways

We have described the major salivary glands. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- Three pairs of major salivary glands produce most of the saliva.
- The parotid glands lie near the ears, and their duct opens near the upper back teeth.
- The submandibular glands lie under the jaw, and their duct opens under the tongue.
- The sublingual glands lie under the tongue, draining through several small ducts.
- Each gland drains its saliva into the mouth through its own duct.
