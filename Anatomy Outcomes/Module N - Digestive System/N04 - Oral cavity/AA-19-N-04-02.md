# Defining Mastication

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Define mastication.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

Mastication is the technical term for chewing, the first step of mechanical digestion. Defining it frames how food is prepared in the mouth for the rest of digestion. Today we define mastication.

- By the end of this session you will be able to define mastication.
- You will be able to describe how it breaks down food.
- You will be able to explain its role in digestion.
- You will be able to connect mastication to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Chewing That Breaks Food Up and Mixes It With Saliva

The organising idea is that mastication is chewing, the mechanical breakdown of food by the teeth, which mixes the food with saliva and prepares it for swallowing and further digestion. Chewing to break up and mix. This idea of preparatory chewing frames the definition.

- Mastication is the process of chewing.
- It is the mechanical breakdown of food by the teeth.
- It mixes the food with saliva.
- It prepares the food for swallowing and further digestion.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Grinder That Breaks Up and Moistens Material

To keep mastication concrete, picture a grinder that breaks material into smaller pieces while moistening it, preparing it for the next stage. Mastication breaks food up with the teeth and mixes it with saliva, and the image keeps the breaking and moistening in view.

- The grinder breaking material up corresponds to the teeth chewing the food.
- The moistening corresponds to mixing the food with saliva.
- Preparing it for the next stage corresponds to readying the food for swallowing.
- The analogy breaks down in that a grinder is mechanical, whereas mastication involves the jaw muscles, teeth, and tongue working together.

---

## Defining Mastication

Begin with the definition itself, stated precisely. It is chewing.

- Mastication is the chewing of food in the mouth.
- It uses the teeth to break the food into smaller pieces.
- It is powered by the muscles of the jaw.
- The tongue and cheeks keep the food between the teeth.

---

## How Mastication Breaks Down Food

Next how it breaks down food, since this is its mechanical role. It reduces the food to smaller pieces.

- The teeth cut and grind the food into smaller pieces.
- This increases the surface area of the food.
- The greater surface area lets enzymes act more effectively.
- Mastication is a form of mechanical digestion.

---

## The Role in Digestion

Finally its role in digestion, since this is why it matters. It prepares food for the rest of the process.

- Mastication mixes the food with saliva, which begins chemical digestion.
- It forms the food into a soft mass that can be swallowed.
- It prepares the food for the rest of digestion.
- Poor chewing can make food harder to digest and swallow.

---

## Why Mastication Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

Mastication underlies chewing and nutrition relevant to your patients. Each point below connects it to practice.

- Chewing prepares food for digestion, relevant to nutrition and recovery.
- Problems with chewing, from dental or muscle problems, affect nutrition in some patients.
- Poor chewing can make food harder to swallow, relevant to the risk of choking.
- Understanding mastication clarifies how digestion begins.
- Understanding it grounds your reasoning about the start of digestion.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors cloud the definition, and naming them keeps it precise. When unsure, return to a grinder that breaks up and moistens material.

- It is a mistake to think mastication is chemical digestion, since it is mechanical.
- It is a mistake to overlook that it mixes food with saliva.
- It is a mistake to forget that it prepares food for swallowing.
- It is a mistake to think chewing plays no role in digestion.

## Key Takeaways

We have defined mastication. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- Mastication is the process of chewing.
- It uses the teeth, powered by the jaw muscles, to break food into smaller pieces.
- It mixes the food with saliva and forms it into a mass that can be swallowed.
- It is a form of mechanical digestion that increases the food's surface area.
- It prepares the food for swallowing and further digestion.
