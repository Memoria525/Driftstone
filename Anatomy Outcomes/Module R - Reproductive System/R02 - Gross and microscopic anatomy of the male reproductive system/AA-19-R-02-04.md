## Identify and describe the structure and functions of accessory glands (i.e., seminal glands [seminal vesicles], prostate gland, bulbourethral [Cowper] glands)

# The Male Accessory Glands

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Identify and describe the structure and functions of accessory glands (seminal glands [seminal vesicles], prostate gland, bulbourethral [Cowper] glands).

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The male accessory glands add fluids to the sperm to form semen, nourishing and protecting the sperm on their journey. Understanding them frames how semen is made. Today we identify and describe the structure and functions of the male accessory glands.

- By the end of this session you will be able to name the male accessory glands.
- You will be able to describe each gland.
- You will be able to state the function of each.
- You will be able to connect the glands to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Glands That Add Fluids to the Sperm to Form Semen

The organising idea is that three sets of accessory glands add fluids to the sperm, nourishing them, protecting them from acidity, and lubricating the passage, together forming semen. Glands that add the fluid of semen. This idea of fluid-adding glands frames the topic.

- The male accessory glands add fluids to the sperm.
- The seminal glands add a nourishing fluid.
- The prostate gland adds a fluid that helps protect the sperm.
- The bulbourethral glands add a lubricating fluid.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Stations That Add Ingredients to a Product on a Line

To keep the glands concrete, picture stations along a line that each add an ingredient to a product, one adding fuel, one adding a protective agent, and one adding a lubricant. The accessory glands add the ingredients of semen this way, and the image keeps the fluid-adding stations in view.

- The station adding fuel corresponds to the seminal glands adding nourishment.
- The station adding a protective agent corresponds to the prostate protecting the sperm.
- The station adding lubricant corresponds to the bulbourethral glands.
- The analogy breaks down in that stations are machinery, whereas the glands are living tissue.

---

## The Seminal Glands

Begin with the seminal glands, which add most of the fluid. They nourish the sperm.

- The seminal glands lie behind the bladder.
- They produce a large part of the fluid of semen.
- Their fluid contains sugar that nourishes the sperm.
- Their duct joins the ductus deferens to form the ejaculatory duct.

---

## The Prostate Gland

Next the prostate gland, which surrounds the urethra. It adds a protective fluid.

- The prostate gland lies below the bladder, surrounding the start of the urethra.
- It produces a fluid that adds to the semen.
- Its fluid helps protect the sperm from the acidity of the female tract.
- Its fluid is added to the semen as it passes through the urethra.

---

## The Bulbourethral Glands

Finally the bulbourethral glands, which lubricate. They add a lubricating fluid.

- The bulbourethral glands are small glands below the prostate.
- They produce a lubricating fluid.
- The fluid lubricates the urethra and neutralises any acidity there.
- It is released before the semen.

---

## Why the Accessory Glands Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The accessory glands underlie semen formation and related conditions relevant to your patients. Each point below connects a gland to practice.

- The glands form the semen that carries and supports the sperm, relevant to fertility.
- The prostate can enlarge with age, affecting urination, relevant to some patients.
- Understanding the glands clarifies how semen is made.
- Prostate conditions are relevant to pelvic-health practice.
- Understanding the glands grounds your reasoning about the male reproductive system.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to stations that add ingredients to a product.

- It is a mistake to think the glands make sperm, since the testis makes them.
- It is a mistake to overlook that the seminal glands add most of the fluid.
- It is a mistake to forget that the prostate surrounds the urethra.
- It is a mistake to confuse the roles of the three glands.

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Naming each gland and its function aloud is the strongest test.

- Name the male accessory glands from memory.
- Describe each gland.
- State the function of each.
- Explain what each gland adds to the semen.
- Relate the glands to a physiotherapy-relevant concern.

## Key Takeaways

We have described the male accessory glands. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The seminal glands behind the bladder add a nourishing sugary fluid, forming much of the semen.
- The prostate gland surrounds the start of the urethra and adds a fluid that helps protect the sperm.
- The bulbourethral glands add a lubricating fluid released before the semen.
- Together they add the fluids that form semen.
- The prostate can enlarge with age, affecting urination.
