# Tracing the Path of Urine

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Trace the path of urine from the collecting duct of the kidney through the external urethral orifice.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

Once urine is made, it follows a fixed path from the collecting ducts through the kidney's collecting spaces, down the ureter, into the bladder, and out through the urethra. Tracing it ties the urinary structures into one journey. Today we trace the path of urine from the collecting duct to the external urethral orifice.

- By the end of this session you will be able to trace urine through the kidney's collecting spaces.
- You will be able to trace it to the bladder.
- You will be able to trace it out through the urethra.
- You will be able to connect the path to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: A One-Way Journey From Collecting Duct to the Outside

The organising idea is that urine follows a one-way journey from the collecting ducts through the kidney's collecting spaces, down the ureter to the bladder, and out through the urethra. A one-way journey out. This idea of an ordered journey frames the topic.

- Urine flows from the collecting ducts through the kidney's collecting spaces.
- It flows down the ureter to the bladder.
- It is stored in the bladder.
- It flows out through the urethra.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Fluid Collected Into a Funnel, Down a Pipe, Into a Tank, and Out

To keep the path concrete, picture fluid collected into a funnel, carried down a pipe into a tank, and released out an outlet. Urine is collected into the renal pelvis, carried down the ureter into the bladder, and released out the urethra, and the image keeps the ordered journey in view.

- The funnel corresponds to the renal pelvis collecting the urine.
- The pipe corresponds to the ureter.
- The tank corresponds to the bladder.
- The outlet corresponds to the urethra.
- The analogy breaks down in that a tank empties by gravity, whereas the bladder empties by its muscle under control.

---

## From the Collecting Ducts to the Ureter

Begin with the path through the kidney's collecting spaces. The urine is gathered and funnelled.

- The collecting ducts carry the urine toward the tips of the renal pyramids.
- The urine drips from the pyramid tips into the minor calyces.
- The minor calyces join into major calyces, which join into the renal pelvis.
- The renal pelvis funnels the urine into the ureter.

---

## Down the Ureter to the Bladder

Next down the ureter to the bladder. The urine is carried by peristalsis.

- The ureter carries the urine from the renal pelvis down to the bladder.
- It moves the urine along by peristalsis.
- The urine enters the bladder at its base.
- The bladder stores the urine until it is convenient to expel it.

---

## Out Through the Urethra

Finally out through the urethra. The urine leaves the body.

- When it is convenient, the bladder contracts and the sphincters relax.
- The urine flows from the bladder into the urethra.
- The urethra carries the urine to the outside.
- The urine leaves the body through the external urethral orifice.

---

## Why This Path Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The path underlies urine handling and continence relevant to your patients. Each point below connects it to practice.

- The path shows how urine is carried, stored, and expelled, relevant to bladder function and continence.
- Blockage anywhere along the path, as by a stone in the ureter, causes pain.
- The bladder's controlled emptying underlies continence, relevant to pelvic-health practice.
- Understanding the path clarifies how urine is handled.
- Understanding it grounds your reasoning about the urinary system.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the path, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to fluid collected into a funnel, down a pipe, into a tank, and out.

- It is a mistake to muddle the order of the collecting spaces.
- It is a mistake to think urine moves down the ureter by gravity alone, since peristalsis moves it.
- It is a mistake to overlook that the bladder stores the urine.
- It is a mistake to forget that the sphincters must relax for urine to leave.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Tracing the path aloud is the strongest test.

- Trace urine through the kidney's collecting spaces from memory.
- Trace it to the bladder.
- Trace it out through the urethra.
- Explain how urine moves down the ureter.
- Relate the path to a physiotherapy-relevant concern.

## Key Takeaways

We have traced the path of urine. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- Urine flows from the collecting ducts to the pyramid tips into the minor calyces.
- The minor calyces join into major calyces and the renal pelvis, which funnels urine into the ureter.
- The ureter carries the urine to the bladder by peristalsis.
- The bladder stores the urine until it is expelled.
- The urine flows out through the urethra to the external urethral orifice.
