## Identify and describe the internal divisions of the renal tissue, including cortical and medullary structures

# The Internal Divisions of the Kidney

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Identify and describe the internal divisions of the renal tissue, including cortical and medullary structures.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

Cutting into the kidney reveals an outer region, an inner region divided into cone-shaped sections, and a central collecting space. Understanding these divisions frames where urine is made and gathered. Today we identify and describe the internal divisions of the renal tissue.

- By the end of this session you will be able to name the internal regions of the kidney.
- You will be able to describe the cortex and medulla.
- You will be able to describe the collecting spaces.
- You will be able to connect the internal divisions to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: An Outer Cortex, an Inner Medulla of Pyramids, and a Central Collecting Space

The organising idea is that the kidney has an outer cortex, an inner medulla divided into cone-shaped pyramids that drain urine, and a central collecting space that funnels the urine into the ureter. Cortex, pyramids, and collecting space. This idea of layered divisions frames the topic.

- The kidney has an outer region, the cortex.
- It has an inner region, the medulla, divided into cone-shaped pyramids.
- The pyramids drain urine toward the centre.
- A central collecting space funnels the urine into the ureter.

---

## Our Master Analogy: An Outer Rind, Inner Cones, and a Central Funnel

To keep the divisions concrete, picture a fruit with an outer rind, inner cone-shaped segments, and a central funnel that collects the juice. The kidney has an outer cortex, inner pyramids, and a central collecting space, and the image keeps the layered divisions in view.

- The outer rind corresponds to the cortex.
- The inner cones correspond to the medullary pyramids.
- The central funnel corresponds to the collecting space.
- The analogy breaks down in that a fruit is inert, whereas the kidney's regions are living tissue making and gathering urine.

---

## The Cortex and Medulla

Begin with the two main regions, the cortex and medulla. These make up the kidney tissue.

- The renal cortex is the outer region of the kidney tissue.
- The renal medulla is the inner region.
- The medulla is divided into cone-shaped sections called renal pyramids.
- The cortex dips down between the pyramids as columns.

---

## The Pyramids and Their Drainage

Next the pyramids and how they drain, since they carry urine toward the centre. Their tips point inward.

- Each renal pyramid is a cone-shaped section of the medulla.
- The tip of each pyramid points toward the centre of the kidney.
- Urine drains through the pyramid toward its tip.
- The tip drips the urine into a collecting cup.

---

## The Collecting Spaces

Finally the collecting spaces, which gather the urine. These funnel it into the ureter.

- Small cups called minor calyces collect the urine from the pyramid tips.
- The minor calyces join into larger major calyces.
- The major calyces join into a central space called the renal pelvis.
- The renal pelvis funnels the urine into the ureter.

---

## Why the Internal Divisions Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The internal divisions underlie how urine is made and gathered relevant to your patients. Each point below connects them to practice.

- The cortex and medulla are where urine is made, relevant to kidney function.
- The collecting spaces gather the urine, and their blockage, as by a stone, causes pain.
- Understanding the divisions clarifies how urine is made and gathered.
- Kidney stones lodging in the collecting spaces cause loin pain you may need to recognise.
- Understanding the divisions grounds your reasoning about the kidney.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to an outer rind, inner cones, and a central funnel.

- It is a mistake to confuse the cortex and medulla, since one is outer and the other inner.
- It is a mistake to overlook that the pyramids drain urine toward the centre.
- It is a mistake to muddle the order of the collecting spaces.
- It is a mistake to forget that the renal pelvis funnels urine into the ureter.
