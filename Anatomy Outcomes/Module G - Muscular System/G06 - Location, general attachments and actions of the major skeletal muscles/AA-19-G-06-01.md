## Identify the location, general attachments, and actions of the major skeletal muscles and demonstrate these muscle actions

# Location, Attachments, and Actions of the Major Muscles

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Identify the location, general attachments, and actions of the major skeletal muscles and demonstrate these muscle actions.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

Knowing the major muscles means knowing three things for each: where it is, what it attaches to, and what it does when it contracts. These three are linked, because a muscle's action follows from its attachments and the joint it crosses. Today we identify the location, attachments, and actions of the major skeletal muscles and how to demonstrate them.

- By the end of this session you will be able to state how a muscle's action follows from its attachments.
- You will be able to identify the location and action of major upper limb and trunk muscles.
- You will be able to identify the location and action of major lower limb muscles.
- You will be able to demonstrate the actions of these muscles.

---

## The Big Idea: Action Follows From Attachment and Joint Crossed

The organising idea is that a muscle's action is determined by the joint it crosses and the side on which it attaches, so that when a muscle shortens it pulls its attachments together and moves the joint in a predictable direction. Knowing where a muscle attaches predicts what it does. This idea that action follows attachment frames the whole topic.

- A muscle's action follows from the joint it crosses and where it attaches.
- When a muscle shortens, it pulls its two attachments toward each other.
- The side of the joint the muscle crosses determines the direction of movement.
- Knowing attachments lets you predict a muscle's action.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Cable Pulling a Hinged Beam

To keep the principle concrete, picture a cable running from a fixed post across a hinge to a movable beam: pull the cable and the beam swings toward the post, and which side of the hinge the cable crosses decides which way the beam moves. A muscle crossing a joint works this way, and the image keeps action following attachment in view.

- The fixed post corresponds to the muscle's origin, the more stationary attachment.
- The movable beam corresponds to the bone moved by the muscle, at its insertion.
- The side of the hinge the cable crosses corresponds to the side of the joint the muscle crosses.
- The analogy breaks down in that a single cable makes one movement, whereas muscles work in coordinated groups producing smooth, controlled movement.

---

## The Principle of Attachments and Action

Begin with the general principle, since it lets you reason about any muscle. Origin and insertion define the pull.

- A muscle's origin is its attachment to the more stationary bone.
- Its insertion is its attachment to the bone that moves.
- When the muscle contracts, the insertion is pulled toward the origin.
- The joint crossed and the side of attachment determine whether the movement is flexion, extension, or another action.

---

## Major Muscles of the Upper Body

Next the major muscles of the upper limb and trunk, with their locations and actions. Learn each with the movement it produces.

- The biceps brachii lies on the front of the arm and flexes the elbow and supinates the forearm.
- The triceps brachii lies on the back of the arm and extends the elbow.
- The deltoid caps the shoulder and abducts the arm.
- The pectoralis major lies on the front of the chest and flexes and adducts the arm.

---

## Major Muscles of the Lower Body

Finally the major muscles of the lower limb, with their locations and actions. These drive standing and walking.

- The quadriceps femoris lies on the front of the thigh and extends the knee.
- The hamstrings lie on the back of the thigh and flex the knee and extend the hip.
- The gluteus maximus lies over the buttock and extends the hip.
- The gastrocnemius lies on the back of the calf and plantarflexes the ankle.

---

## Why This Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

Knowing muscle location, attachment, and action is fundamental to movement analysis and rehabilitation. Each point below connects the knowledge to practice.

- Predicting a muscle's action from its attachments underlies your analysis of movement and its impairments.
- Knowing locations lets you palpate and target specific muscles in treatment.
- Understanding actions lets you prescribe exercises that strengthen the intended muscle.
- Demonstrating actions helps you teach patients the movements you want them to perform.
- This knowledge is the everyday foundation of musculoskeletal assessment and exercise.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors weaken muscle knowledge, and naming them keeps it reliable. When unsure, return to the cable pulling a hinged beam.

- It is a mistake to memorise actions without linking them to attachments, since attachments predict action.
- It is a mistake to think a muscle produces only one action, since many cross more than one joint.
- It is a mistake to assume the origin never moves, since which attachment moves can depend on the task.
- It is a mistake to treat muscles as acting alone, since they work in coordinated groups.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Demonstrating each action aloud and physically is the strongest test.

- State how a muscle's action follows from its attachments from memory.
- Identify the location and action of the biceps brachii and triceps brachii.
- Identify the location and action of the quadriceps and hamstrings.
- Demonstrate the action of the deltoid and gluteus maximus.
- Explain why knowing attachments lets you predict action.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have linked muscle location, attachment, and action. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- A muscle's action follows from the joint it crosses and where it attaches.
- When a muscle shortens, its insertion is pulled toward its origin.
- Major upper body muscles include the biceps, triceps, deltoid, and pectoralis major.
- Major lower body muscles include the quadriceps, hamstrings, gluteus maximus, and gastrocnemius.
- Knowing location, attachment, and action is the foundation of movement analysis and exercise.
