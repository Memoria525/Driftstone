## Describe the structure, location, and function of ascending and descending spinal cord tracts

# The Ascending and Descending Spinal Cord Tracts

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Describe the structure, location, and function of ascending and descending spinal cord tracts.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The white matter of the spinal cord is organised into tracts, some carrying sensory information up to the brain and some carrying motor commands down to the muscles. Understanding them explains how the cord connects the body to the brain and how cord injury interrupts specific functions. Today we describe the structure, location, and function of the ascending and descending spinal cord tracts.

- By the end of this session you will be able to distinguish ascending from descending tracts.
- You will be able to state the function of each kind.
- You will be able to give an example of each.
- You will be able to connect the tracts to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Two-Way Traffic Between Body and Brain

The organising idea is that the cord's tracts carry two-way traffic, with ascending tracts carrying sensory information up to the brain and descending tracts carrying motor commands down to the muscles. Up for sensation, down for movement. This idea of two-way tract traffic frames the topic.

- The spinal cord's white matter is organised into tracts.
- Ascending tracts carry sensory information up to the brain.
- Descending tracts carry motor commands down from the brain.
- Together they connect the body to the brain in both directions.

---

## Our Master Analogy: An Up Escalator and a Down Escalator

To keep the tracts concrete, picture a building with an up escalator carrying people to the upper floors and a down escalator carrying them back down, each dedicated to one direction. The ascending and descending tracts are these dedicated escalators, and the image keeps the two directions of traffic in view.

- The up escalator corresponds to the ascending sensory tracts.
- The down escalator corresponds to the descending motor tracts.
- The dedicated directions correspond to each tract carrying one direction of information.
- The analogy breaks down in that escalators carry the same people both ways, whereas the tracts carry distinct sensory and motor information.

---

## The Ascending Tracts

Begin with the ascending tracts, which carry sensation up. These bring information to the brain.

- Ascending tracts carry sensory information from the body up to the brain.
- They convey sensations such as touch, pain, temperature, and position.
- An example is the tract carrying fine touch and position sense up the posterior part of the cord.
- Their fibres synapse and often cross on the way to the brain.

---

## The Descending Tracts

Next the descending tracts, which carry commands down. These bring commands to the muscles.

- Descending tracts carry motor commands from the brain down to the muscles.
- They convey the commands for voluntary and automatic movement.
- The corticospinal tract is the main descending tract for voluntary movement.
- Its fibres largely cross in the medulla, so each side of the brain controls the opposite side of the body.

---

## The Location and Crossing of the Tracts

Finally the location and crossing of the tracts, which matter for interpreting injury. The tracts run in the funiculi and often cross.

- The tracts run within the white funiculi of the cord, in consistent locations.
- Ascending sensory tracts and descending motor tracts occupy particular columns.
- Many tracts cross from one side to the other, either in the cord or in the brainstem.
- The crossing means a lesion can affect functions on the opposite side to its location.

---

## Why the Tracts Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The tracts underlie how cord injury produces its specific pattern of loss. Each point below connects the tracts to practice.

- Damage to ascending tracts causes sensory loss, while damage to descending tracts causes motor loss.
- The corticospinal tract carries voluntary movement, so its damage produces the weakness you rehabilitate.
- The crossing of tracts determines the side on which a deficit appears.
- The pattern of tract damage explains the mix of sensory and motor loss in a cord injury.
- Understanding the tracts grounds your interpretation of spinal cord injury.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors blur the tracts, and naming them keeps them distinct. When unsure, return to the up and down escalators.

- It is a mistake to think ascending tracts carry motor commands, since they carry sensation.
- It is a mistake to believe descending tracts carry sensation, since they carry motor commands.
- It is a mistake to overlook that many tracts cross to the opposite side.
- It is a mistake to treat the tracts as randomly located, since they run in consistent columns.

---

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Distinguishing the tracts aloud is the strongest test.

- Distinguish ascending from descending tracts from memory.
- State the function of each kind.
- Give an example of each.
- Explain how the crossing of tracts affects the side of a deficit.
- Relate the tracts to a physiotherapy-relevant injury.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have described the cord's ascending and descending tracts. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- Ascending tracts carry sensory information up to the brain.
- Descending tracts carry motor commands down to the muscles, chiefly the corticospinal tract.
- The tracts run in consistent columns within the white matter.
- Many tracts cross to the opposite side, in the cord or brainstem.
- Damage to ascending tracts causes sensory loss and to descending tracts motor loss.
