# The Cross-Section of the Spinal Cord

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Identify and describe the anatomical features seen in a cross-sectional view of the spinal cord (anterior horn, lateral horn, posterior horn, gray commissure, central canal, anterior funiculus, lateral funiculus, posterior funiculus).

---

## Where We Are Going Today

A cross-section of the spinal cord reveals a central butterfly of gray matter surrounded by white matter, with each region carrying particular kinds of fibre. Learning these features lets you understand where sensory and motor processing occur and where the tracts run. Today we identify and describe the features seen in a cross-section of the spinal cord.

- By the end of this session you will be able to describe the gray matter horns of the cord.
- You will be able to describe the central canal and gray commissure.
- You will be able to describe the white matter funiculi.
- You will be able to connect the cross-section to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: A Gray Butterfly of Processing Within White Columns of Tracts

The organising idea is that the cord's central gray matter, shaped like a butterfly, processes sensory input at the back and issues motor output at the front, while the surrounding white matter carries the ascending and descending tracts. Central processing within surrounding tracts. This idea of a gray core within white columns frames the topic.

- In cross-section the cord has central gray matter surrounded by white matter.
- The gray matter is shaped like a butterfly or the letter H.
- Its horns process sensory input and issue motor output.
- The surrounding white matter carries the ascending and descending tracts.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Processing Core Wrapped in Cable Trunks

To keep the cross-section concrete, picture a central processing core with an input section at the back and an output section at the front, wrapped by bundles of cable trunks carrying signals up and down. The gray matter is the processing core and the white funiculi are the cable trunks, and the image keeps processing and transmission in view.

- The input section at the back corresponds to the posterior horn receiving sensation.
- The output section at the front corresponds to the anterior horn issuing motor commands.
- The wrapping cable trunks correspond to the white funiculi carrying tracts.
- The analogy breaks down in that cable trunks are passive, whereas the funiculi carry actively conducting axons.

---

## The Gray Matter Horns

Begin with the gray matter horns, which process input and output. These are the anterior, lateral, and posterior horns.

- The posterior horn contains sensory neurons receiving information from the posterior roots.
- The anterior horn contains motor neurons whose axons leave through the anterior roots.
- The lateral horn, present in some regions, contains autonomic neurons.
- The horns are the processing regions of the cord.

---

## The Central Canal and Gray Commissure

Next the central features connecting the two sides. These are the central canal and gray commissure.

- The central canal runs down the centre of the cord and contains cerebrospinal fluid.
- It is the continuation of the ventricular system into the cord.
- The gray commissure is the bridge of gray matter connecting the two sides around the central canal.
- It allows the two halves of the gray matter to communicate.

---

## The White Matter Funiculi

Finally the white matter columns, which carry the tracts. These are the anterior, lateral, and posterior funiculi.

- The white matter is divided into columns called funiculi.
- The anterior funiculus lies at the front, the lateral funiculus at the side, and the posterior funiculus at the back.
- Each funiculus carries bundles of ascending and descending tracts.
- The tracts within them carry sensory information up and motor commands down.

---

## Why the Cross-Section Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The cord's cross-section underlies how injuries at different sites present. Each point below connects the features to practice.

- Damage to the anterior horn causes motor loss, while damage to the posterior horn affects sensation.
- Damage to the white funiculi interrupts the tracts, affecting pathways passing through.
- The pattern of loss in a cord injury reflects which regions of the cross-section are affected.
- Understanding the cross-section helps you interpret spinal cord injury.
- Understanding the features grounds your reasoning about cord-level deficits.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to a processing core wrapped in cable trunks.

- It is a mistake to place motor neurons in the posterior horn, since they lie in the anterior horn.
- It is a mistake to think the gray matter is on the outside, since in the cord it is central.
- It is a mistake to overlook the lateral horn's autonomic role.
- It is a mistake to confuse the funiculi with the horns, since one carries tracts and the other processes.

## Key Takeaways

We have described the cord's cross-section. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The cord has central butterfly-shaped gray matter surrounded by white matter.
- The posterior horn receives sensation, the anterior horn issues motor commands, and the lateral horn handles autonomic function.
- The central canal carries cerebrospinal fluid, and the gray commissure connects the two sides.
- The white funiculi, anterior, lateral, and posterior, carry the ascending and descending tracts.
- The cross-section underlies how injuries at different sites present.
