# The Gross Anatomy of the Spinal Cord

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Identify and describe the gross anatomy of the spinal cord, including its enlargements (cervical, lumbar), conus medullaris, cauda equina, and filum terminale.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The spinal cord is the great cable connecting the brain to the body, with swellings where the limbs attach and a tapering end that gives rise to a bundle of nerve roots. Understanding its gross anatomy explains where injuries have their effects and why lumbar puncture is safe below a certain level. Today we identify and describe the gross anatomy of the spinal cord.

- By the end of this session you will be able to describe the overall form of the spinal cord.
- You will be able to identify the cervical and lumbar enlargements.
- You will be able to describe the conus medullaris, cauda equina, and filum terminale.
- You will be able to connect the cord's anatomy to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: A Tapering Cable With Limb Enlargements and a Trailing Root Bundle

The organising idea is that the spinal cord is a cable that swells where the limb nerves attach, tapers to an end partway down the back, and continues below as a bundle of nerve roots. Enlargements for the limbs and a trailing root bundle. This idea of a tapering cable with enlargements frames the topic.

- The spinal cord runs from the brainstem down the vertebral canal.
- It has enlargements where the nerves for the limbs attach.
- It tapers to an end, the conus medullaris, partway down the back.
- Below its end, nerve roots continue as the cauda equina, and a thread anchors the cord.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Cable Thicker at Its Branch Points With a Frayed End

To keep the anatomy concrete, picture a cable that is thicker where major branches leave it, tapers to a point, and frays into a bundle of loose wires below that point, with a fine thread tying the end down. The spinal cord is this cable, and the image keeps the enlargements, tapering end, and trailing roots in view.

- The thicker branch points correspond to the cervical and lumbar enlargements.
- The tapering point corresponds to the conus medullaris.
- The frayed bundle of loose wires corresponds to the cauda equina.
- The fine tying thread corresponds to the filum terminale, though the analogy breaks down in that a cable is inert while the cord is living nervous tissue.

---

## The Form and Enlargements

Begin with the overall form and the two enlargements. The cord swells where the limbs attach.

- The spinal cord is a cylinder of nervous tissue running down the vertebral canal.
- It has a cervical enlargement, where the nerves for the upper limbs attach.
- It has a lumbar enlargement, where the nerves for the lower limbs attach.
- The enlargements are thicker because more nerve cells serve the limbs.

---

## The Conus Medullaris and Filum Terminale

Next the tapering end and its anchor. The cord ends partway down the back.

- The spinal cord tapers to a cone-shaped end called the conus medullaris.
- In the adult, the cord ends at about the level of the first or second lumbar vertebra.
- The filum terminale is a fine thread of connective tissue extending from the conus.
- The filum terminale anchors the cord to the base of the vertebral canal.

---

## The Cauda Equina

Finally the bundle of roots below the cord's end. These continue down within the canal.

- Below the conus medullaris, the nerve roots for the lower body continue down the vertebral canal.
- This bundle of nerve roots is called the cauda equina, meaning horse's tail.
- The roots travel down before leaving the canal at their appropriate levels.
- Because the cord itself ends higher, this lower region contains only roots, not cord.

---

## Why the Cord's Anatomy Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The cord's gross anatomy underlies injury patterns and clinical procedures. Each point below connects it to practice.

- The cervical and lumbar enlargements serve the limbs, so injuries there affect limb function you rehabilitate.
- Because the cord ends around the first or second lumbar vertebra, lumbar puncture is performed below this level to avoid the cord.
- Injury below the cord's end affects the cauda equina roots, producing a distinct pattern you must recognise.
- The level of a cord injury determines which functions are affected.
- Understanding the cord's anatomy grounds your reasoning about spinal injury.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture of the cord, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to the cable with a frayed end.

- It is a mistake to think the cord runs the full length of the vertebral canal, since it ends around the first or second lumbar vertebra.
- It is a mistake to confuse the conus medullaris with the cauda equina, since one is the cord's end and the other the trailing roots.
- It is a mistake to overlook the enlargements serving the limbs.
- It is a mistake to forget the filum terminale anchoring the cord.

## Key Takeaways

We have described the gross anatomy of the spinal cord. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The spinal cord runs down the vertebral canal with cervical and lumbar enlargements serving the limbs.
- It tapers to the conus medullaris at about the first or second lumbar vertebra.
- The filum terminale anchors the cord's end to the base of the canal.
- Below the cord's end, the cauda equina is a bundle of nerve roots.
- The cord's anatomy underlies injury patterns and the safe level for lumbar puncture.
