## Describe the composition and arrangement of the gray and white matter in the CNS

# Gray and White Matter in the Central Nervous System

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Describe the composition and arrangement of the gray and white matter in the CNS.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The brain and spinal cord are built from two kinds of nervous tissue, gray matter and white matter, arranged in a pattern that reverses between the two organs. Understanding what each is made of and where it lies is the foundation for reading brain and cord anatomy. Today we describe the composition and arrangement of gray and white matter in the central nervous system.

- By the end of this session you will be able to state what gray and white matter are made of.
- You will be able to explain why each has its colour.
- You will be able to describe how they are arranged in the brain and spinal cord.
- You will be able to connect the arrangement to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Processing Tissue and Cabling Tissue

The organising idea is that gray matter is where neurons process information, while white matter is the myelinated cabling that connects processing regions, so the two are the workspaces and the wiring of the central nervous system. One computes; the other transmits. This processing-and-cabling contrast frames the topic.

- Gray matter is where neurons process and integrate information.
- White matter is the myelinated axons connecting regions.
- Gray matter is the workspace and white matter is the wiring.
- Their arrangement differs between the brain and the spinal cord.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Offices and the Cabling Between Them

To keep the two tissues concrete, picture a building with offices where work is done and cable trunks running between them carrying signals. Gray matter is the offices, and white matter is the cabling. The white cabling is pale because it is sheathed, and the offices are darker. The image keeps processing and wiring distinct.

- The offices where work is done correspond to the gray matter.
- The cable trunks between offices correspond to the white matter.
- The pale sheathing on the cables corresponds to the myelin that makes white matter white.
- The analogy breaks down in that cabling is passive, whereas white matter axons actively conduct and their myelin shapes the speed of conduction.

---

## The Composition of Gray and White Matter

Begin with what each tissue is made of, since composition explains colour. Cell bodies versus myelinated axons.

- Gray matter is composed mainly of neuron cell bodies, dendrites, and unmyelinated fibres.
- Its lack of myelin gives it a grayish colour.
- White matter is composed mainly of myelinated axons.
- The fatty myelin sheaths give it a white colour.

---

## The Arrangement in the Brain

Next the arrangement in the brain, where gray matter lies at the surface. The pattern is outer gray.

- In the brain, gray matter forms an outer layer, the cortex, over the surface.
- White matter lies beneath the cortex, connecting cortical regions.
- Additional islands of gray matter, the deep nuclei, lie within the white matter.
- So in the brain the pattern is gray outside and white inside, with deep gray nuclei.

---

## The Arrangement in the Spinal Cord

Finally the arrangement in the spinal cord, which reverses the brain's pattern. The pattern is inner gray.

- In the spinal cord, gray matter lies in the centre, forming an H or butterfly shape.
- White matter surrounds the central gray matter.
- So in the cord the pattern is gray inside and white outside.
- This is the reverse of the brain's arrangement.

---

## Why This Arrangement Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The gray and white matter arrangement underlies how injuries at different sites present. Each point below connects the arrangement to practice.

- Damage to gray matter affects processing at that site, while damage to white matter interrupts connections.
- A spinal cord injury damaging the central gray affects local function, while damage to the surrounding white matter interrupts the tracts passing through.
- Cortical gray matter damage, as in stroke, affects the function localised to that region.
- White matter damage interrupts the pathways between regions, producing disconnection effects.
- Understanding the arrangement grounds your reasoning about the site of neurological injury.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to offices and cabling.

- It is a mistake to think the arrangement is the same in the brain and cord, since it reverses between them.
- It is a mistake to believe white matter processes information, since that is the role of gray matter.
- It is a mistake to place all gray matter at the surface, since the brain also has deep gray nuclei.
- It is a mistake to overlook that myelin is what makes white matter white.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Describing the arrangement in each organ aloud is the strongest test.

- State what gray and white matter are made of from memory.
- Explain why each has its colour.
- Describe the arrangement of gray and white matter in the brain.
- Describe the arrangement in the spinal cord.
- Relate the arrangement to the site of a neurological injury.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have described the two tissues of the central nervous system. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- Gray matter is neuron cell bodies where information is processed.
- White matter is myelinated axons that connect regions, made white by myelin.
- In the brain, gray forms the outer cortex over inner white matter, with deep gray nuclei.
- In the spinal cord, gray matter is central and white matter surrounds it, reversing the brain's pattern.
- The arrangement underlies how injuries at different sites present.
