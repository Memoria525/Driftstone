## Compare and contrast the structure and location of a tract and nerve

# Tract and Nerve Compared

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Compare and contrast the structure and location of a tract and nerve.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

Bundles of axons are named differently depending on where they run, a tract in the central nervous system and a nerve in the peripheral. Distinguishing these terms parallels the nucleus-and-ganglion distinction and keeps your neuroanatomy precise. Today we compare and contrast the structure and location of a tract and a nerve.

- By the end of this session you will be able to define a tract and a nerve.
- You will be able to state where each is located.
- You will be able to explain what they have in common.
- You will be able to connect the terms to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Axon Bundles Named by Their Location

The organising idea is that a tract and a nerve are both bundles of axons, differing chiefly in location, with a tract in the central nervous system and a nerve in the peripheral. Same kind of bundle, different address. This idea of naming axon bundles by location frames the comparison.

- A tract and a nerve are both bundles of axons.
- A tract lies within the central nervous system.
- A nerve lies within the peripheral nervous system.
- The two are distinguished mainly by their location.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Internal Cabling Versus External Cables

To keep the terms concrete, picture a building whose signal cables run either within the walls of the head office or out along external lines to the branches. The internal cabling is a tract, and the external cable is a nerve, though both are bundles of wires. The image keeps location as the distinguishing feature.

- The internal cabling within the head office corresponds to a tract in the central nervous system.
- The external cable to the branches corresponds to a nerve in the peripheral nervous system.
- The bundles of wires in both correspond to the bundled axons.
- The analogy breaks down in that building cables are passive, whereas the axons of tracts and nerves actively conduct and, in the periphery, can regenerate.

---

## Defining a Tract

Begin with the tract, the central bundle. It forms the white matter of the brain and cord.

- A tract is a bundle of axons within the central nervous system.
- It runs within the brain and spinal cord.
- Tracts make up the white matter, connecting regions of the central nervous system.
- Named tracts carry specific kinds of information, such as the corticospinal tract for movement.

---

## Defining a Nerve

Next the nerve, the peripheral bundle. It carries information to and from the body.

- A nerve is a bundle of axons within the peripheral nervous system.
- It runs outside the brain and spinal cord.
- Nerves connect the central nervous system to the muscles, skin, and organs.
- Named nerves, such as the median nerve, carry sensory and motor information to and from body regions.

---

## Comparing the Two

Holding the two together lets us state the comparison cleanly, which the objective asks. They share structure but differ in location.

- Both a tract and a nerve are bundles of axons.
- A tract lies in the central nervous system, while a nerve lies in the peripheral.
- A tract forms part of the white matter, while a nerve runs through the body's tissues.
- The key difference is location, central versus peripheral.

---

## Why This Comparison Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

Distinguishing these terms underlies localising where a lesion interrupts a pathway. Each point below connects the comparison to practice.

- A lesion of a tract, as in the spinal cord, produces different signs from a lesion of a peripheral nerve.
- The corticospinal tract is the central pathway for voluntary movement you assess after central injury.
- Peripheral nerves can regenerate, while central tracts largely cannot, affecting recovery.
- Distinguishing tract from nerve helps you localise a lesion to the central or peripheral nervous system.
- Understanding the terms grounds your reasoning about pathways and their injury.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors blur the two terms, and naming them keeps them distinct. When unsure, return to internal cabling versus external cables.

- It is a mistake to call a central bundle a nerve, since within the central nervous system it is a tract.
- It is a mistake to place a tract in the peripheral nervous system, since it is central.
- It is a mistake to think the two differ in what they contain, since both are bundles of axons.
- It is a mistake to overlook that peripheral nerves regenerate better than central tracts.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Contrasting the two terms aloud is the strongest test.

- Define a tract and a nerve from memory.
- State where each is located.
- Explain what the two have in common.
- Give an example of a named tract and a named nerve.
- Relate the terms to localising a neurological lesion.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have compared two names for bundles of axons. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- A tract and a nerve are both bundles of axons.
- A tract lies in the central nervous system, and a nerve lies in the peripheral.
- Tracts form the white matter, while nerves run through the body's tissues.
- The corticospinal tract and the median nerve are examples of each.
- The two differ mainly in location, which affects how their injuries present and recover.
