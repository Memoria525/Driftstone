# The Three Major Regions of the Cerebrum

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Identify and describe the three major cerebral regions (cortex, white matter, cerebral nuclei [basal nuclei]).

---

## Where We Are Going Today

Cutting into the cerebrum reveals three regions: an outer shell of gray matter, an inner mass of white matter, and islands of gray matter buried deep within. Understanding them explains where processing happens and how it is connected. Today we identify and describe the three major cerebral regions.

- By the end of this session you will be able to name the three major cerebral regions.
- You will be able to describe what each is made of.
- You will be able to state the function of each.
- You will be able to connect these regions to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: A Gray Shell, White Wiring, and Deep Gray Islands

The organising idea is that the cerebrum has an outer gray cortex for processing, an inner white matter for connection, and deep gray nuclei that help control movement, so the three regions are the workspace, the wiring, and specialised deep control centres. Shell, wiring, and islands. This idea of three regions frames the topic.

- The cerebrum has three major regions.
- The cortex is the outer shell of gray matter where processing occurs.
- The white matter beneath connects cortical regions.
- The cerebral nuclei are islands of gray matter deep within the white matter.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Building With Offices, Cabling, and Central Control Rooms

To keep the regions concrete, picture a building with offices around its outer edge, cabling running through its interior, and a few central control rooms buried deep inside. The cortex is the outer offices, the white matter is the cabling, and the cerebral nuclei are the deep control rooms. The image keeps processing, connection, and deep control in view.

- The outer offices correspond to the cortex where processing occurs.
- The interior cabling corresponds to the white matter connecting regions.
- The deep control rooms correspond to the cerebral nuclei.
- The analogy breaks down in that a building's parts are installed separately, whereas the cerebral regions develop as one integrated organ.

---

## The Cerebral Cortex

Begin with the outer shell, where processing occurs. This is the cerebral cortex.

- The cerebral cortex is the outer layer of gray matter covering the cerebrum.
- It consists mainly of neuron cell bodies and is folded into gyri and sulci.
- It is where conscious processing, voluntary movement, sensation, and higher functions occur.
- It is the site of the primary functional areas of the brain.

---

## The Cerebral White Matter

Next the inner mass, which connects the cortex. This is the white matter.

- The cerebral white matter lies beneath the cortex.
- It consists mainly of myelinated axons connecting regions of the brain.
- It links cortical areas to one another and to other parts of the nervous system.
- It is the wiring through which the cortex communicates.

---

## The Cerebral Nuclei

Finally the deep gray islands, which help control movement. These are the cerebral, or basal, nuclei.

- The cerebral nuclei, also called the basal nuclei, are islands of gray matter deep within the white matter.
- They consist of clusters of neuron cell bodies.
- They help regulate and coordinate voluntary movement.
- Their dysfunction is involved in movement disorders such as Parkinson disease.

---

## Why These Regions Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The three regions underlie processing, connection, and movement control relevant to rehabilitation. Each point below connects a region to practice.

- The cortex houses the motor and sensory areas whose injury produces the deficits you rehabilitate.
- The white matter connects regions, so its damage interrupts pathways and disconnects functions.
- The cerebral nuclei regulate movement, and their dysfunction causes movement disorders you treat.
- Localising a lesion to one region helps you interpret the deficit.
- Understanding the regions grounds your reasoning about brain injury and movement disorders.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to the building with offices, cabling, and control rooms.

- It is a mistake to think all gray matter is at the surface, since the cerebral nuclei are deep.
- It is a mistake to believe white matter processes information, since that is the cortex's role.
- It is a mistake to overlook the cerebral nuclei's role in movement.
- It is a mistake to treat the regions as unconnected, since the white matter links them.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Naming each region and its role aloud is the strongest test.

- Name the three major cerebral regions from memory.
- Describe what each is made of.
- State the function of each.
- Explain the role of the cerebral nuclei in movement.
- Relate one region to a physiotherapy-relevant condition.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have described the three regions of the cerebrum. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The cerebral cortex is the outer gray matter where processing occurs.
- The white matter beneath is myelinated axons connecting regions.
- The cerebral nuclei are deep gray islands that help regulate voluntary movement.
- Their dysfunction is involved in movement disorders such as Parkinson disease.
- The regions are the processing shell, the connecting wiring, and the deep control centres.
