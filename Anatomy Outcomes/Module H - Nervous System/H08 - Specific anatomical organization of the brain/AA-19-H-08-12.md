## Compare and contrast the three cerebral white matter tracts (i.e., association, commissural, projection)

# The Three Cerebral White Matter Tracts

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Compare and contrast the three cerebral white matter tracts (association, commissural, projection).

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The white matter of the cerebrum is organised into three kinds of tract, each connecting different targets: within a hemisphere, between hemispheres, or between the cortex and lower structures. Learning them explains how the brain's regions communicate. Today we compare and contrast the three cerebral white matter tracts.

- By the end of this session you will be able to name the three white matter tracts.
- You will be able to state what each connects.
- You will be able to give an example of each.
- You will be able to connect the tracts to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Tracts Named by What They Connect

The organising idea is that the three white matter tracts are defined by what they connect, so association tracts link regions within a hemisphere, commissural tracts link the two hemispheres, and projection tracts link the cortex with lower structures. Connection target names the tract. This idea of naming by connection frames the comparison.

- The cerebral white matter has three kinds of tract.
- Association tracts connect regions within the same hemisphere.
- Commissural tracts connect the two hemispheres.
- Projection tracts connect the cortex with lower structures such as the brainstem and cord.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Three Kinds of Cabling in a Building

To keep the tracts concrete, picture three kinds of cabling in a building: cables linking rooms on the same floor, cables crossing between the two wings, and cables running up and down between the floors and the outside world. The three tracts are these three kinds of cabling, and the image keeps their connection targets in view.

- The same-floor cabling corresponds to the association tracts within a hemisphere.
- The cabling between the two wings corresponds to the commissural tracts between hemispheres.
- The up-and-down cabling corresponds to the projection tracts to and from lower structures.
- The analogy breaks down in that building cabling is installed, whereas the tracts develop as bundles of living axons.

---

## Association Tracts

Begin with the tracts within a hemisphere, linking its own regions. These are the association tracts.

- Association tracts connect different regions within the same cerebral hemisphere.
- They link cortical areas so that they can work together.
- They allow, for example, sensory and motor areas on one side to coordinate.
- They keep communication within a single hemisphere.

---

## Commissural Tracts

Next the tracts between the hemispheres, linking the two sides. These are the commissural tracts.

- Commissural tracts connect corresponding regions of the two hemispheres.
- They allow the two hemispheres to communicate and coordinate.
- The largest commissural tract is the corpus callosum.
- They integrate the activity of the left and right sides of the brain.

---

## Projection Tracts

Finally the tracts between the cortex and lower structures, carrying signals in and out. These are the projection tracts.

- Projection tracts connect the cortex with lower structures such as the brainstem and spinal cord.
- They carry motor commands down from the cortex and sensory information up to it.
- The corticospinal tract, carrying voluntary movement commands, is a projection tract.
- They are the routes by which the cortex communicates with the rest of the nervous system.

---

## Why These Tracts Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The tracts underlie how brain regions communicate and how their damage disconnects functions. Each point below connects a tract to practice.

- Projection tracts such as the corticospinal tract carry voluntary movement commands, so their damage produces weakness you rehabilitate.
- Commissural damage can impair coordination between the two sides of the body.
- Association tract damage can disconnect cooperating regions within a hemisphere.
- Understanding the tracts clarifies how a white matter lesion disconnects functions.
- Understanding the tracts grounds your reasoning about brain connectivity and injury.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors blur the tracts, and naming them keeps them distinct. When unsure, return to three kinds of cabling in a building.

- It is a mistake to confuse commissural and projection tracts, since one links hemispheres and the other links the cortex to lower structures.
- It is a mistake to think association tracts cross between hemispheres, since they stay within one.
- It is a mistake to overlook that the corticospinal tract is a projection tract.
- It is a mistake to treat all white matter as one undifferentiated mass.

---

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Naming each tract and what it connects aloud is the strongest test.

- Name the three white matter tracts from memory.
- State what each connects.
- Give an example of each.
- Explain which tract carries voluntary movement commands.
- Relate one tract to a physiotherapy-relevant deficit.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have compared the three cerebral white matter tracts. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- Association tracts connect regions within the same hemisphere.
- Commissural tracts connect the two hemispheres, the largest being the corpus callosum.
- Projection tracts connect the cortex with lower structures, including the corticospinal tract for movement.
- Each tract is named by what it connects.
- The tracts underlie how brain regions communicate and how white matter damage disconnects functions.
