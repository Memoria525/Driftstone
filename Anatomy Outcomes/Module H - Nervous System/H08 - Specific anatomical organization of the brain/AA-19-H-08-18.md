## Describe the structure, location and major functions of the midbrain (mesencephalon), including the cerebral peduncles, superior colliculi, and inferior colliculi

# The Midbrain

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Describe the structure, location and major functions of the midbrain (mesencephalon), including the cerebral peduncles, superior colliculi, and inferior colliculi.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The midbrain is the top segment of the brainstem, carrying movement pathways and hosting reflex centres for vision and hearing. Understanding its structures explains how we orient to sights and sounds and how motor commands pass through. Today we describe the structure, location, and functions of the midbrain.

- By the end of this session you will be able to locate the midbrain.
- You will be able to describe the cerebral peduncles and their function.
- You will be able to describe the superior and inferior colliculi and their functions.
- You will be able to connect the midbrain to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: A Movement Highway With Reflex Relay Stations

The organising idea is that the midbrain carries movement pathways on its front through the cerebral peduncles, while its back carries reflex centres for visual and auditory orienting in the colliculi. A movement highway with orienting relays. This idea of a highway with reflex stations frames the topic.

- The midbrain is the uppermost part of the brainstem.
- Its front contains the cerebral peduncles, carrying movement pathways.
- Its back contains the superior and inferior colliculi, reflex centres for vision and hearing.
- It relays movement commands and helps orient the body to sights and sounds.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Bridge Carrying a Highway With Lookout Posts

To keep the midbrain concrete, picture a bridge that carries a busy highway across its front while lookout posts on its back watch for sights and sounds and trigger the body to turn toward them. The cerebral peduncles are the highway and the colliculi are the lookout posts, and the image keeps movement and orienting in view.

- The highway across the front corresponds to the cerebral peduncles carrying movement pathways.
- The lookout posts for sights correspond to the superior colliculi for visual reflexes.
- The lookout posts for sounds correspond to the inferior colliculi for auditory reflexes.
- The analogy breaks down in that a bridge is static, whereas the midbrain actively processes and relays signals.

---

## The Location of the Midbrain

Begin with where the midbrain lies, since it is the top of the brainstem. It sits below the diencephalon.

- The midbrain is the uppermost subdivision of the brainstem.
- It lies just below the diencephalon and above the pons.
- The cerebral aqueduct, connecting the third and fourth ventricles, runs through it.
- It connects the forebrain above with the lower brainstem below.

---

## The Cerebral Peduncles

Next the cerebral peduncles, on the front of the midbrain. These carry movement pathways.

- The cerebral peduncles are two large bundles on the front of the midbrain.
- They carry the major motor pathways descending from the cortex.
- These pathways include the fibres heading toward the spinal cord to control voluntary movement.
- They are the routes by which motor commands pass through the midbrain.

---

## The Superior and Inferior Colliculi

Finally the colliculi, on the back of the midbrain. These are reflex centres for vision and hearing.

- The superior colliculi are two bumps on the back of the midbrain involved in visual reflexes.
- They help orient the head and eyes toward visual stimuli.
- The inferior colliculi, below them, are involved in auditory reflexes.
- They help orient the head toward sounds.

---

## Why the Midbrain Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The midbrain carries movement pathways and orienting reflexes relevant to movement control. Each point below connects it to practice.

- The cerebral peduncles carry voluntary movement pathways, so midbrain injury affects movement.
- The orienting reflexes of the colliculi are relevant to how patients turn toward stimuli.
- Midbrain involvement in movement is relevant to some movement disorders.
- Understanding the midbrain clarifies how motor commands pass toward the cord.
- Understanding the midbrain grounds your reasoning about brainstem and movement pathways.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture of the midbrain, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to the bridge with a highway and lookout posts.

- It is a mistake to swap the colliculi, since the superior serve vision and the inferior serve hearing.
- It is a mistake to place the cerebral peduncles on the back, since they are on the front.
- It is a mistake to overlook that motor pathways pass through the cerebral peduncles.
- It is a mistake to think the midbrain has no role in movement.

- Locate the midbrain from memory.

## Key Takeaways

We have described the midbrain and its structures. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The midbrain is the uppermost part of the brainstem, below the diencephalon.
- The cerebral peduncles on its front carry major motor pathways descending from the cortex.
- The superior colliculi on its back serve visual reflexes and the inferior colliculi serve auditory reflexes.
- It relays movement commands and helps orient the body to sights and sounds.
- Its structures are relevant to movement pathways and orienting reflexes.
