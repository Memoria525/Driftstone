# The Four Major Parts of the Adult Brain

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Identify and describe the four major parts of the adult brain (cerebrum, diencephalon, brainstem, and cerebellum).

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The adult brain divides into four major parts, each with a broad role, and knowing them gives you a map onto which every finer structure can be placed. Understanding their locations and functions frames the whole of brain anatomy. Today we identify and describe the four major parts of the adult brain.

- By the end of this session you will be able to name the four major parts of the brain.
- You will be able to describe the location of each.
- You will be able to state the broad function of each.
- You will be able to connect the four parts to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: A Four-Part Map of the Brain

The organising idea is that the brain can be understood as four major parts, the cerebrum on top, the diencephalon deep in the centre, the brainstem below connecting to the cord, and the cerebellum behind, each with a broad functional role. A four-part map for everything else. This idea of a four-part map frames the topic.

- The adult brain divides into four major parts.
- The cerebrum is the large upper part, and the diencephalon lies deep beneath it.
- The brainstem lies below, connecting to the spinal cord.
- The cerebellum lies behind the brainstem.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Building With Floors, a Core, a Stem, and an Annex

To keep the parts concrete, picture a building with a large upper storey where most work happens, a central service core deep inside, a stem of essential utilities running down to the foundations, and an annex handling coordination. The four brain parts are these features, and the image keeps their positions and roles in view.

- The large upper storey corresponds to the cerebrum.
- The central service core corresponds to the diencephalon.
- The utility stem to the foundations corresponds to the brainstem connecting to the cord.
- The coordination annex corresponds to the cerebellum, though the analogy breaks down in that a building's parts are built separately while the brain's parts develop as one integrated organ.

---

## The Cerebrum and Diencephalon

Begin with the two forebrain-derived parts, the cerebrum and the diencephalon. These sit at the top and centre.

- The cerebrum is the largest part of the brain, forming its bulk and folded outer surface.
- It is responsible for voluntary movement, sensation, thought, and higher functions.
- The diencephalon lies deep within the centre of the brain, beneath the cerebrum.
- It includes the thalamus and hypothalamus and handles relay of information and regulation of body functions.

---

## The Brainstem

Next the brainstem, which connects the brain to the cord and controls vital functions. It lies below.

- The brainstem lies below the cerebrum and diencephalon, continuing into the spinal cord.
- It consists of the midbrain, pons, and medulla oblongata.
- It controls vital automatic functions such as breathing and heart rate.
- It carries the pathways passing between the brain and the spinal cord.

---

## The Cerebellum

Finally the cerebellum, which coordinates movement. It lies behind the brainstem.

- The cerebellum lies behind the brainstem, beneath the back of the cerebrum.
- It coordinates movement, balance, and posture.
- It refines and smooths voluntary movements planned elsewhere.
- Its name means little brain, reflecting its folded, brain-like appearance.

---

## Why the Four Parts Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The four parts underlie the functions you assess and the effects of injury at each site. Each point below connects a part to practice.

- The cerebrum controls voluntary movement, so its injury, as in stroke, produces the deficits you rehabilitate.
- The diencephalon relays sensation and regulates body functions relevant to your patients.
- The brainstem controls vital functions, so its injury is life-threatening and affects many pathways.
- The cerebellum coordinates movement, so its injury causes the incoordination you assess and treat.
- Understanding the four parts grounds your localisation of neurological injury.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the map, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to the building with floors, a core, a stem, and an annex.

- It is a mistake to place the cerebellum within the cerebrum, since it is a separate part behind the brainstem.
- It is a mistake to overlook the diencephalon, since it is a distinct central part.
- It is a mistake to think the brainstem only relays signals, since it also controls vital functions.
- It is a mistake to confuse the roles of the cerebrum and cerebellum in movement.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Naming each part with its location and role aloud is the strongest test.

- Name the four major parts of the brain from memory.
- Describe the location of each.
- State the broad function of each.
- Distinguish the roles of the cerebrum and cerebellum in movement.
- Relate one part to a physiotherapy-relevant injury.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have mapped the brain into four major parts. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The cerebrum is the large upper part controlling voluntary movement, sensation, and higher functions.
- The diencephalon lies deep in the centre, relaying information and regulating body functions.
- The brainstem lies below, controlling vital functions and carrying pathways to the cord.
- The cerebellum lies behind, coordinating movement, balance, and posture.
- The four parts provide a map onto which finer structures and injuries are placed.
