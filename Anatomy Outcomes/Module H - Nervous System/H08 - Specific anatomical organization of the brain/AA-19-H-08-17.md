## Name the three subdivisions of the brainstem

# The Three Subdivisions of the Brainstem

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Name the three subdivisions of the brainstem.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The brainstem connects the brain to the spinal cord and controls the most vital automatic functions, and it is divided into three parts stacked in sequence. Naming them frames the detailed study of each. Today we name the three subdivisions of the brainstem.

- By the end of this session you will be able to name the three subdivisions of the brainstem.
- You will be able to describe their arrangement.
- You will be able to state the broad role of the brainstem.
- You will be able to connect the brainstem to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Three Stacked Segments Connecting Brain to Cord

The organising idea is that the brainstem is three segments stacked in sequence, the midbrain on top, the pons in the middle, and the medulla below, connecting the brain to the spinal cord and controlling vital functions. Three stacked segments. This idea of a three-part stalk frames the topic.

- The brainstem connects the brain to the spinal cord.
- It has three subdivisions stacked in sequence.
- From top to bottom they are the midbrain, pons, and medulla oblongata.
- Together they control vital functions and carry pathways between brain and cord.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Three-Section Utility Stem

To keep the subdivisions concrete, picture a three-section stem carrying utilities from a building's upper floors down to its foundations, each section handling part of the connection. The brainstem is this stem in three sections, and the image keeps the stacked arrangement in view.

- The upper section corresponds to the midbrain.
- The middle section corresponds to the pons.
- The lower section corresponds to the medulla oblongata.
- The analogy breaks down in that a utility stem only carries services, whereas the brainstem also generates vital control such as breathing.

---

## The Midbrain

Begin with the top subdivision, the midbrain. It sits just below the diencephalon.

- The midbrain is the uppermost subdivision of the brainstem.
- It lies just below the diencephalon.
- It contains pathways and centres involved in movement and reflexes.
- It connects the forebrain above to the pons below.

---

## The Pons

Next the middle subdivision, the pons. It bulges forward between the midbrain and medulla.

- The pons is the middle subdivision of the brainstem.
- It lies between the midbrain above and the medulla below.
- It bulges forward and connects to the cerebellum behind.
- It contains centres involved in breathing and carries pathways between brain regions.

---

## The Medulla Oblongata

Finally the bottom subdivision, the medulla. It continues into the spinal cord.

- The medulla oblongata is the lowest subdivision of the brainstem.
- It lies below the pons and continues into the spinal cord.
- It contains centres controlling vital functions such as heart rate and breathing.
- It is where major motor pathways cross from one side to the other.

---

## Why the Brainstem Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The brainstem controls vital functions and carries pathways central to movement. Each point below connects it to practice.

- The brainstem controls breathing and heart rate, so its injury is life-threatening.
- It carries the pathways between the brain and cord, so its damage affects movement and sensation.
- Its role in reflexes and posture is relevant to movement control.
- Brainstem injury produces distinctive patterns you must recognise.
- Understanding the brainstem grounds your reasoning about serious neurological injury.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to the three-section utility stem.

- It is a mistake to reverse the order, since the midbrain is uppermost and the medulla lowest.
- It is a mistake to place the cerebellum among the brainstem subdivisions, since it is a separate part.
- It is a mistake to think the brainstem only relays signals, since it controls vital functions.
- It is a mistake to overlook that motor pathways cross in the medulla.

## Key Takeaways

We have named the three subdivisions of the brainstem. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The brainstem connects the brain to the spinal cord.
- Its three subdivisions from top to bottom are the midbrain, pons, and medulla oblongata.
- The midbrain lies below the diencephalon, the pons connects to the cerebellum, and the medulla continues into the cord.
- Together they control vital functions and carry pathways between brain and cord.
- The subdivisions frame the detailed study of each that follows.
