## Identify and describe the primary functional cortical areas of the cerebrum (e.g., primary motor cortex, primary somatosensory cortex, primary auditory cortex, primary visual cortex, primary olfactory cortex, primary gustatory cortex)

# The Primary Functional Cortical Areas

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Identify and describe the primary functional cortical areas of the cerebrum (primary motor cortex, primary somatosensory cortex, primary auditory cortex, primary visual cortex, primary olfactory cortex, primary gustatory cortex).

---

## Where We Are Going Today

Specific regions of the cortex are dedicated to specific functions, so that movement, touch, hearing, vision, smell, and taste each have a primary area. Learning where these primary areas lie lets you predict the deficit from the site of an injury. Today we identify and describe the primary functional cortical areas of the cerebrum.

- By the end of this session you will be able to name the primary functional cortical areas.
- You will be able to state the location of each.
- You will be able to state the function of each.
- You will be able to connect these areas to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Dedicated Areas for Specific Functions

The organising idea is that the cortex has primary areas each dedicated to a specific function, located in the lobe suited to that function, so that damage to an area produces a predictable, specific deficit. One area, one function. This idea of dedicated functional areas frames the topic.

- The cortex contains primary areas dedicated to specific functions.
- Each primary area lies in the lobe suited to its function.
- Motor and sensory areas cluster around the central sulcus, and the senses lie in their respective lobes.
- Damage to a primary area produces a specific, predictable deficit.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Specialist Departments at Fixed Addresses

To keep the areas concrete, picture an organisation with specialist departments each at a fixed address, one for action, one for touch, one for hearing, one for vision, and so on. The primary cortical areas are these departments at fixed brain addresses, and the image keeps one-area-one-function in view.

- The action department corresponds to the primary motor cortex.
- The touch department corresponds to the primary somatosensory cortex.
- The vision and hearing departments correspond to the primary visual and auditory cortices.
- The analogy breaks down in that departments can relocate, whereas the primary areas are fixed in their cortical locations.

---

## The Motor and Somatosensory Areas

Begin with the movement and touch areas, which flank the central sulcus. These are the primary motor and somatosensory cortices.

- The primary motor cortex lies in the precentral gyrus of the frontal lobe.
- It controls voluntary movement of the opposite side of the body.
- The primary somatosensory cortex lies in the postcentral gyrus of the parietal lobe.
- It receives touch, pressure, temperature, and position sense from the opposite side of the body.

---

## The Visual and Auditory Areas

Next the vision and hearing areas, in the back and side of the brain. These are the primary visual and auditory cortices.

- The primary visual cortex lies in the occipital lobe at the back of the brain.
- It processes visual information from the eyes.
- The primary auditory cortex lies in the temporal lobe.
- It processes sound information from the ears.

---

## The Olfactory and Gustatory Areas

Finally the smell and taste areas, which are less obvious in location. These are the primary olfactory and gustatory cortices.

- The primary olfactory cortex lies in the temporal lobe and processes smell.
- The primary gustatory cortex lies near the insula and processes taste.
- These areas handle the chemical senses.
- They complete the set of primary functional areas.

---

## Why These Areas Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The primary areas let you predict deficits from the site of brain injury. Each point below connects an area to practice.

- The primary motor cortex controls movement, so its injury produces the weakness you rehabilitate.
- The primary somatosensory cortex processes touch and position, so its injury affects sensation and body awareness you assess.
- The visual cortex processes vision, relevant to visual deficits after stroke.
- Because these areas control the opposite side, a lesion on one side produces deficits on the other.
- Localising a deficit to a primary area helps you interpret and plan rehabilitation.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the map, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to specialist departments at fixed addresses.

- It is a mistake to place vision in the frontal lobe, since it is processed in the occipital lobe.
- It is a mistake to swap the motor and somatosensory areas across the central sulcus.
- It is a mistake to think an area controls its own side, since the motor and sensory areas serve the opposite side.
- It is a mistake to overlook the chemical sense areas for smell and taste.

---

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Naming each area with its location and function aloud is the strongest test.

- Name the primary functional cortical areas from memory.
- State the location of each.
- State the function of each.
- Explain why a lesion on one side affects the opposite side of the body.
- Relate one area to a physiotherapy-relevant deficit.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have mapped the primary functional cortical areas. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The primary motor cortex is in the precentral gyrus and controls opposite-side movement.
- The primary somatosensory cortex is in the postcentral gyrus and receives opposite-side sensation.
- The primary visual cortex is in the occipital lobe and the primary auditory cortex in the temporal lobe.
- The primary olfactory and gustatory cortices process smell and taste.
- Damage to a primary area produces a specific, predictable deficit.
