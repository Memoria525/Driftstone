# The Cerebral Hemispheres and Their Lobes

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Identify and describe the cerebral hemispheres and the five lobes of each (frontal, parietal, temporal, occipital, insula).

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The cerebrum is divided into two hemispheres, each split into five lobes with broadly different roles. Learning the lobes gives you a functional map of the cerebral surface onto which strokes and lesions can be located. Today we identify and describe the cerebral hemispheres and their five lobes.

- By the end of this session you will be able to describe the cerebral hemispheres.
- You will be able to name the five lobes of each hemisphere.
- You will be able to state the broad function of each lobe.
- You will be able to connect the lobes to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Two Hemispheres, Each With Five Functional Lobes

The organising idea is that the cerebrum is divided into two hemispheres, each with five lobes broadly specialised for different functions, so the lobes form a functional map of the cerebral surface. A five-part map on each side. This idea of a lobed functional map frames the topic.

- The cerebrum is divided into left and right hemispheres.
- Each hemisphere is divided into five lobes.
- The lobes are the frontal, parietal, temporal, occipital, and insula.
- Each lobe is broadly specialised for particular functions.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Building With Named Wings for Different Departments

To keep the lobes concrete, picture a large building divided into two mirror halves, each with named wings housing different departments, one for planning and action, one for sensation, one for hearing and memory, one for vision, and a hidden inner wing. The lobes are these wings, and the image keeps the functional map in view.

- The planning-and-action wing corresponds to the frontal lobe.
- The sensation wing corresponds to the parietal lobe, and the vision wing to the occipital lobe.
- The hearing-and-memory wing corresponds to the temporal lobe.
- The hidden inner wing corresponds to the insula, though the analogy breaks down in that building wings are separate while the lobes are continuous regions of one folded sheet.

---

## The Cerebral Hemispheres

Begin with the two hemispheres, which are broadly mirror images. They control opposite sides of the body.

- The cerebrum is divided into left and right hemispheres by the longitudinal fissure.
- The two hemispheres are connected by a band of white matter that lets them communicate.
- Each hemisphere largely controls and receives sensation from the opposite side of the body.
- The hemispheres show some specialisation, such as language in one hemisphere.

---

## The Frontal, Parietal, and Occipital Lobes

Next three of the lobes, for action, sensation, and vision. These occupy the front, top, and back.

- The frontal lobe, at the front, is responsible for voluntary movement, planning, and personality.
- The parietal lobe, behind it, processes touch and other body sensations.
- The occipital lobe, at the back, processes vision.
- Each is separated from its neighbours by named sulci or fissures.

---

## The Temporal Lobe and Insula

Finally the temporal lobe and the hidden insula. These handle hearing, memory, and deeper functions.

- The temporal lobe, at the side, processes hearing and contributes to memory.
- It lies below the lateral sulcus that separates it from the frontal and parietal lobes.
- The insula is a fifth lobe hidden deep within the lateral sulcus.
- The insula contributes to functions including taste and awareness of the internal body state.

---

## Why the Lobes Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The lobes underlie the functions affected by stroke and brain injury you rehabilitate. Each point below connects a lobe to practice.

- The frontal lobe controls voluntary movement, so its injury produces the weakness you rehabilitate.
- The parietal lobe processes sensation, so its injury affects the touch and body awareness you assess.
- The occipital lobe processes vision, relevant to visual deficits after brain injury.
- The temporal lobe's role in memory affects rehabilitation and learning after injury.
- Localising a deficit to a lobe helps you interpret and plan rehabilitation.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the map, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to the building with named wings.

- It is a mistake to forget the insula, the fifth and hidden lobe.
- It is a mistake to place vision in the frontal lobe, since it is processed in the occipital lobe.
- It is a mistake to think each hemisphere controls its own side, since it largely controls the opposite side.
- It is a mistake to treat the lobes as sharply separate, since they are continuous regions of one sheet.

---

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Naming each lobe with its function aloud is the strongest test.

- Describe the cerebral hemispheres from memory.
- Name the five lobes of each hemisphere.
- State the broad function of each lobe.
- Explain which side of the body each hemisphere largely controls.
- Relate one lobe to a physiotherapy-relevant deficit.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have mapped the hemispheres and their lobes. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The cerebrum has left and right hemispheres, each largely controlling the opposite side of the body.
- Each hemisphere has five lobes: frontal, parietal, temporal, occipital, and insula.
- The frontal lobe governs movement and planning, the parietal sensation, and the occipital vision.
- The temporal lobe governs hearing and memory, and the insula deeper functions such as taste.
- The lobes form a functional map onto which strokes and lesions are located.
