# The Major Landmarks of the Cerebrum

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Identify and describe the major landmarks of the cerebrum (longitudinal fissure, lateral sulcus [fissure], central sulcus, transverse fissure, precentral gyrus, and postcentral gyrus).

---

## Where We Are Going Today

A few key grooves and ridges serve as landmarks that divide the cerebrum and locate its most important functional regions. Learning these landmarks lets you find the motor and sensory strips and the boundaries between lobes. Today we identify and describe the major landmarks of the cerebrum.

- By the end of this session you will be able to name the major cerebral landmarks.
- You will be able to describe what each landmark separates or marks.
- You will be able to locate the motor and sensory strips.
- You will be able to connect these landmarks to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Landmarks That Divide the Brain and Locate Function

The organising idea is that a handful of major grooves divide the cerebrum into hemispheres and lobes, and two key gyrus landmarks mark the motor and sensory strips, so the landmarks both divide the brain and locate its functions. Dividers and function-finders. This idea of landmarks that divide and locate frames the topic.

- A few major grooves divide the cerebrum into hemispheres and lobes.
- The longitudinal fissure separates the hemispheres, and other grooves separate the lobes.
- Two key gyri, just in front of and behind the central sulcus, carry motor and sensory functions.
- The landmarks both divide the brain and locate its functions.

---

## Our Master Analogy: The Main Roads and Districts of a City Map

To keep the landmarks concrete, picture a city map where a few major roads divide the city into districts, and two well-known streets mark where key institutions sit. The cerebral landmarks are these main roads and marker streets, dividing the brain and locating its key regions. The image keeps dividing and locating in view.

- The main road splitting the city corresponds to the longitudinal fissure separating the hemispheres.
- The other major roads correspond to the sulci separating the lobes.
- The marker streets for key institutions correspond to the precentral and postcentral gyri.
- The analogy breaks down in that city roads are built, whereas the cerebral grooves are folds that form as the brain develops.

---

## The Fissures That Separate Major Regions

Begin with the deep grooves that divide the brain into major parts. These are the longitudinal and transverse fissures.

- The longitudinal fissure is the deep midline groove separating the two cerebral hemispheres.
- The transverse fissure separates the cerebrum above from the cerebellum below.
- These deep fissures divide the brain into its largest regions.
- They are the most prominent grooves on the brain's surface.

---

## The Sulci That Separate Lobes

Next the grooves that separate lobes within a hemisphere. These are the central and lateral sulci.

- The central sulcus runs across the top of each hemisphere, separating the frontal lobe from the parietal lobe.
- The lateral sulcus, or lateral fissure, runs along the side, separating the temporal lobe below from the frontal and parietal lobes above.
- These sulci are the key boundaries between the lobes.
- They are landmarks for locating the functional regions around them.

---

## The Motor and Sensory Gyri

Finally the two gyri that carry motor and sensory functions, on either side of the central sulcus. These are the precentral and postcentral gyri.

- The precentral gyrus lies just in front of the central sulcus, in the frontal lobe.
- It is the primary motor strip, controlling voluntary movement.
- The postcentral gyrus lies just behind the central sulcus, in the parietal lobe.
- It is the primary sensory strip, receiving body sensation.

---

## Why These Landmarks Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The landmarks locate the motor and sensory regions central to what you assess. Each point below connects a landmark to practice.

- The precentral gyrus is the motor strip, so its injury produces the weakness you rehabilitate.
- The postcentral gyrus is the sensory strip, so its injury affects the sensation you assess.
- The central sulcus locates the boundary between the motor and sensory strips on imaging.
- Locating a lesion relative to these landmarks helps you predict the resulting deficit.
- Understanding the landmarks grounds your interpretation of brain imaging and injury.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the map, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to the main roads and districts of a city map.

- It is a mistake to swap the precentral and postcentral gyri, since one is motor and the other sensory.
- It is a mistake to confuse the longitudinal fissure with the central sulcus, since they divide different things.
- It is a mistake to place the motor strip behind the central sulcus, since it lies in front.
- It is a mistake to overlook the transverse fissure separating the cerebrum from the cerebellum.

---

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Naming each landmark and what it marks aloud is the strongest test.

- Name the major cerebral landmarks from memory.
- Describe what each landmark separates or marks.
- Locate the motor and sensory strips relative to the central sulcus.
- State which fissure separates the hemispheres.
- Relate one landmark to a physiotherapy-relevant deficit.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have identified the cerebrum's key landmarks. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The longitudinal fissure separates the hemispheres, and the transverse fissure separates the cerebrum from the cerebellum.
- The central sulcus separates the frontal and parietal lobes, and the lateral sulcus separates the temporal lobe.
- The precentral gyrus, in front of the central sulcus, is the primary motor strip.
- The postcentral gyrus, behind the central sulcus, is the primary sensory strip.
- The landmarks both divide the brain and locate its motor and sensory functions.
