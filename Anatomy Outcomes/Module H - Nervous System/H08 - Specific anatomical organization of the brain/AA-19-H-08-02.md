## Identify and describe the 5 secondary brain vesicles formed from the neural tube and name the parts of the adult brain arising from each

# The Five Secondary Brain Vesicles

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Identify and describe the 5 secondary brain vesicles formed from the neural tube and name the parts of the adult brain arising from each.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The three primary brain vesicles subdivide into five secondary vesicles, and these map directly onto the major regions of the adult brain. Learning them completes the developmental framework and connects it to adult anatomy. Today we identify and describe the five secondary brain vesicles and the adult parts arising from each.

- By the end of this session you will be able to name the five secondary brain vesicles.
- You will be able to relate each to its primary vesicle.
- You will be able to name the adult brain parts arising from each.
- You will be able to connect the vesicles to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Three Swellings Become Five, Then the Adult Brain

The organising idea is that the forebrain and hindbrain each divide in two while the midbrain stays single, giving five secondary vesicles that become the recognisable regions of the adult brain. Three become five become the adult brain. This idea of subdivision into the adult plan frames the topic.

- The three primary vesicles subdivide into five secondary vesicles.
- The forebrain divides into two, and the hindbrain divides into two.
- The midbrain remains a single vesicle.
- Each secondary vesicle gives rise to specific adult brain regions.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Company Splitting Into Divisions

To keep the vesicles concrete, picture a company with three departments where two of them each split into two divisions while one stays whole, giving five divisions that become the final organisational structure. The secondary vesicles are these five divisions, and the image keeps the subdivision into the adult plan in view.

- The department that splits into two forward divisions corresponds to the forebrain dividing.
- The department that stays whole corresponds to the midbrain remaining single.
- The department that splits into two rear divisions corresponds to the hindbrain dividing.
- The analogy breaks down in that company divisions are drawn on paper, whereas the vesicles grow and differentiate into complex tissue.

---

## The Secondary Vesicles From the Forebrain

Begin with the two forebrain derivatives, which become the cerebrum and the diencephalon. These are the telencephalon and diencephalon.

- The telencephalon is the more anterior forebrain vesicle and gives rise to the cerebrum, the cerebral hemispheres.
- It becomes the largest part of the adult brain.
- The diencephalon is the more posterior forebrain vesicle and gives rise to the thalamus, hypothalamus, and epithalamus.
- These structures lie deep in the centre of the adult brain.

---

## The Secondary Vesicle From the Midbrain

Next the single midbrain derivative, which stays undivided. This is the mesencephalon.

- The mesencephalon is the undivided midbrain vesicle.
- It gives rise to the midbrain of the adult brainstem.
- It connects the forebrain above to the hindbrain below.
- It is the only primary vesicle that does not subdivide.

---

## The Secondary Vesicles From the Hindbrain

Finally the two hindbrain derivatives, which become the pons, cerebellum, and medulla. These are the metencephalon and myelencephalon.

- The metencephalon is the more anterior hindbrain vesicle and gives rise to the pons and the cerebellum.
- The pons and cerebellum lie at the back of the adult brainstem region.
- The myelencephalon is the more posterior hindbrain vesicle and gives rise to the medulla oblongata.
- The medulla continues into the spinal cord.

---

## Why the Secondary Vesicles Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The vesicles map development onto the adult brain regions you reason about. Each point below connects them to practice.

- The telencephalon becomes the cerebrum, central to voluntary movement and cognition you assess.
- The diencephalon becomes the thalamus and hypothalamus, central to sensation and regulation.
- The hindbrain derivatives become the cerebellum and brainstem, central to coordination and vital functions.
- Understanding the developmental map clarifies the organisation of the adult brain.
- The topic grounds your understanding of adult brain regions and their functions.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture of the vesicles, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to a company splitting into divisions.

- It is a mistake to have the midbrain subdivide, since it stays single.
- It is a mistake to confuse the telencephalon and diencephalon, since one becomes the cerebrum and the other the thalamus and hypothalamus.
- It is a mistake to place the cerebellum outside the hindbrain derivatives, since it arises from the metencephalon.
- It is a mistake to overlook that the myelencephalon becomes the medulla.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Naming each vesicle and its adult derivatives aloud is the strongest test.

- Name the five secondary brain vesicles from memory.
- Relate each to its primary vesicle.
- Name the adult brain parts arising from each.
- Explain which primary vesicle does not subdivide.
- Relate one vesicle to an adult brain region relevant to practice.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have described the five secondary vesicles and their adult derivatives. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The forebrain divides into the telencephalon, becoming the cerebrum, and the diencephalon, becoming the thalamus, hypothalamus, and epithalamus.
- The midbrain remains the mesencephalon, becoming the midbrain.
- The hindbrain divides into the metencephalon, becoming the pons and cerebellum, and the myelencephalon, becoming the medulla.
- The five vesicles map onto the major regions of the adult brain.
- The developmental map underlies the organisation of the adult brain.
