# The Cranial Nerves by Name and Number

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: List and identify the cranial nerves by name and number.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

Twelve pairs of cranial nerves emerge from the brain to serve the head, neck, and beyond, and knowing them by name and number is the foundation for assessing them. Learning them in order gives you a checklist for cranial nerve examination. Today we list and identify the cranial nerves by name and number.

- By the end of this session you will be able to list the twelve cranial nerves in order.
- You will be able to match each name to its number.
- You will be able to state where the cranial nerves emerge.
- You will be able to connect the cranial nerves to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Twelve Numbered Pairs Emerging From the Brain

The organising idea is that there are twelve pairs of cranial nerves, numbered in order from front to back as they emerge from the brain, each with a name reflecting its function or distribution. Twelve numbered pairs in sequence. This idea of an ordered numbered set frames the topic.

- There are twelve pairs of cranial nerves.
- They are numbered one to twelve from front to back.
- Each has a name reflecting its function or distribution.
- Most emerge from the brainstem, with the first two from the forebrain.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Numbered List of Named Departments

To keep the nerves concrete, picture a numbered directory of twelve named departments, each entry pairing a number with a name and a role. The cranial nerves are this numbered, named directory, and the image keeps the ordered pairing of number and name in view.

- Each numbered entry corresponds to a cranial nerve's number.
- Each department name corresponds to the nerve's name.
- The ordered directory corresponds to the front-to-back numbering.
- The analogy breaks down in that a directory is arbitrary, whereas the cranial nerve numbering follows their real order of emergence.

---

## Cranial Nerves One to Four

Begin with the first four nerves, serving smell, vision, and eye movement. These emerge near the front.

- Cranial nerve one is the olfactory nerve, for smell.
- Cranial nerve two is the optic nerve, for vision.
- Cranial nerve three is the oculomotor nerve, for most eye movements.
- Cranial nerve four is the trochlear nerve, for one of the eye muscles.

---

## Cranial Nerves Five to Eight

Next nerves five to eight, serving the face, more eye movement, and hearing. These emerge from the mid-brainstem.

- Cranial nerve five is the trigeminal nerve, for facial sensation and chewing.
- Cranial nerve six is the abducens nerve, for one of the eye muscles.
- Cranial nerve seven is the facial nerve, for facial expression and taste.
- Cranial nerve eight is the vestibulocochlear nerve, for hearing and balance.

---

## Cranial Nerves Nine to Twelve

Finally nerves nine to twelve, serving the throat, viscera, and neck and tongue. These emerge from the lower brainstem.

- Cranial nerve nine is the glossopharyngeal nerve, for the throat and taste.
- Cranial nerve ten is the vagus nerve, for the throat and many internal organs.
- Cranial nerve eleven is the accessory nerve, for some neck muscles.
- Cranial nerve twelve is the hypoglossal nerve, for the tongue.

---

## Why the Cranial Nerves Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

Knowing the cranial nerves underlies cranial nerve assessment relevant to many conditions. Each point below connects them to practice.

- Cranial nerve assessment is part of the neurological examination you perform, especially after brain injury.
- The vestibulocochlear nerve is central to balance you assess and rehabilitate.
- The facial nerve is affected in conditions such as facial palsy you may treat.
- The vagus nerve's wide distribution links the cranial nerves to autonomic function.
- Knowing the nerves by name and number gives you a checklist for examination.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors weaken cranial nerve knowledge, and naming them keeps it reliable. When unsure, return to the numbered directory.

- It is a mistake to muddle the numbering, since the nerves are numbered in a fixed order.
- It is a mistake to think all cranial nerves emerge from the brainstem, since the first two arise from the forebrain.
- It is a mistake to confuse similar names such as the oculomotor and trochlear nerves.
- It is a mistake to overlook that the vagus nerve extends well beyond the head and neck.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Listing the nerves in order aloud is the strongest test.

- List the twelve cranial nerves in order from memory.
- Match each name to its number.
- State where the cranial nerves emerge.
- Name the nerves for hearing and for facial expression.
- Relate one cranial nerve to a physiotherapy-relevant condition.

## Key Takeaways

We have listed the twelve cranial nerves. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- There are twelve pairs of cranial nerves, numbered from front to back.
- Nerves one to four are the olfactory, optic, oculomotor, and trochlear.
- Nerves five to eight are the trigeminal, abducens, facial, and vestibulocochlear.
- Nerves nine to twelve are the glossopharyngeal, vagus, accessory, and hypoglossal.
