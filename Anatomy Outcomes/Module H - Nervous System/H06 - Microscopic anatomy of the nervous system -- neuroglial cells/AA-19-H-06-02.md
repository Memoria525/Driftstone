## Define myelination and describe its function, including comparing and contrasting how myelination occurs in the CNS and PNS

# Myelination in the Central and Peripheral Nervous Systems

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Define myelination and describe its function, including comparing and contrasting how myelination occurs in the CNS and PNS.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

Myelination is the wrapping of axons in a fatty insulating sheath that dramatically speeds nerve conduction, and it is achieved by different cells in the central and peripheral nervous systems. Understanding it explains why demyelinating diseases are so disabling and why nerve conduction can be fast. Today we define myelination and compare how it occurs in the central and peripheral nervous systems.

- By the end of this session you will be able to define myelination.
- You will be able to describe its function in speeding conduction.
- You will be able to contrast how it occurs in the central and peripheral nervous systems.
- You will be able to connect myelination to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Insulation That Speeds Signal Conduction

The organising idea is that myelination wraps an axon in a fatty insulating sheath with gaps, allowing the nerve signal to jump between the gaps and travel far faster than it could along a bare axon. Insulation with gaps for speed. This idea of insulation for speed frames the topic.

- Myelination is the wrapping of an axon in a fatty insulating sheath called myelin.
- The sheath is interrupted by regular gaps along the axon.
- The signal jumps between the gaps, greatly speeding its conduction.
- Different glial cells produce myelin in the central and peripheral nervous systems.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Insulated Cable With Repeater Points

To keep myelination concrete, picture an insulated signal cable with regular exposed points where the signal is refreshed and leaps to the next point, travelling far faster than along a bare uninsulated wire. Myelin is the insulation and the gaps are the leap points. The image keeps insulation and jumping conduction together.

- The insulation along the cable corresponds to the myelin sheath.
- The regular exposed points corresponds to the gaps in the sheath.
- The signal leaping between points corresponds to the signal jumping between the gaps.
- The analogy breaks down in that cable insulation is manufactured, whereas myelin is formed by living glial cells wrapping the axon.

---

## Defining Myelination and Its Function

Begin with the definition and function, since these are shared everywhere. Myelin speeds conduction.

- Myelination is the process by which glial cells wrap an axon in layers of their membrane, forming myelin.
- The myelin sheath insulates the axon electrically.
- Gaps in the sheath allow the signal to jump from gap to gap rather than crawling along the whole axon.
- This jumping conduction makes myelinated axons conduct far faster than unmyelinated ones.

---

## Myelination in the Central Nervous System

Next how myelination occurs centrally, by one cell type serving several axons. Oligodendrocytes do the work.

- In the central nervous system, oligodendrocytes produce myelin.
- A single oligodendrocyte extends processes that myelinate segments of several different axons.
- The myelinated axons form the white matter of the brain and spinal cord.
- Central myelin generally does not regenerate well after damage.

---

## Myelination in the Peripheral Nervous System

Finally how myelination occurs peripherally, by one cell per segment. Schwann cells do the work.

- In the peripheral nervous system, Schwann cells produce myelin.
- Each Schwann cell wraps a single segment of one axon.
- The Schwann cells also guide the regrowth of peripheral axons after injury.
- Peripheral myelin therefore supports the regeneration that central myelin does not.

---

## Why Myelination Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

Myelination underlies conduction speed, demyelinating disease, and nerve recovery. Each point below connects it to practice.

- Myelin sets conduction speed, and its loss slows or blocks signals, as in demyelinating conditions you will encounter.
- The difference between central and peripheral myelination underlies why peripheral nerves recover better.
- Schwann cell guidance explains recovery after peripheral nerve injury.
- Demyelinating disease such as multiple sclerosis produces the deficits you help rehabilitate.
- Understanding myelination grounds your reasoning about nerve conduction and recovery.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture of myelination, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to insulated cable with repeater points.

- It is a mistake to think myelin is continuous, since it has regular gaps that allow jumping conduction.
- It is a mistake to have oligodendrocytes myelinate peripheral nerves, since Schwann cells do that.
- It is a mistake to believe one Schwann cell myelinates several axons, since each myelinates one segment of one axon.
- It is a mistake to assume central and peripheral myelin recover the same way.

## Key Takeaways

We have defined myelination and compared it in the two systems. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- Myelination wraps an axon in a fatty insulating sheath with regular gaps.
- The signal jumps between the gaps, greatly speeding conduction.
- Oligodendrocytes myelinate several central axons each, forming white matter that regenerates poorly.
- Schwann cells myelinate one peripheral segment each and guide regeneration.
- Myelination underlies conduction speed, demyelinating disease, and nerve recovery.
