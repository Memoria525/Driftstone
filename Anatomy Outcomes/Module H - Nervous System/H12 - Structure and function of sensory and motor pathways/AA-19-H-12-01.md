## Describe the locations and functions of the first-, second- and third-order neurons in a sensory pathway

# The Neurons of a Sensory Pathway

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Describe the locations and functions of the first-, second- and third-order neurons in a sensory pathway.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

Sensation reaches the brain through a chain of three neurons, each covering one leg of the journey from the body to the cortex. Understanding this chain explains how sensory information is relayed and where injury interrupts it. Today we describe the locations and functions of the first-, second-, and third-order neurons in a sensory pathway.

- By the end of this session you will be able to describe the three-neuron sensory chain.
- You will be able to state the location of each neuron.
- You will be able to state the function of each neuron.
- You will be able to connect the pathway to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: A Relay of Three Neurons From Body to Cortex

The organising idea is that a sensory pathway is a relay of three neurons in series, the first bringing information from the body to the cord or brainstem, the second carrying it up to the thalamus, and the third carrying it to the cortex. Three legs of one relay. This idea of a three-neuron relay frames the topic.

- A sensory pathway typically uses three neurons in series.
- The first-order neuron brings information from the receptor to the central nervous system.
- The second-order neuron carries it up to the thalamus.
- The third-order neuron carries it from the thalamus to the cortex.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Three-Leg Relay Race

To keep the pathway concrete, picture a relay race with three runners, each carrying the baton over one leg of the course before handing it to the next. The three sensory neurons are these runners, passing the sensory signal from the body to the cortex. The image keeps the three legs of the relay in view.

- The first runner from the start corresponds to the first-order neuron from the receptor.
- The second runner corresponds to the second-order neuron to the thalamus.
- The third runner to the finish corresponds to the third-order neuron to the cortex.
- The analogy breaks down in that runners are identical, whereas each neuron has a distinct location and role.

---

## The First-Order Neuron

Begin with the first neuron, which brings sensation in. Its cell body sits in the ganglion.

- The first-order neuron carries sensory information from the receptor to the central nervous system.
- Its cell body lies in the posterior root ganglion or a cranial nerve ganglion.
- Its axon enters the spinal cord or brainstem.
- It delivers the sensory signal to the second-order neuron.

---

## The Second-Order Neuron

Next the second neuron, which carries sensation up. It usually crosses to the other side.

- The second-order neuron carries information from the cord or brainstem up to the thalamus.
- Its cell body lies in the cord or brainstem.
- Its axon usually crosses to the opposite side on the way up.
- It delivers the signal to the third-order neuron in the thalamus.

---

## The Third-Order Neuron

Finally the third neuron, which carries sensation to the cortex. It completes the relay.

- The third-order neuron carries information from the thalamus to the cerebral cortex.
- Its cell body lies in the thalamus.
- Its axon projects to the primary somatosensory cortex.
- It delivers the signal to the cortex, where the sensation reaches awareness.

---

## Why the Sensory Pathway Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The sensory pathway underlies how sensation reaches the brain and where injury interrupts it. Each point below connects the pathway to practice.

- Because the second-order neuron crosses, a lesion above the crossing affects sensation on the opposite side of the body.
- Damage at any point in the chain interrupts sensation, and the level of loss helps localise the lesion.
- The pathway explains why a cord or brain lesion affects sensation in a predictable pattern.
- Understanding the relay helps you interpret sensory loss in your patients.
- Understanding the pathway grounds your reasoning about sensory deficits.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the pathway, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to the three-leg relay race.

- It is a mistake to think sensation reaches the cortex through a single neuron, since it uses a three-neuron relay.
- It is a mistake to place the first-order cell body within the cord, since it lies in the posterior root ganglion.
- It is a mistake to overlook that the second-order neuron usually crosses to the opposite side.
- It is a mistake to forget that the third-order neuron relays from the thalamus to the cortex.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Tracing the relay aloud is the strongest test.

- Describe the three-neuron sensory chain from memory.
- State the location of each neuron.
- State the function of each neuron.
- Explain why a lesion above the crossing affects the opposite side.
- Relate the pathway to a physiotherapy-relevant deficit.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have described the sensory relay. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- A sensory pathway uses three neurons in series.
- The first-order neuron, with its cell body in the posterior root ganglion, brings sensation to the cord or brainstem.
- The second-order neuron carries it up to the thalamus, usually crossing to the opposite side.
- The third-order neuron carries it from the thalamus to the cortex.
- The crossing means a lesion above it affects sensation on the opposite side of the body.
