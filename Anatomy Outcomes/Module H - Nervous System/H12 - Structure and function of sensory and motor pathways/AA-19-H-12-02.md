## Describe the locations and functions of the upper and lower motor neurons in a motor pathway

# The Upper and Lower Motor Neurons

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Describe the locations and functions of the upper and lower motor neurons in a motor pathway.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

Voluntary movement is delivered by a chain of two motor neurons, an upper one from the brain and a lower one to the muscle, and damage to each produces a distinct pattern of signs. Understanding them is essential for interpreting weakness. Today we describe the locations and functions of the upper and lower motor neurons in a motor pathway.

- By the end of this session you will be able to describe the two-neuron motor chain.
- You will be able to state the location and function of each neuron.
- You will be able to contrast the signs of upper and lower motor neuron damage.
- You will be able to connect the pathway to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: A Two-Neuron Chain From Cortex to Muscle

The organising idea is that voluntary movement travels through two motor neurons in series, an upper motor neuron from the cortex to the cord and a lower motor neuron from the cord to the muscle, and damage to each produces contrasting signs. Two neurons, two patterns of damage. This idea of a two-neuron motor chain frames the topic.

- Voluntary movement uses two motor neurons in series.
- The upper motor neuron runs from the cortex to the spinal cord.
- The lower motor neuron runs from the spinal cord to the muscle.
- Damage to each produces a distinct pattern of signs.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Head-Office Manager and a Local Foreman

To keep the pathway concrete, picture a command chain with a head-office manager who issues instructions down to a local foreman, who then directs the workers. The upper motor neuron is the manager and the lower motor neuron is the foreman directing the muscle. The image keeps the two-level command in view.

- The head-office manager corresponds to the upper motor neuron from the cortex.
- The local foreman corresponds to the lower motor neuron to the muscle.
- The workers corresponds to the muscle fibres.
- The analogy breaks down in that a manager and foreman are people, whereas the neurons are single cells whose damage produces specific signs.

---

## The Upper Motor Neuron

Begin with the upper motor neuron, which runs from the brain to the cord. It commands the lower neuron.

- The upper motor neuron has its cell body in the cerebral cortex.
- Its axon descends through the brain and spinal cord, largely crossing to the opposite side.
- It does not reach the muscle directly but connects to the lower motor neuron.
- It carries the command for voluntary movement from the cortex toward the cord.

---

## The Lower Motor Neuron

Next the lower motor neuron, which runs from the cord to the muscle. It directly drives the muscle.

- The lower motor neuron has its cell body in the anterior horn of the spinal cord or in a brainstem nucleus.
- Its axon leaves the cord through the anterior root and travels in a peripheral nerve to the muscle.
- It directly stimulates the muscle to contract.
- It is the final common pathway from the nervous system to the muscle.

---

## Contrasting Upper and Lower Motor Neuron Damage

Finally the contrasting signs of damage, which are central to diagnosis. The two produce opposite patterns.

- Upper motor neuron damage typically causes weakness with increased muscle tone and exaggerated reflexes.
- Lower motor neuron damage typically causes weakness with decreased muscle tone, reduced reflexes, and muscle wasting.
- The two patterns help localise where in the motor pathway a lesion lies.
- Recognising the pattern is a core skill in interpreting weakness.

---

## Why This Pathway Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The motor pathway underlies the interpretation of weakness central to rehabilitation. Each point below connects it to practice.

- Distinguishing upper from lower motor neuron signs helps you localise a lesion and understand the weakness you rehabilitate.
- Upper motor neuron lesions, as in stroke and spinal cord injury, produce increased tone you manage.
- Lower motor neuron lesions, as in peripheral nerve injury, produce wasting and reduced tone you address.
- The crossing of the upper motor neuron explains why a brain lesion affects the opposite side.
- Understanding the pathway grounds your interpretation of motor deficits.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the pathway, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to the manager and foreman.

- It is a mistake to think the upper motor neuron reaches the muscle directly, since it connects to the lower motor neuron.
- It is a mistake to swap the signs, since upper motor neuron damage increases tone and lower motor neuron damage decreases it.
- It is a mistake to place the lower motor neuron cell body in the cortex, since it lies in the anterior horn or brainstem.
- It is a mistake to overlook that the upper motor neuron largely crosses to the opposite side.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Contrasting the two neurons aloud is the strongest test.

- Describe the two-neuron motor chain from memory.
- State the location and function of each neuron.
- Contrast the signs of upper and lower motor neuron damage.
- Explain why a brain lesion affects the opposite side of the body.
- Relate the pathway to a physiotherapy-relevant deficit.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have described the motor pathway. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- Voluntary movement uses an upper and a lower motor neuron in series.
- The upper motor neuron runs from the cortex to the cord, largely crossing to the opposite side.
- The lower motor neuron runs from the anterior horn to the muscle as the final common pathway.
- Upper motor neuron damage increases tone and reflexes, while lower motor neuron damage decreases them and causes wasting.
- Distinguishing the two patterns is central to interpreting weakness.
