# The Autonomic and Somatic Nervous Systems Compared

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Compare and contrast the autonomic nervous system (ANS) to the somatic nervous system (SNS) with respect to site of origination, number of neurons involved in the pathway, effectors, and neurotransmitters.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The motor side of the nervous system has two divisions, the somatic for voluntary movement and the autonomic for automatic control, and they differ in structure at every step. Comparing them clarifies how the body controls muscle versus organs. Today we compare and contrast the autonomic and somatic nervous systems.

- By the end of this session you will be able to contrast the origin of each system.
- You will be able to contrast the number of neurons in each pathway.
- You will be able to contrast their effectors and neurotransmitters.
- You will be able to connect the comparison to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Voluntary Muscle Control Versus Automatic Organ Control

The organising idea is that the somatic system controls skeletal muscle with a single excitatory neuron, while the autonomic system controls organs with a two-neuron chain that can excite or inhibit, so the two differ at every step. Simple voluntary versus two-step automatic. This idea of contrasting control systems frames the comparison.

- The somatic system controls skeletal muscle voluntarily.
- The autonomic system controls organs and glands automatically.
- They differ in origin, neuron number, effectors, and neurotransmitters.
- The differences all follow from their different jobs.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Direct Switch Versus a Relayed Control Circuit

To keep the comparison concrete, picture two control systems: a direct switch wired straight to a light, and a relayed circuit that passes through an intermediate relay before reaching the appliance, able to turn it up or down. The somatic system is the direct switch and the autonomic is the relayed circuit, and the image keeps the structural contrast in view.

- The direct switch to the light corresponds to the somatic single neuron to the muscle.
- The relayed circuit through an intermediate corresponds to the autonomic two-neuron chain.
- Turning the appliance up or down corresponds to the autonomic ability to excite or inhibit.
- The analogy breaks down in that switches are wired to a plan, whereas the neurons are living cells using chemical messengers.

---

## The Site of Origin and Number of Neurons

Begin with origin and neuron number, since these differ structurally. One uses a single neuron, the other two.

- The somatic system's neurons originate in the brainstem and spinal cord and run directly to the muscle.
- It uses a single neuron from the central nervous system to the effector.
- The autonomic system's neurons also originate in the central nervous system but relay in a ganglion.
- It uses a two-neuron chain, a preganglionic and a postganglionic neuron.

---

## The Effectors

Next the effectors each controls, since these differ. One controls skeletal muscle, the other organs.

- The somatic system's effector is skeletal muscle.
- Its effect is always to excite the muscle, producing contraction.
- The autonomic system's effectors are smooth muscle, cardiac muscle, and glands.
- Its effect can be either to excite or to inhibit the effector.

---

## The Neurotransmitters

Finally the neurotransmitters used, since these differ between the systems. The chemicals differ at the effector.

- The somatic system uses acetylcholine at the muscle, always exciting it.
- The autonomic system uses acetylcholine at its ganglia.
- At the effector, the autonomic system uses acetylcholine or noradrenaline, depending on the division.
- The neurotransmitter and the receptor determine whether the autonomic effect is excitation or inhibition.

---

## Why This Comparison Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The comparison clarifies what you can train and how the body's automatic responses affect exercise. Each point below connects the comparison to practice.

- The somatic system produces the voluntary movement you assess and train.
- The autonomic system controls the heart and airways that respond during exercise.
- Understanding that organ control is automatic clarifies why you cannot train it directly.
- Autonomic responses such as heart rate changes affect exercise tolerance you monitor.
- Understanding the comparison grounds your reasoning about voluntary and automatic control.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors blur the two systems, and naming them keeps them distinct. When unsure, return to the direct switch versus the relayed circuit.

- It is a mistake to give the somatic system a two-neuron chain, since it uses a single neuron.
- It is a mistake to think the somatic system can inhibit muscle, since it only excites it.
- It is a mistake to believe the autonomic system uses only one neurotransmitter, since it uses acetylcholine and noradrenaline.
- It is a mistake to treat the two systems as controlling the same effectors.

- Contrast the origin of the two systems from memory.

## Key Takeaways

We have compared the somatic and autonomic systems. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The somatic system controls skeletal muscle with a single excitatory neuron using acetylcholine.
- The autonomic system controls organs and glands with a two-neuron chain that can excite or inhibit.
- The autonomic system relays in a ganglion and uses acetylcholine or noradrenaline at its effectors.
- The somatic system produces voluntary movement, while the autonomic runs automatically.
- The comparison clarifies what movement you can train and how automatic responses affect exercise.
