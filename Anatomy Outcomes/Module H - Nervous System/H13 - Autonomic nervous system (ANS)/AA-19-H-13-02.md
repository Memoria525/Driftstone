# The Sympathetic and Parasympathetic Divisions Compared

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Compare and contrast the two main divisions of the ANS (sympathetic and parasympathetic) with respect to their major functions, neurotransmitters, origin in the CNS, the location of their preganglionic and postganglionic cell bodies, and the length of the preganglionic versus postganglionic axons.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The autonomic nervous system has two divisions that oppose each other, the sympathetic for fight-or-flight and the parasympathetic for rest-and-digest, differing in function, chemistry, and anatomy. Understanding them explains the body's automatic responses during exercise and rest. Today we compare and contrast the sympathetic and parasympathetic divisions.

- By the end of this session you will be able to contrast the functions of the two divisions.
- You will be able to contrast their origins and ganglion locations.
- You will be able to contrast their neuron lengths and neurotransmitters.
- You will be able to connect the comparison to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Fight-or-Flight Versus Rest-and-Digest

The organising idea is that the sympathetic division prepares the body for exertion in fight-or-flight while the parasympathetic division conserves energy in rest-and-digest, and their anatomy and chemistry differ to suit these opposite roles. Two opposing automatic states. This idea of opposing divisions frames the comparison.

- The sympathetic division prepares the body for exertion, the fight-or-flight response.
- The parasympathetic division conserves energy, the rest-and-digest response.
- The two divisions differ in origin, ganglion location, neuron length, and neurotransmitters.
- The differences suit their opposite functions.

---

## Our Master Analogy: An Accelerator and a Brake

To keep the divisions concrete, picture a vehicle with an accelerator that speeds it up for action and a brake that slows it for rest. The sympathetic division is the accelerator and the parasympathetic is the brake, working in opposition to control the body's pace. The image keeps the opposing roles in view.

- The accelerator corresponds to the sympathetic fight-or-flight division.
- The brake corresponds to the parasympathetic rest-and-digest division.
- Their opposition corresponds to the balance between the two divisions.
- The analogy breaks down in that a vehicle uses one at a time, whereas the two divisions are usually both active, with one dominating.

---

## The Functions and Origins

Begin with function and origin, since these define the divisions. They arise from different regions.

- The sympathetic division prepares for exertion, raising heart rate and diverting blood to muscle.
- It originates from the thoracic and lumbar regions of the spinal cord.
- The parasympathetic division promotes rest and digestion, slowing the heart and stimulating the gut.
- It originates from the brainstem and the sacral region of the spinal cord.

---

## The Ganglion Locations and Neuron Lengths

Next the ganglion locations and neuron lengths, which follow from the origins. The two differ markedly.

- The sympathetic division has its ganglia near the spinal cord, so its preganglionic axons are short and its postganglionic axons long.
- Its ganglia lie close to the cord in the sympathetic trunk or nearby.
- The parasympathetic division has its ganglia near or within the target organs, so its preganglionic axons are long and its postganglionic axons short.
- Its ganglia lie close to the effectors they serve.

---

## The Neurotransmitters

Finally the neurotransmitters, which differ at the effector. This is a key contrast.

- Both divisions use acetylcholine at their ganglia.
- The sympathetic division mostly uses noradrenaline at its effectors.
- The parasympathetic division uses acetylcholine at its effectors.
- The neurotransmitter at the effector distinguishes the two divisions chemically.

---

## Why This Comparison Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The two divisions underlie the body's automatic responses during exercise and rest. Each point below connects the comparison to practice.

- The sympathetic division raises heart rate and diverts blood to muscle during exercise, which you monitor.
- The parasympathetic division promotes the recovery state after exercise.
- The balance between the divisions affects exercise tolerance and recovery.
- Autonomic responses are relevant to how patients respond to activity and rest.
- Understanding the comparison grounds your reasoning about autonomic responses to exercise.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors blur the divisions, and naming them keeps them distinct. When unsure, return to the accelerator and brake.

- It is a mistake to swap the neuron lengths, since the sympathetic has short preganglionic and the parasympathetic long preganglionic axons.
- It is a mistake to give both divisions the same effector neurotransmitter, since the sympathetic mostly uses noradrenaline and the parasympathetic acetylcholine.
- It is a mistake to think only one division is active at a time, since both are usually active.
- It is a mistake to confuse the origins, since the sympathetic is thoracolumbar and the parasympathetic craniosacral.

## Key Takeaways

We have compared the two autonomic divisions. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The sympathetic division drives fight-or-flight and the parasympathetic drives rest-and-digest.
- The sympathetic arises from the thoracolumbar cord, with ganglia near the cord, short preganglionic and long postganglionic axons.
- The parasympathetic arises from the brainstem and sacral cord, with ganglia near the organs, long preganglionic and short postganglionic axons.
- Both use acetylcholine at their ganglia, but the sympathetic mostly uses noradrenaline at effectors and the parasympathetic acetylcholine.
- The divisions underlie the body's automatic responses during exercise and rest.
