# The Effects of Autonomic Innervation on Effectors

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Compare and contrast the effects (or lack thereof) of sympathetic and parasympathetic innervation on various effectors (heart, iris of eye, airways of lungs, sweat glands, gastrointestinal tract).

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The two autonomic divisions usually produce opposite effects on the same organ, so that one speeds while the other slows, but some organs receive only one division. Understanding these effects explains the body's automatic adjustments during exercise and rest. Today we compare and contrast the effects of sympathetic and parasympathetic innervation on various effectors.

- By the end of this session you will be able to state the sympathetic and parasympathetic effects on key organs.
- You will be able to explain the usual opposition of the two divisions.
- You will be able to identify organs served by only one division.
- You will be able to connect these effects to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Usually Opposing Effects, Sometimes Only One Division

The organising idea is that the two divisions usually produce opposite effects on an organ, tuning it up or down, but some effectors receive only one division and so are controlled in one direction. Opposition on most organs, single control on some. This idea of opposing yet sometimes solo control frames the topic.

- The two divisions usually produce opposite effects on the same organ.
- The sympathetic prepares for exertion and the parasympathetic for rest.
- Some effectors, such as most blood vessels and sweat glands, receive only sympathetic supply.
- The balance and pattern of innervation tune each organ's activity.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Dial Turned Up or Down, or With Only One Knob

To keep the effects concrete, picture control dials on the organs, most with two knobs turning them up or down, but some with only a single knob that turns them in one direction. The autonomic divisions are these knobs, opposing on most organs but solo on some. The image keeps opposition and single control in view.

- The two knobs turning an organ up or down correspond to the opposing sympathetic and parasympathetic effects.
- A single knob on some organs corresponds to effectors served by only one division.
- Turning the dial up corresponds to the sympathetic activating an organ for exertion.
- The analogy breaks down in that dials are set by hand, whereas the divisions act automatically and continuously.

---

## The Effects on the Heart and Airways

Begin with the heart and airways, key to exercise. The divisions oppose one another here.

- The sympathetic division increases heart rate and the force of the heartbeat, while the parasympathetic slows the heart.
- During exercise, the sympathetic dominates to raise the heart's output.
- The sympathetic division widens the airways of the lungs, while the parasympathetic narrows them.
- During exercise, the sympathetic widens the airways to increase airflow.

---

## The Effects on the Eye and Gastrointestinal Tract

Next the eye and gut, which also show opposing control. The divisions again oppose one another.

- The sympathetic division widens the pupil of the eye, while the parasympathetic narrows it.
- Widening the pupil is part of the fight-or-flight response.
- The sympathetic division slows the activity of the gastrointestinal tract, while the parasympathetic stimulates it.
- Rest-and-digest promotes digestion, while exertion diverts resources away from it.

---

## The Effectors Served by Only One Division

Finally the effectors receiving only one division, chiefly sympathetic. These are controlled in one direction.

- Most blood vessels receive only sympathetic supply, which adjusts their diameter to control blood flow.
- Sweat glands receive only sympathetic supply, which produces sweating.
- These effectors lack a parasympathetic supply, so they are controlled in one direction.
- Their activity is tuned by increasing or decreasing the sympathetic input.

---

## Why These Effects Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The autonomic effects underlie the body's automatic responses to exercise you monitor. Each point below connects the effects to practice.

- The sympathetic effects raise heart rate, widen the airways, and divert blood to muscle during exercise.
- The parasympathetic effects promote the recovery state after exercise.
- Sympathetic control of sweat glands underlies the sweating you observe during exertion.
- Understanding these effects clarifies the body's automatic adjustments to activity and rest.
- Understanding them grounds your reasoning about autonomic responses to exercise.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to dials turned up or down.

- It is a mistake to think every organ receives both divisions, since some receive only sympathetic supply.
- It is a mistake to swap the effects, since the sympathetic speeds the heart and the parasympathetic slows it.
- It is a mistake to believe the sympathetic stimulates digestion, since it slows the gut.
- It is a mistake to overlook that sweat glands and most blood vessels are sympathetic only.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Contrasting the effects aloud is the strongest test.

- State the sympathetic and parasympathetic effects on the heart and airways from memory.
- State their effects on the eye and gastrointestinal tract.
- Identify organs served by only one division.
- Explain the usual opposition of the two divisions.
- Relate these effects to the body's response to exercise.

## Key Takeaways

We have compared the autonomic effects on the organs. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The two divisions usually produce opposite effects on an organ.
- The sympathetic speeds the heart, widens the airways, widens the pupil, and slows the gut.
- The parasympathetic slows the heart, narrows the airways, narrows the pupil, and stimulates the gut.
- Most blood vessels and sweat glands receive only sympathetic supply.
- These effects underlie the body's automatic adjustments during exercise and rest.
