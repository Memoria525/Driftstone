## Where We Are Going Today

The brachial plexus supplies the entire upper limb, and its named nerves are the ones you assess in every arm and hand problem. Understanding it explains the patterns of weakness and sensory loss in upper limb nerve injuries. Today we describe the spinal nerves that form the brachial plexus, its distributions, and its major nerves.

## The Big Idea: The Plexus That Supplies the Whole Upper Limb

The organising idea is that the brachial plexus gathers the lower cervical and first thoracic nerves and distributes them as the named nerves that supply the whole upper limb. One plexus for the whole arm. This idea of a limb-supplying plexus frames the topic.

- The brachial plexus is formed from the lower cervical and first thoracic spinal nerves.
- It supplies the entire upper limb, from shoulder to hand.
- Its fibres regroup into several major named nerves.
- These nerves carry both motor and sensory fibres to the limb.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Regional Distribution Hub for One Territory

To keep the plexus concrete, picture a regional distribution hub that gathers supplies from several sources and sends them out along a few major routes to serve one whole territory. The brachial plexus is this hub for the upper limb, sending out its major nerves, and the image keeps the whole-limb supply in view.

- The gathered sources correspond to the spinal nerves forming the plexus.
- The major routes out correspond to the major named nerves.
- The one served territory corresponds to the whole upper limb.
- The analogy breaks down in that a hub follows a distribution plan, whereas the plexus is a network of living nerve fibres.

---

## The Spinal Nerves That Form It

Begin with the nerves that form the plexus. It arises from the lower neck.

- The brachial plexus is formed by the anterior rami of the fifth to eighth cervical nerves and the first thoracic nerve.
- These are the levels C5 to T1.
- The plexus lies in the neck and shoulder region, passing toward the axilla.
- Its fibres mingle and regroup into its named nerves.

---

## The Motor and Sensory Distributions

Next its distributions, which cover the whole upper limb. It supplies movement and sensation.

- Its motor distribution supplies the muscles of the shoulder, arm, forearm, and hand.
- Its sensory distribution supplies the skin of most of the upper limb.
- It therefore controls the movement and sensation of the entire upper limb.
- Its complex regrouping allows precise distribution to the many muscles of the limb.

---

## The Major Named Nerves

Finally its major named nerves, which you assess in upper limb problems. Five are key.

- The musculocutaneous nerve supplies the flexors of the front of the arm.
- The median nerve supplies most of the forearm flexors and the thumb muscles, and sensation to part of the hand.
- The ulnar nerve supplies some forearm and many hand muscles, and sensation to part of the hand.
- The radial nerve supplies the extensors of the arm and forearm, and the axillary nerve supplies the deltoid and shoulder.

---

## Why the Brachial Plexus Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The brachial plexus underlies upper limb nerve injuries central to hand and arm rehabilitation. Each point below connects it to practice.

- Its named nerves are the ones you assess in every upper limb nerve injury.
- Injury to a specific nerve, such as the median or ulnar, produces a recognisable pattern of weakness and sensory loss.
- Brachial plexus injuries, as from trauma, produce complex patterns you must interpret.
- The plexus's supply underlies the movement and sensation you rehabilitate in the arm and hand.
- Understanding the brachial plexus grounds your reasoning about upper limb nerve injury.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to the regional distribution hub.

- It is a mistake to think the brachial plexus supplies the neck, since that is the cervical plexus.
- It is a mistake to confuse the median and ulnar nerves, since they supply different muscles and skin.
- It is a mistake to omit the first thoracic contribution, since the plexus is C5 to T1.
- It is a mistake to overlook the radial nerve's role in extension.

## Key Takeaways

We have described the brachial plexus. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The brachial plexus is formed by the anterior rami of C5 to T1.
- It supplies the entire upper limb, from shoulder to hand.
- Its major nerves are the musculocutaneous, median, ulnar, radial, and axillary nerves.
- Each nerve supplies specific muscles and skin, producing recognisable patterns of loss.
- The plexus underlies the upper limb nerve injuries central to arm and hand rehabilitation.
