# The Number of Spinal Nerve Pairs by Region

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: List the number of spinal nerve pairs and their breakdown by region (cervical, thoracic, lumbar, sacral, coccygeal).

---

## Where We Are Going Today

There are thirty-one pairs of spinal nerves, grouped by the region of the spine they emerge from. Knowing the count in each region lets you name any spinal nerve and locate the level of a problem. Today we list the number of spinal nerve pairs and their breakdown by region.

- By the end of this session you will be able to state the total number of spinal nerve pairs.
- You will be able to state the number in each region.
- You will be able to explain the naming of the nerves by region.
- You will be able to connect the count to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Thirty-One Pairs Grouped by Spinal Region

The organising idea is that there are thirty-one pairs of spinal nerves, grouped into five regions matching the regions of the spine, so that each nerve is named by its region and number. Thirty-one pairs in five regional groups. This idea of a regional count frames the topic.

- There are thirty-one pairs of spinal nerves in total.
- They are grouped by the region of the spine they emerge from.
- The five regions are cervical, thoracic, lumbar, sacral, and coccygeal.
- Each nerve is named by its region and its number within that region.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Numbered Exits Along Five Stretches of a Motorway

To keep the count concrete, picture a motorway divided into five stretches, each with a set of numbered exits, so every exit is named by its stretch and number. The spinal nerves are these numbered exits along the five regions of the spine, and the image keeps the regional numbering in view.

- The five stretches of motorway correspond to the five spinal regions.
- The numbered exits correspond to the numbered spinal nerves.
- Naming an exit by stretch and number corresponds to naming a nerve by region and number.
- The analogy breaks down in that motorway exits are evenly spaced, whereas the number of nerves differs between spinal regions.

---

## The Cervical and Thoracic Nerves

Begin with the upper two regions, the cervical and thoracic. These have the most nerves after the thoracic.

- There are eight pairs of cervical spinal nerves.
- There are more cervical nerves than cervical vertebrae, because the first emerges above the first vertebra.
- There are twelve pairs of thoracic spinal nerves.
- The thoracic region has the largest number of spinal nerve pairs.

---

## The Lumbar, Sacral, and Coccygeal Nerves

Next the lower three regions, the lumbar, sacral, and coccygeal. These serve the lower body.

- There are five pairs of lumbar spinal nerves.
- There are five pairs of sacral spinal nerves.
- There is one pair of coccygeal spinal nerves.
- These lower nerves supply the lower limbs and pelvic region.

---

## The Total and the Naming

Finally the total and how the nerves are named, since this ties the count together. The nerves are named by region and number.

- Adding the regions gives thirty-one pairs in total.
- Each nerve is named by the first letter of its region and its number, such as the fourth cervical nerve.
- The naming lets you specify any spinal nerve precisely.
- The count and naming underlie locating the level of a spinal problem.

---

## Why the Count Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

Knowing the spinal nerve count underlies locating and naming spinal levels. Each point below connects the count to practice.

- Naming a spinal nerve by region and number lets you specify the level of a problem precisely.
- The cervical nerves supply the upper limbs you rehabilitate, and the lumbar and sacral nerves the lower limbs.
- Knowing the levels helps you interpret the pattern of a root or nerve injury.
- The count underlies the dermatome and plexus maps you use in assessment.
- Understanding the count grounds your localisation of spinal problems.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the count, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to numbered exits along five stretches.

- It is a mistake to give eight thoracic nerves, since there are eight cervical and twelve thoracic.
- It is a mistake to match the cervical nerve count to the vertebra count, since there are eight nerves and seven vertebrae.
- It is a mistake to forget the single coccygeal pair.
- It is a mistake to miscount the total, which is thirty-one pairs.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Listing the regional counts aloud is the strongest test.

- State the total number of spinal nerve pairs from memory.
- State the number in each region.
- Explain why there are eight cervical nerves but seven cervical vertebrae.
- Explain how the nerves are named.
- Relate the count to locating a spinal problem.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have counted the spinal nerves by region. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- There are thirty-one pairs of spinal nerves in total.
- There are eight cervical, twelve thoracic, five lumbar, five sacral, and one coccygeal pair.
- The cervical nerves outnumber the cervical vertebrae because the first emerges above the first vertebra.
- Each nerve is named by its region and number.
- The count underlies locating and naming spinal levels in assessment.
