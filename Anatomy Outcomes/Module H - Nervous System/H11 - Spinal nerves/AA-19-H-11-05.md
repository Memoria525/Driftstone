# The Cervical Plexus

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: For the cervical plexus, list the spinal nerves that form it, describe the plexus' major motor and sensory distributions, and list the major named nerves that originate from the plexus.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The cervical plexus supplies the neck and, crucially, the diaphragm, making it central to breathing. Understanding it explains why a high neck injury can stop breathing. Today we describe the spinal nerves that form the cervical plexus, its distributions, and its major nerves.

- By the end of this session you will be able to list the spinal nerves forming the cervical plexus.
- You will be able to describe its motor and sensory distributions.
- You will be able to name its major nerves, especially the phrenic nerve.
- You will be able to connect the cervical plexus to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: The Neck Plexus That Also Drives Breathing

The organising idea is that the cervical plexus supplies the skin and muscles of the neck and, through the phrenic nerve, drives the diaphragm, making it essential to breathing. A neck plexus that also breathes. This idea of a neck-and-breathing plexus frames the topic.

- The cervical plexus is formed from the upper cervical spinal nerves.
- It supplies the skin and muscles of the neck.
- Through the phrenic nerve it supplies the diaphragm.
- It is therefore essential to breathing as well as neck function.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Local Control Box That Also Runs the Main Pump

To keep the plexus concrete, picture a local control box that manages a district's minor functions but also carries the line that runs the building's main pump. The cervical plexus manages the neck but also carries the phrenic nerve to the diaphragm, and the image keeps its dual role in view.

- The local district functions correspond to the neck skin and muscles.
- The line running the main pump corresponds to the phrenic nerve to the diaphragm.
- The single control box corresponds to the cervical plexus.
- The analogy breaks down in that a control box is wired to a plan, whereas the plexus is a network of living nerve fibres.

---

## The Spinal Nerves That Form It

Begin with the nerves that form the plexus. It arises from the upper cervical levels.

- The cervical plexus is formed by the anterior rami of the first four cervical spinal nerves.
- These are the levels C1 to C4, with a contribution to the phrenic nerve.
- The plexus lies in the neck, beside the upper cervical vertebrae.
- Its fibres mingle and regroup into its named nerves.

---

## The Motor and Sensory Distributions

Next its distributions, both to the neck and to the diaphragm. It has motor and sensory roles.

- Its motor distribution supplies several muscles of the neck.
- Its most important motor branch, the phrenic nerve, supplies the diaphragm.
- Its sensory distribution supplies the skin of the neck, upper chest, and part of the scalp and ear.
- It therefore serves both movement and sensation of the neck, and breathing.

---

## The Major Named Nerves

Finally its major named nerves, especially the phrenic nerve. This is the key nerve to know.

- The phrenic nerve is the major nerve of the cervical plexus, supplying the diaphragm.
- It arises mainly from the fourth cervical nerve, with contributions from the third and fifth.
- Several smaller cutaneous nerves supply the skin of the neck and nearby areas.
- The phrenic nerve is the most clinically important branch.

---

## Why the Cervical Plexus Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The cervical plexus underlies breathing and neck function relevant to rehabilitation. Each point below connects it to practice.

- The phrenic nerve drives the diaphragm, so a high cervical injury above its origin can stop breathing.
- This is why the level of a spinal cord injury critically affects respiratory function.
- The plexus's supply to the neck is relevant to neck movement and sensation you assess.
- Understanding the phrenic nerve is central to respiratory physiotherapy after spinal injury.
- Understanding the cervical plexus grounds your reasoning about breathing after neck injury.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to the control box that runs the main pump.

- It is a mistake to overlook the phrenic nerve, the plexus's most important branch.
- It is a mistake to think the cervical plexus supplies the upper limb, since that is the brachial plexus.
- It is a mistake to place the phrenic nerve's origin below the cervical region, since it arises mainly from C4.
- It is a mistake to forget that a high cervical injury threatens breathing.

## Key Takeaways

We have described the cervical plexus. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The cervical plexus is formed by the anterior rami of C1 to C4.
- It supplies the skin and muscles of the neck.
- Through the phrenic nerve, arising mainly from C4, it supplies the diaphragm.
- It is essential to breathing as well as neck function.
- A high cervical injury above the phrenic origin can stop breathing.
