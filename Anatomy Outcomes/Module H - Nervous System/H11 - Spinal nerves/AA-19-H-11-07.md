# The Lumbar and Sacral Plexuses

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: For the lumbar and sacral plexuses, list the spinal nerves that form them, describe the plexus' major motor and sensory distributions, and list the major named nerves that originate from the plexuses.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The lumbar and sacral plexuses together supply the lower limb, giving rise to the large nerves that power standing and walking. Understanding them explains the patterns of weakness and sensory loss in lower limb nerve injuries. Today we describe the spinal nerves that form the lumbar and sacral plexuses, their distributions, and their major nerves.

- By the end of this session you will be able to list the spinal nerves forming the lumbar and sacral plexuses.
- You will be able to describe their motor and sensory distributions.
- You will be able to name their major nerves.
- You will be able to connect these plexuses to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Two Plexuses That Supply the Lower Limb

The organising idea is that the lumbar and sacral plexuses together supply the lower limb, with the lumbar plexus serving mainly the front of the thigh and the sacral plexus serving the back of the limb and the foot. Two plexuses for the leg. This idea of paired lower-limb plexuses frames the topic.

- The lumbar and sacral plexuses are formed from the lumbar and sacral spinal nerves.
- Together they supply the lower limb.
- The lumbar plexus serves mainly the front of the thigh.
- The sacral plexus serves the back of the limb, the leg, and the foot.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Two Distribution Hubs Sharing One Territory

To keep the plexuses concrete, picture two distribution hubs serving one large territory, one covering the front and one the back, together supplying the whole area. The lumbar and sacral plexuses are these two hubs for the lower limb, and the image keeps the shared supply in view.

- The front-covering hub corresponds to the lumbar plexus serving the front of the thigh.
- The back-covering hub corresponds to the sacral plexus serving the back of the limb.
- The one shared territory corresponds to the lower limb.
- The analogy breaks down in that hubs follow a plan, whereas the plexuses are networks of living nerve fibres.

---

## The Lumbar Plexus

Begin with the lumbar plexus, serving mainly the front of the thigh. It arises from the lumbar nerves.

- The lumbar plexus is formed mainly by the anterior rami of the first four lumbar nerves.
- It supplies the muscles and skin of the front and inner thigh.
- Its major nerve is the femoral nerve, which supplies the quadriceps and skin of the front of the thigh.
- The obturator nerve is another major branch, supplying the inner thigh muscles.

---

## The Sacral Plexus

Next the sacral plexus, serving the back of the limb and the foot. It arises from lower nerves.

- The sacral plexus is formed by the anterior rami of the lower lumbar and upper sacral nerves.
- It supplies the muscles and skin of the buttock, back of the thigh, and most of the leg and foot.
- Its major nerve is the sciatic nerve, the largest nerve in the body.
- The sciatic nerve divides into the tibial and common fibular nerves supplying the leg and foot.

---

## The Major Named Nerves

Finally the major nerves of the two plexuses together, which you assess in lower limb problems. Three are key.

- The femoral nerve, from the lumbar plexus, extends the knee and gives sensation to the front of the thigh.
- The obturator nerve, from the lumbar plexus, supplies the muscles that adduct the thigh.
- The sciatic nerve, from the sacral plexus, supplies the back of the thigh and, through its branches, the leg and foot.
- These nerves power standing, walking, and the movement of the lower limb.

---

## Why These Plexuses Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The lumbar and sacral plexuses underlie lower limb nerve injuries central to walking and mobility. Each point below connects them to practice.

- The femoral and sciatic nerves power standing and walking, so their injury profoundly affects mobility.
- Sciatic nerve problems, as in sciatica, produce pain and signs down the leg you assess and treat.
- Injury to a specific nerve produces a recognisable pattern of weakness and sensory loss.
- The plexuses' supply underlies the movement and sensation you rehabilitate in the lower limb.
- Understanding these plexuses grounds your reasoning about lower limb nerve injury.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to two hubs sharing one territory.

- It is a mistake to confuse the femoral and sciatic nerves, since one serves the front of the thigh and the other the back of the limb.
- It is a mistake to think one plexus supplies the whole lower limb, since the two share the supply.
- It is a mistake to overlook that the sciatic nerve divides into the tibial and common fibular nerves.
- It is a mistake to forget the obturator nerve's role in thigh adduction.

## Key Takeaways

We have described the lumbar and sacral plexuses. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The lumbar plexus is formed mainly from L1 to L4 and serves the front of the thigh, chiefly via the femoral and obturator nerves.
- The sacral plexus is formed from the lower lumbar and upper sacral nerves and serves the back of the limb and foot.
- Its major nerve is the sciatic, the largest nerve, dividing into the tibial and common fibular nerves.
- Together the plexuses supply the whole lower limb.
- They underlie the lower limb nerve injuries central to walking and mobility.
