# The Dermatome and Its Clinical Significance

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Describe the concept of a dermatome and its clinical significance.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

Each spinal nerve carries sensation from a specific strip of skin, called a dermatome, and mapping these strips lets you locate the level of a nerve problem from where sensation is altered. Understanding dermatomes is a core clinical skill. Today we describe the concept of a dermatome and its clinical significance.

- By the end of this session you will be able to define a dermatome.
- You will be able to explain how dermatomes map the body.
- You will be able to explain the clinical significance of dermatomes.
- You will be able to connect dermatomes to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Each Spinal Nerve Owns a Strip of Skin

The organising idea is that each spinal nerve carries sensation from a defined strip of skin, so that altered sensation in a particular strip points to a problem at that nerve's level. One nerve, one skin strip. This idea of nerve-mapped skin frames the topic.

- A dermatome is the area of skin supplied by a single spinal nerve.
- The dermatomes together map the whole body surface.
- Altered sensation in a dermatome points to a problem at that nerve's level.
- Dermatomes let you localise a nerve problem from the pattern of sensory change.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Map Divided Into Postal Districts

To keep dermatomes concrete, picture the body surface divided into postal districts, each served by one delivery route, so that a failure of one route affects exactly its district. Each dermatome is a district served by one spinal nerve, and a sensory change in a district points to its nerve. The image keeps the nerve-mapped districts in view.

- Each postal district corresponds to a dermatome.
- Each delivery route corresponds to a spinal nerve.
- A failure affecting one district corresponds to a nerve problem altering its dermatome.
- The analogy breaks down in that postal districts have sharp borders, whereas dermatomes overlap somewhat at their edges.

---

## Defining a Dermatome

Begin with the definition itself, stated precisely. It is nerve-mapped skin.

- A dermatome is the region of skin whose sensation is supplied by a single spinal nerve.
- Each spinal nerve, except the first cervical, supplies a dermatome.
- The dermatomes are arranged in a consistent pattern over the body.
- Adjacent dermatomes overlap somewhat, so a single nerve loss may not abolish all sensation in its strip.

---

## How Dermatomes Map the Body

Next how the dermatomes are arranged, since the pattern is used clinically. They run in bands.

- On the trunk the dermatomes are arranged in roughly horizontal bands.
- On the limbs they run in longer strips reflecting how the limbs developed.
- The pattern is consistent enough to be mapped and used in examination.
- Knowing the map lets you identify which nerve supplies a given area of skin.

---

## The Clinical Significance

Finally the clinical significance, which is the point of the concept. Dermatomes localise nerve problems.

- Altered sensation confined to a dermatome points to a problem at that spinal nerve's level.
- This helps localise a nerve root compression, as from a disc problem, to a specific level.
- The pattern of a rash, as in shingles, often follows a dermatome, revealing the affected nerve.
- Testing sensation across dermatomes is part of the neurological examination.

---

## Why Dermatomes Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

Dermatomes are a core tool for localising nerve problems. Each point below connects them to practice.

- Testing dermatomes lets you localise the level of a nerve root problem in a patient with limb pain or numbness.
- A dermatomal pattern of sensory loss points to a specific nerve root rather than a peripheral nerve.
- Dermatome mapping is part of the assessment of spinal and nerve conditions you treat.
- Recognising a dermatomal rash helps you identify conditions such as shingles.
- Understanding dermatomes grounds your localisation of sensory problems.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the concept, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to the map of postal districts.

- It is a mistake to think dermatomes have sharp, non-overlapping borders, since adjacent ones overlap.
- It is a mistake to confuse a dermatomal pattern with a peripheral nerve pattern, since they differ.
- It is a mistake to expect total sensory loss from a single nerve, since overlap preserves some sensation.
- It is a mistake to overlook the clinical use of dermatomes in localising problems.
