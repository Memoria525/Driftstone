## Describe the general functions of the nervous system

# The General Functions of the Nervous System

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Describe the general functions of the nervous system.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The nervous system is the body's control and communication network, and every movement you assess and every sensation your patients report depends on it. Understanding its general functions frames the whole of neuroanatomy and much of rehabilitation. Today we describe the general functions of the nervous system.

- By the end of this session you will be able to name the three general functions of the nervous system.
- You will be able to describe each function in turn.
- You will be able to explain how the three functions form a single sequence.
- You will be able to connect these functions to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Sense, Decide, Act

The organising idea is that the nervous system works as a three-step sequence, taking in information, processing it, and producing a response, so that sensation, integration, and motor output form one continuous loop. Every neural event is a step in this sense-decide-act cycle. This idea of a three-step loop frames the functions.

- The nervous system performs three general functions in sequence.
- It senses changes through sensory input.
- It processes that information through integration.
- It responds through motor output.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Building's Control Room

To keep the functions concrete, picture a building's central control room: sensors report conditions throughout the building, controllers weigh the readings and decide what to do, and commands are sent out to adjust the heating, doors, and lighting. The nervous system is this control room, and the image keeps sensing, deciding, and acting together.

- The sensors reporting conditions correspond to sensory input.
- The controllers weighing readings and deciding correspond to integration.
- The commands sent to adjust the building correspond to motor output.
- The analogy breaks down in that a control room is wired to fixed machinery, whereas the nervous system also learns, adapts, and generates experience and thought.

---

## Sensory Input

Begin with the first function, gathering information. This is sensory input.

- Sensory input is the detection of changes inside and outside the body.
- Sensory receptors gather information about stimuli such as touch, temperature, and position.
- This information is carried by sensory neurons toward the central nervous system.
- Without sensory input the system would have no information on which to act.

---

## Integration

Next the second function, processing the information. This is integration.

- Integration is the processing and interpretation of sensory information.
- The central nervous system weighs the incoming information against memory and current needs.
- It decides what response, if any, is appropriate.
- Integration is where perception, decision, and planning occur.

---

## Motor Output

Finally the third function, producing a response. This is motor output.

- Motor output is the response produced by the nervous system.
- Motor neurons carry commands from the central nervous system to effectors.
- The effectors are muscles and glands that carry out the response.
- Motor output includes movement, changes in organ activity, and secretion.

---

## Why These Functions Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The three functions underlie movement, sensation, and the control you assess and rehabilitate. Each point below connects a function to practice.

- Motor output is the movement you assess, train, and restore in rehabilitation.
- Sensory input underlies the touch, position sense, and pain your patients report.
- Integration underlies motor control and the planning of movement you retrain after injury.
- A problem at any step, from sensation to processing to output, produces a movement deficit you must localise.
- Understanding the three functions grounds your reasoning about neurological presentations.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors narrow the nervous system's roles, and naming them keeps them complete. When unsure, return to the building's control room.

- It is a mistake to see the nervous system only as producing movement, since it also senses and integrates.
- It is a mistake to overlook integration, which is where decisions and planning occur.
- It is a mistake to treat the functions as separate, since they form one continuous loop.
- It is a mistake to forget that motor output includes gland secretion, not just movement.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Naming the three functions in sequence aloud is the strongest test.

- Name the three general functions of the nervous system from memory.
- Describe each function in turn.
- Explain how the three functions form a single sequence.
- Give an example of each function in everyday movement.
- Relate one function to a physiotherapy-relevant concern.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have described the nervous system's general functions. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The nervous system senses, integrates, and responds in a continuous loop.
- Sensory input detects changes inside and outside the body.
- Integration processes the information and decides on a response.
- Motor output carries commands to muscles and glands.
- A problem at any step produces a deficit you must localise in practice.
