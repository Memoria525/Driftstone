## Identify and describe the epidural space, subdural space and subarachnoid space associated with the brain and the spinal cord, and identify which space contains cerebrospinal fluid

# The Spaces Around the Meninges

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Identify and describe the epidural space, subdural space and subarachnoid space associated with the brain and the spinal cord, and identify which space contains cerebrospinal fluid.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The meninges define several spaces around the central nervous system, and knowing which space holds cerebrospinal fluid and which is a site of bleeding is clinically essential. Understanding these spaces explains lumbar puncture, epidural anaesthesia, and the patterns of intracranial bleeding. Today we identify and describe the epidural, subdural, and subarachnoid spaces.

- By the end of this session you will be able to name the spaces around the meninges.
- You will be able to describe the location of each space.
- You will be able to identify which space holds cerebrospinal fluid.
- You will be able to connect the spaces to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Named Spaces Defined by the Meningeal Layers

The organising idea is that each space is named by its relationship to the meningeal layers, so the epidural space lies outside the dura, the subdural space lies beneath the dura, and the subarachnoid space lies beneath the arachnoid and holds the cerebrospinal fluid. Spaces named by which membrane bounds them. This idea of layer-defined spaces frames the topic.

- The meningeal spaces are named by their relationship to the meningeal layers.
- The epidural space lies outside the dura.
- The subdural space lies between the dura and the arachnoid.
- The subarachnoid space lies beneath the arachnoid and holds the cerebrospinal fluid.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Gaps Between Layers of Packaging

To keep the spaces concrete, picture an item wrapped in layers of packaging, with potential gaps outside the tough outer wrap, just beneath it, and beneath the padded middle layer where a cushioning fluid sits. The spaces around the meninges are these gaps, named by which wrap they lie against. The image keeps the layer-defined spaces in view.

- The gap outside the tough outer wrap corresponds to the epidural space outside the dura.
- The gap just beneath the outer wrap corresponds to the subdural space beneath the dura.
- The fluid-filled gap beneath the padded layer corresponds to the subarachnoid space beneath the arachnoid.
- The analogy breaks down in that packaging gaps are empty, whereas these spaces contain fat, fluid, or vessels and are living tissue boundaries.

---

## The Epidural Space

Begin with the outermost space, outside the dura. It differs between brain and cord.

- The epidural space lies between the dura and the surrounding bone.
- Around the spinal cord it is a real space filled with fat and blood vessels.
- Around the brain it is only a potential space, because the cranial dura is fused to the skull.
- The spinal epidural space is where epidural injections are administered.

---

## The Subdural Space

Next the space just beneath the dura. It is a potential space.

- The subdural space lies between the dura and the arachnoid.
- It is normally only a potential space, with the layers in contact.
- It can become a real space when blood collects there, as in a subdural haemorrhage.
- It does not normally contain cerebrospinal fluid.

---

## The Subarachnoid Space

Finally the space beneath the arachnoid, which holds the cerebrospinal fluid. This is the key space.

- The subarachnoid space lies between the arachnoid and the pia mater.
- It is a real space containing cerebrospinal fluid.
- It cushions the brain and spinal cord and carries the larger blood vessels.
- It is the space sampled in a lumbar puncture to obtain cerebrospinal fluid.

---

## Why These Spaces Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The spaces underlie clinical procedures and the patterns of bleeding around the central nervous system. Each point below connects the spaces to practice.

- The subarachnoid space is sampled in lumbar puncture, performed below the end of the cord to obtain cerebrospinal fluid.
- The spinal epidural space is where epidural anaesthesia and injections are delivered, relevant to patients you rehabilitate.
- Bleeding into the epidural or subdural space after head injury produces distinct clinical patterns.
- Knowing that the subarachnoid space holds cerebrospinal fluid clarifies where the fluid circulates.
- Understanding the spaces grounds your reasoning about procedures and injury around the central nervous system.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors blur the spaces, and naming them keeps them distinct. When unsure, return to gaps between layers of packaging.

- It is a mistake to place cerebrospinal fluid in the epidural or subdural space, since it lies in the subarachnoid space.
- It is a mistake to think an epidural space surrounds the brain, since the cranial dura is fused to the skull.
- It is a mistake to treat the subdural space as normally open, since it is a potential space.
- It is a mistake to confuse the spaces, since each is defined by a different meningeal layer.

## Key Takeaways

We have identified the spaces around the meninges. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The epidural space lies outside the dura, real around the cord and only potential around the brain.
- The subdural space lies between the dura and arachnoid and is a potential space.
- The subarachnoid space lies beneath the arachnoid and contains the cerebrospinal fluid.
- The subarachnoid space is sampled in lumbar puncture and the spinal epidural space is used for anaesthesia.
- The spaces underlie procedures and the patterns of bleeding around the central nervous system.
