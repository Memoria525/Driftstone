## Identify and describe the structure and function of the cranial dural septa

# The Cranial Dural Septa

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Identify and describe the structure and function of the cranial dural septa.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The inner layer of the cranial dura folds inward to form partitions, the dural septa, that divide the cranial cavity and hold the brain in place. Understanding them explains how the brain is compartmentalised and stabilised. Today we identify and describe the structure and function of the cranial dural septa.

- By the end of this session you will be able to name the major dural septa.
- You will be able to describe where each lies.
- You will be able to explain their function in supporting the brain.
- You will be able to connect the septa to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Internal Partitions That Divide and Steady the Brain

The organising idea is that the dural septa are inward folds of the meningeal dura that partition the cranial cavity, separating major regions of the brain and limiting its movement within the skull. Internal walls that divide and steady. This idea of internal partitions frames the topic.

- The dural septa are inward folds of the cranial meningeal dura.
- They partition the cranial cavity into compartments.
- They separate major regions of the brain from one another.
- They limit excessive movement of the brain within the skull.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Padded Dividers in a Shipping Crate

To keep the septa concrete, picture a shipping crate fitted with padded internal dividers that separate fragile items and stop them shifting during transport. The dural septa are these internal dividers within the skull, separating parts of the brain and steadying them. The image keeps partitioning and stabilising together.

- The padded internal dividers correspond to the dural septa.
- Separating fragile items corresponds to separating regions of the brain.
- Stopping the items shifting corresponds to limiting the brain's movement.
- The analogy breaks down in that crate dividers are inserted, whereas the dural septa are folds of the brain's own covering membrane.

---

## The Falx Cerebri

Begin with the largest septum, which divides the two cerebral hemispheres. This is the falx cerebri.

- The falx cerebri is a large sickle-shaped fold of dura in the midline.
- It descends into the longitudinal fissure between the two cerebral hemispheres.
- It partially separates the left and right hemispheres.
- It contains the superior sagittal sinus along its upper edge.

---

## The Tentorium Cerebelli and Other Septa

Next the septum between the cerebrum and cerebellum, and smaller folds. These complete the partitioning.

- The tentorium cerebelli is a horizontal fold of dura between the cerebrum above and the cerebellum below.
- It supports the back of the cerebrum and separates it from the cerebellum.
- The falx cerebelli is a smaller fold partially separating the two cerebellar hemispheres.
- The diaphragma sellae is a small fold roofing the space that holds the pituitary gland.

---

## The Function of the Septa

Finally the function of the septa together, since they act as a system. They stabilise and separate.

- The septa divide the cranial cavity into compartments.
- They separate major brain regions, limiting the spread of movement between them.
- They stabilise the brain, restricting its displacement during movement of the head.
- They also enclose several of the dural venous sinuses along their edges.

---

## Why the Dural Septa Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The septa underlie the compartmentalisation of the brain relevant to injury. Each point below connects the septa to practice.

- By limiting brain movement, the septa reduce the displacement of the brain during head movement and impact.
- In raised intracranial pressure, brain tissue can be forced against or under the septa, a serious event called herniation.
- The septa's role in stabilising the brain is relevant to understanding head injury.
- Their edges carry venous sinuses that drain the brain.
- Understanding the septa grounds your reasoning about the brain's protection and injury.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture of the septa, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to padded dividers in a crate.

- It is a mistake to think the septa fully separate the brain regions, since they only partially divide them.
- It is a mistake to believe the septa are separate structures inserted into the skull, since they are dural folds.
- It is a mistake to overlook that the septa carry venous sinuses along their edges.
- It is a mistake to forget their role in limiting brain movement.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Naming each septum and its location aloud is the strongest test.

- Name the major dural septa from memory.
- Describe where the falx cerebri and tentorium cerebelli lie.
- Explain the function of the septa in supporting the brain.
- Explain how the septa relate to the venous sinuses.
- Relate the septa to a physiotherapy-relevant concern.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have described the internal partitions of the cranial dura. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The dural septa are inward folds of the cranial meningeal dura.
- The falx cerebri separates the cerebral hemispheres, and the tentorium cerebelli separates the cerebrum from the cerebellum.
- Smaller septa include the falx cerebelli and the diaphragma sellae.
- The septa partition the cranial cavity, stabilise the brain, and enclose venous sinuses.
- They underlie the compartmentalisation of the brain relevant to head injury.
