## Compare and contrast the structure of the dura mater surrounding the brain and the spinal cord

# The Cranial and Spinal Dura Mater Compared

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Compare and contrast the structure of the dura mater surrounding the brain and the spinal cord.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The dura mater differs between the brain and the spinal cord in a way that has direct clinical consequences, especially for the spaces around it. Understanding the difference explains why an epidural space exists around the cord but not the brain. Today we compare and contrast the structure of the cranial and spinal dura mater.

- By the end of this session you will be able to describe the cranial dura and its layers.
- You will be able to describe the spinal dura.
- You will be able to contrast the two and explain the epidural space.
- You will be able to connect the difference to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: One Layer or Two, and Whether a Space Exists

The organising idea is that the cranial dura is a double layer fused to the skull, while the spinal dura is a single layer separated from the bone by a fat-filled epidural space. The difference is whether the dura hugs the bone or floats within a space. This idea of fused versus free dura frames the comparison.

- The dura mater differs in structure between the brain and the spinal cord.
- The cranial dura has two layers and is fused to the skull.
- The spinal dura is a single layer separated from the bone by a space.
- This difference creates an epidural space around the cord but not the brain.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Wallpaper Glued to a Wall Versus a Curtain Hung Inside It

To keep the comparison concrete, picture two ways of lining a room: wallpaper glued directly to the wall with no gap, and a curtain hung a little inside the wall with a padded gap behind it. The cranial dura is the glued wallpaper, and the spinal dura is the hung curtain with padding behind. The image keeps the presence or absence of a space in view.

- The wallpaper glued to the wall corresponds to the cranial dura fused to the skull.
- The curtain hung inside the wall corresponds to the spinal dura separated from the bone.
- The padded gap behind the curtain corresponds to the fat-filled epidural space around the cord.
- The analogy breaks down in that wallpaper and curtains are inert, whereas the dura is living tissue enclosing and protecting nervous tissue.

---

## The Cranial Dura Mater

Begin with the cranial dura, which is doubled and fused to the skull. Its two layers do different jobs.

- The cranial dura mater has two layers, an outer periosteal layer and an inner meningeal layer.
- The outer periosteal layer is fused to the inner surface of the skull, serving as its periosteum.
- The two layers are mostly fused together but separate in places to form the dural venous sinuses.
- The inner meningeal layer also folds inward to form partitions between brain regions.

---

## The Spinal Dura Mater

Next the spinal dura, which is single and free of the bone. A space lies around it.

- The spinal dura mater has only a single meningeal layer.
- It is not fused to the vertebral bone.
- Between the spinal dura and the bony vertebral canal lies the epidural space.
- This epidural space is filled with fat and blood vessels, cushioning the cord.

---

## Comparing the Two

Holding the two together lets us state the comparison cleanly, which the objective asks. The layers and spaces differ.

- The cranial dura has two layers, while the spinal dura has one.
- The cranial dura is fused to the skull, while the spinal dura is separated from the bone.
- A fat-filled epidural space surrounds the spinal dura but not the cranial dura.
- The cranial dura forms venous sinuses and partitions, which the spinal dura does not.

---

## Why This Comparison Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The difference underlies clinical procedures and the patterns of bleeding around the central nervous system. Each point below connects the comparison to practice.

- The spinal epidural space is where epidural injections and anaesthesia are administered, relevant to patients you rehabilitate.
- The absence of an epidural space around the brain means extradural bleeding there strips the dura from the skull under pressure.
- Understanding the fat-filled spinal epidural space clarifies the anatomy of spinal procedures.
- The dural partitions of the cranial dura are relevant to how the brain is compartmentalised.
- The comparison grounds your reasoning about the anatomy of the central nervous system coverings.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors blur the comparison, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to glued wallpaper versus a hung curtain.

- It is a mistake to give the spinal dura two layers, since it has one.
- It is a mistake to think an epidural space surrounds the brain, since the cranial dura is fused to the skull.
- It is a mistake to overlook that the cranial dura forms the venous sinuses.
- It is a mistake to assume the dura is the same throughout, since it differs between brain and cord.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Contrasting the two dura arrangements aloud is the strongest test.

- Describe the cranial dura and its two layers from memory.
- Describe the spinal dura.
- Contrast the two and explain the epidural space.
- Explain why an epidural space exists around the cord but not the brain.
- Relate the comparison to a physiotherapy-relevant procedure.

## Key Takeaways

We have compared the dura of the brain and cord. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The cranial dura has two layers and is fused to the skull.
- The spinal dura has a single layer separated from the bone.
- A fat-filled epidural space surrounds the spinal dura but not the cranial dura.
- The cranial dura forms the venous sinuses and inward partitions.
- The difference underlies spinal procedures and the patterns of bleeding around the central nervous system.
