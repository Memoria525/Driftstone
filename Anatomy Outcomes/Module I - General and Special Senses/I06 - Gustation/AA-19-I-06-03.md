# The Cranial Nerves of Taste

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: List the cranial nerves that transmit taste information from the tongue to the brain.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

Taste from the tongue is carried to the brain by three cranial nerves, each serving a different part of the tongue and throat. Knowing them connects taste to the cranial nerves and explains how taste loss can localise a nerve problem. Today we list the cranial nerves that transmit taste information.

- By the end of this session you will be able to list the cranial nerves that carry taste.
- You will be able to state which region each serves.
- You will be able to explain how taste loss can localise a nerve problem.
- You will be able to connect the nerves to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Three Cranial Nerves Divide the Tongue and Throat for Taste

The organising idea is that taste is carried by three cranial nerves, the facial nerve for the front of the tongue, the glossopharyngeal for the back, and the vagus for the throat. Three nerves for three regions of taste. This idea of divided taste territory frames the topic.

- Taste is transmitted to the brain by three cranial nerves.
- The facial nerve carries taste from the front of the tongue.
- The glossopharyngeal nerve carries taste from the back of the tongue.
- The vagus nerve carries taste from the throat.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Three Delivery Routes Serving Three Zones

To keep the nerves concrete, picture three delivery routes each serving a different zone of a region, one the front, one the back, and one the far throat. The three taste nerves serve these zones, and the image keeps the divided territory in view.

- The route serving the front zone corresponds to the facial nerve for the front of the tongue.
- The route serving the back zone corresponds to the glossopharyngeal nerve for the back of the tongue.
- The route serving the far throat corresponds to the vagus nerve for the throat.
- The analogy breaks down in that delivery routes are drawn on a map, whereas the nerve territories are set by anatomy.

---

## The Facial and Glossopharyngeal Nerves

Begin with the two nerves serving the tongue, front and back. These carry most taste.

- The facial nerve, cranial nerve seven, carries taste from the front two-thirds of the tongue.
- It is a mixed nerve that also controls facial expression.
- The glossopharyngeal nerve, cranial nerve nine, carries taste from the back third of the tongue.
- It is a mixed nerve that also serves the throat.

---

## The Vagus Nerve

Next the nerve serving the throat. It carries taste from beyond the tongue.

- The vagus nerve, cranial nerve ten, carries taste from the throat and the area around the epiglottis.
- It is a mixed nerve with a wide distribution to the internal organs.
- It carries the smallest share of taste, from the region beyond the tongue.
- Together the three nerves cover all the regions of taste.

---

## How Taste Loss Localises a Problem

Finally how the divided territory helps localise a nerve problem. Regional taste loss points to a nerve.

- Because each nerve serves a specific region, taste loss in that region points to that nerve.
- Loss of taste from the front of the tongue points to the facial nerve.
- Loss of taste from the back of the tongue points to the glossopharyngeal nerve.
- Testing taste by region can therefore help localise a nerve problem.

---

## Why the Taste Nerves Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The taste nerves connect taste to the cranial nerves relevant to some conditions. Each point below connects them to practice.

- The facial nerve carries taste and controls facial expression, so facial nerve conditions can affect both.
- Testing taste is part of the cranial nerve examination in a neurological assessment.
- Taste loss can accompany conditions affecting these nerves.
- Understanding the nerves clarifies how taste reaches the brain.
- Understanding them grounds your reasoning about the cranial nerves and taste.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors weaken this knowledge, and naming them keeps it reliable. When unsure, return to three delivery routes serving three zones.

- It is a mistake to think one nerve carries all taste, since three nerves divide the territory.
- It is a mistake to swap the facial and glossopharyngeal nerves, since one serves the front and the other the back of the tongue.
- It is a mistake to overlook the vagus nerve's small taste role in the throat.
- It is a mistake to forget that these are all mixed nerves with other functions.
