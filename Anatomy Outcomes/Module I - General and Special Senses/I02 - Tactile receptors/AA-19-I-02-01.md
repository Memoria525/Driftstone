# The Types of Tactile Receptor

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Compare and contrast the location, structure, and function of the different types of tactile receptors (tactile [Merkel] disc and lamellated [Pacinian] corpuscle).

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The skin contains several kinds of touch receptor, each tuned to a different quality of touch, from light pressure to deep vibration. Learning them explains how the skin distinguishes a gentle touch from a firm one or a steady press from a vibration. Today we compare and contrast the types of tactile receptor.

- By the end of this session you will be able to name the main tactile receptors.
- You will be able to describe the structure and location of each.
- You will be able to state what quality of touch each detects.
- You will be able to connect the tactile receptors to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Different Receptors for Different Qualities of Touch

The organising idea is that the skin has several tactile receptors, each specialised for a different quality of touch, so that together they let us distinguish light touch, steady pressure, vibration, and stretch. Different receptors for different touches. This idea of specialised touch receptors frames the comparison.

- The skin contains several types of tactile receptor.
- Each is specialised for a different quality of touch.
- They differ in structure, depth in the skin, and the touch they detect.
- Together they give the rich sense of touch.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Different Instruments for Different Signals

To keep the receptors concrete, picture a set of instruments each tuned to a different signal: one for the lightest contact, one for steady pressure, one for rapid vibration, and one for stretch. The tactile receptors are these tuned instruments, and the image keeps the specialisation in view.

- The instrument for lightest contact corresponds to the receptors for fine touch.
- The instrument for steady pressure corresponds to the receptors for sustained pressure.
- The instrument for rapid vibration corresponds to the deep vibration receptor.
- The analogy breaks down in that instruments are separate devices, whereas the receptors feed one integrated sense of touch.

---

## The Superficial Touch Receptors

Begin with the receptors near the surface, detecting fine touch and light pressure. These lie in the upper skin.

- Tactile discs, also called Merkel discs, lie in the deepest layer of the epidermis.
- They detect light touch, pressure, and the texture and edges of objects.
- Tactile corpuscles lie in the upper dermis, especially in sensitive areas such as the fingertips.
- They detect light touch and are important for fine discrimination.

---

## The Deeper Pressure and Vibration Receptors

Next the receptors deeper in the skin, detecting pressure and vibration. These lie in the lower skin.

- Lamellated corpuscles, also called Pacinian corpuscles, lie deep in the dermis and subcutaneous layer.
- They have an onion-like layered structure around a nerve ending.
- They detect deep pressure and rapid vibration.
- Their layered structure makes them respond to changes in pressure rather than to steady pressure.

---

## The Stretch and Sustained-Pressure Receptors

Finally receptors for stretch and sustained pressure, which respond to ongoing deformation. These detect steady signals.

- Bulbous corpuscles, also called Ruffini endings, lie in the dermis.
- They detect skin stretch and sustained pressure.
- They respond continuously while the stimulus lasts.
- Together with the other receptors, they complete the range of touch qualities detected.

---

## Why the Tactile Receptors Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The tactile receptors underlie the touch sensation you assess and its loss in injury. Each point below connects them to practice.

- The tactile receptors provide the touch and pressure sensation you test in sensory examination.
- Fine discrimination from the superficial receptors is central to hand function you rehabilitate.
- Loss of touch sensation, as in nerve injury, raises injury risk and impairs function.
- Vibration sense from the deep receptors is tested in neurological examination.
- Understanding the receptors grounds your reasoning about touch sensation.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors blur the receptors, and naming them keeps them distinct. When unsure, return to different instruments for different signals.

- It is a mistake to think one receptor detects all qualities of touch, since each is specialised.
- It is a mistake to place the lamellated corpuscles near the surface, since they lie deep.
- It is a mistake to expect the lamellated corpuscle to respond to steady pressure, since it responds to change.
- It is a mistake to overlook the receptors for stretch and sustained pressure.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Naming each receptor with its touch quality aloud is the strongest test.

- Name the main tactile receptors from memory.
- Describe the structure and location of each.
- State what quality of touch each detects.
- Explain why the lamellated corpuscle responds to vibration rather than steady pressure.
- Relate one receptor to a physiotherapy-relevant concern.

## Key Takeaways

We have compared the tactile receptors. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The skin has several tactile receptors, each specialised for a different quality of touch.
- Tactile discs and corpuscles near the surface detect light touch and fine discrimination.
- Lamellated corpuscles deep in the skin detect deep pressure and vibration.
- Bulbous corpuscles detect skin stretch and sustained pressure.
- Together they give the rich sense of touch central to sensory examination and hand function.
