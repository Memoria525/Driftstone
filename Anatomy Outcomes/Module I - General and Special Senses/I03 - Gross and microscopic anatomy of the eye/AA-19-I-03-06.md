## Identify and describe the actions of the extrinsic eye (extraocular) muscles

# The Extrinsic Eye Muscles

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Identify and describe the actions of the extrinsic eye (extraocular) muscles.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

Six small muscles attach to each eyeball and move it in every direction, allowing the eyes to aim and track together. Understanding them explains how we direct our gaze and why some nerve injuries cause double vision. Today we identify and describe the actions of the extrinsic eye muscles.

- By the end of this session you will be able to name the extrinsic eye muscles.
- You will be able to describe the action of each.
- You will be able to explain how they work together to aim the eye.
- You will be able to connect the muscles to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Six Muscles That Aim the Eye in Every Direction

The organising idea is that six extrinsic muscles attach to each eyeball and pull it in different directions, so that together they can aim the eye anywhere and keep the two eyes moving together. Six movers for one aiming eye. This idea of coordinated aiming muscles frames the topic.

- Six extrinsic muscles attach to each eyeball.
- Each pulls the eye in a particular direction.
- Together they can aim the eye in any direction.
- They also keep the two eyes moving together.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Guy-Wires Aiming a Camera on a Mount

To keep the muscles concrete, picture a camera on a mount with six wires attached, each pulling it in a different direction so it can be aimed anywhere and held steady. The extrinsic eye muscles are these aiming wires, and the image keeps coordinated aiming in view.

- The wires pulling in different directions correspond to the six muscles.
- Aiming the camera anywhere corresponds to directing the gaze.
- Coordinating the wires corresponds to the muscles working together.
- The analogy breaks down in that wires are passive, whereas the muscles are actively controlled and finely coordinated by the nervous system.

---

## The Four Rectus Muscles

Begin with the four straight muscles, which move the eye up, down, and side to side. These are the recti.

- The superior rectus rolls the eye upward.
- The inferior rectus rolls the eye downward.
- The medial rectus turns the eye inward, toward the nose.
- The lateral rectus turns the eye outward, away from the nose.

---

## The Two Oblique Muscles

Next the two angled muscles, which produce rotation and diagonal movement. These are the obliques.

- The superior oblique turns the eye downward and outward and rotates it.
- The inferior oblique turns the eye upward and outward and rotates it.
- The obliques act at an angle, unlike the straight pull of the recti.
- Together with the recti they allow movement in every direction.

---

## How the Muscles Work Together

Finally how the muscles coordinate, since aiming requires teamwork. They act in balanced pairs.

- The muscles act in opposing pairs, one relaxing as its partner contracts.
- The two eyes move together so that both aim at the same point.
- This coordination keeps a single, fused image and allows tracking of moving objects.
- The muscles are controlled by three cranial nerves.

---

## Why the Extrinsic Eye Muscles Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The eye muscles underlie gaze and its assessment relevant to some conditions. Each point below connects them to practice.

- Eye movement is assessed in cranial nerve examination, part of the neurological assessment you perform.
- Weakness of an eye muscle, from nerve injury, causes double vision and misalignment you may observe.
- Coordinated eye movement is relevant to balance and to following moving targets.
- Understanding the muscles clarifies how gaze is directed and why it can fail.
- Understanding them grounds your reasoning about eye movement.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to guy-wires aiming a camera.

- It is a mistake to confuse the extrinsic muscles that move the eye with the intrinsic muscles inside it.
- It is a mistake to think the recti alone can produce all movements, since the obliques add rotation and diagonal movement.
- It is a mistake to overlook that the two eyes must move together.
- It is a mistake to forget that three cranial nerves control these muscles.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Naming each muscle and its action aloud is the strongest test.

- Name the extrinsic eye muscles from memory.
- Describe the action of each.
- Explain how they work together to aim the eye.
- Explain why an eye muscle weakness causes double vision.
- Relate the muscles to a physiotherapy-relevant assessment.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have described the extrinsic eye muscles. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- Six extrinsic muscles move each eyeball.
- The four recti move the eye up, down, in, and out.
- The two obliques produce rotation and diagonal movement.
- The muscles act in opposing pairs and keep the two eyes moving together.
- They underlie gaze and its assessment in neurological examination.
