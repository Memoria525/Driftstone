## Identify and describe the location and structure of the olfactory epithelium

# The Olfactory Epithelium

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Identify and describe the location and structure of the olfactory epithelium.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The sense of smell begins in a small patch of specialised tissue high in the nose, the olfactory epithelium. Understanding its location and structure explains how airborne molecules are detected. Today we identify and describe the location and structure of the olfactory epithelium.

- By the end of this session you will be able to state where the olfactory epithelium lies.
- You will be able to describe its structure.
- You will be able to describe the olfactory receptor cells.
- You will be able to connect the olfactory epithelium to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: A Patch of Chemical Sensors High in the Nose

The organising idea is that the olfactory epithelium is a small patch of specialised tissue high in the nasal cavity, containing receptor cells that detect airborne chemicals and send signals to the brain. A high patch of smell sensors. This idea of a chemical-sensing patch frames the topic.

- The olfactory epithelium is a patch of specialised tissue in the nasal cavity.
- It lies high in the roof of the nasal cavity.
- It contains olfactory receptor cells that detect airborne chemicals.
- These cells send signals toward the brain.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Sensor Panel in the Ceiling of an Air Duct

To keep the epithelium concrete, picture a sensor panel mounted in the ceiling of an air duct, positioned so that passing air reaches it and its sensors detect the chemicals carried in the air. The olfactory epithelium is this ceiling panel high in the nasal cavity, and the image keeps its position and sensing role in view.

- The ceiling sensor panel corresponds to the olfactory epithelium high in the nose.
- The passing air corresponds to the air drawn through the nasal cavity.
- The sensors detecting chemicals correspond to the olfactory receptor cells.
- The analogy breaks down in that a sensor panel is manufactured, whereas the olfactory epithelium is living tissue whose receptor cells are replaced throughout life.

---

## The Location

Begin with where the epithelium lies, since its high position is important. It sits in the roof of the nasal cavity.

- The olfactory epithelium lies high in the roof of the nasal cavity.
- It sits just beneath the base of the skull.
- Because it is high, air must reach it for us to smell, which is why sniffing helps.
- Its position places it near the olfactory nerves passing into the skull.

---

## The Structure

Next its structure, which includes the receptor cells and supporting cells. It is a specialised epithelium.

- The olfactory epithelium contains olfactory receptor cells, supporting cells, and basal cells.
- The olfactory receptor cells are specialised neurons that detect airborne chemicals.
- The supporting cells nourish and support the receptor cells.
- The basal cells continually produce new receptor cells to replace those that wear out.

---

## The Olfactory Receptor Cells

Finally the receptor cells themselves, which detect smells. They are neurons with extensions into the mucus.

- The olfactory receptor cells are neurons whose endings project into the mucus covering the epithelium.
- Airborne chemicals dissolve in the mucus and bind to these endings.
- Binding triggers a nerve signal in the receptor cell.
- The signal travels toward the brain along the receptor cell's axon.

---

## Why the Olfactory Epithelium Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The olfactory epithelium underlies smell, which has links to safety and wellbeing. Each point below connects it to practice.

- Smell contributes to safety, such as detecting smoke or spoiled food, relevant to a patient's independence.
- Loss of smell can follow head injury, relevant to some patients you treat.
- Smell is linked to appetite and enjoyment of food, relevant to nutrition and wellbeing.
- Understanding the epithelium clarifies how smell is detected and how it can be lost.
- Understanding it grounds your reasoning about the sense of smell.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to the sensor panel in the ceiling of an air duct.

- It is a mistake to place the olfactory epithelium low in the nose, since it lies high in the roof.
- It is a mistake to think the receptor cells are not neurons, since they are specialised neurons.
- It is a mistake to overlook that new receptor cells are continually produced.
- It is a mistake to forget that chemicals must dissolve in mucus to be detected.
