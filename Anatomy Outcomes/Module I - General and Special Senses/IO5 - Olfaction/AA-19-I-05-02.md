## Where We Are Going Today

- By the end of this session you will be able to classify the olfactory receptor cell by stimulus.

## The Big Idea: Smell Is a Chemical Sense

The organising idea is that the olfactory receptor cell is a chemoreceptor, because it detects chemicals, specifically airborne molecules that dissolve in the mucus of the nose. Smell is a chemical sense. This idea of a chemical-detecting receptor frames the classification.

- Receptors are classified by the kind of stimulus they detect.
- The olfactory receptor cell detects chemicals.
- It is therefore classified as a chemoreceptor.
- The chemicals it detects are airborne molecules dissolved in mucus.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Chemical Detector Rather Than a Pressure or Light Sensor

To keep the classification concrete, picture a panel of different detectors, and note that the smell detector belongs with the chemical detectors, not the pressure or light sensors. The olfactory receptor is a chemical detector, and the image keeps its category in view.

- The chemical detector corresponds to the olfactory receptor as a chemoreceptor.
- The pressure and light sensors corresponds to mechanoreceptors and photoreceptors it is not.
- Detecting a substance rather than a force corresponds to responding to chemicals.
- The analogy breaks down in that a detector gives a simple readout, whereas the olfactory system distinguishes a vast range of smells.

---

## Classifying the Receptor

Begin with the classification itself, based on the stimulus. It detects chemicals.

- Receptors that detect chemicals are called chemoreceptors.
- The olfactory receptor cell detects airborne chemical molecules.
- It is therefore a chemoreceptor.
- It shares this category with the taste receptors and with receptors that monitor blood chemistry.

---

## How the Receptor Detects Its Stimulus

Next how the receptor detects chemicals, since this confirms its category. It responds to molecules binding.

- Airborne molecules enter the nose and dissolve in the mucus over the epithelium.
- The dissolved molecules bind to the endings of the olfactory receptor cells.
- The binding triggers a nerve signal in the receptor cell.
- The receptor thus responds to a chemical stimulus, confirming it as a chemoreceptor.

---

## The Range of Smells

Finally the remarkable range the receptors detect, since this distinguishes smell. Many receptor types combine.

- There are many different types of olfactory receptor, each responding to particular molecules.
- The pattern of which receptors are activated allows a vast range of smells to be distinguished.
- The brain interprets these patterns as distinct odours.
- This variety is why smell can distinguish so many different substances.

---

## Why This Classification Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

Classifying smell as a chemical sense connects it to the receptor scheme and to safety. Each point below connects it to practice.

- Recognising smell as a chemical sense places it within the general classification of receptors.
- Smell contributes to safety by detecting hazards such as smoke and gas.
- Loss of smell, as after head injury, removes this chemical warning sense.
- Understanding the classification clarifies how smell fits among the senses.
- Understanding it grounds your reasoning about the sense of smell.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors cloud the classification, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to the chemical detector.

- It is a mistake to classify the olfactory receptor as a mechanoreceptor, since it detects chemicals.
- It is a mistake to think smell detects light or pressure, since it detects molecules.
- It is a mistake to overlook that chemicals must dissolve in mucus to be detected.
- It is a mistake to assume one receptor type detects all smells, since many types combine.
