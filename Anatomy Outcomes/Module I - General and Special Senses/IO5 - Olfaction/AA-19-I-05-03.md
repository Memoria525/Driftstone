# The Olfactory Pathway

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Trace the path of olfaction from the olfactory receptors, to the initiation of a nerve impulse in the olfactory nerves, through the olfactory bulb, olfactory tract, and to the various parts of the brain.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

Once a smell is detected, its signal travels a short and unusual pathway that reaches the brain's emotional and memory centres directly. Tracing it explains why smells so strongly evoke emotion and memory. Today we trace the olfactory pathway from the receptors to the brain.

- By the end of this session you will be able to trace the olfactory pathway to the brain.
- You will be able to name the structures the signal passes through.
- You will be able to explain the pathway's link to emotion and memory.
- You will be able to connect the pathway to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: A Short Pathway Straight to Emotion and Memory

The organising idea is that the olfactory signal travels a short pathway from the nose through the olfactory bulb and tract directly to brain areas involved in emotion and memory, largely bypassing the usual thalamic relay. A short route to the emotional brain. This idea of a direct emotional pathway frames the topic.

- The olfactory signal travels from the nose to the brain by a short pathway.
- It passes through the olfactory bulb and olfactory tract.
- It reaches brain areas involved in emotion and memory relatively directly.
- Unlike other senses, it largely bypasses the usual thalamic relay to the cortex.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Direct Line to the Records and Mood Department

To keep the pathway concrete, picture a direct telephone line that connects the nose straight to the department that handles mood and records, without going through the usual switchboard. The olfactory pathway is this direct line to the emotional and memory areas, and the image keeps its directness in view.

- The direct line corresponds to the short olfactory pathway.
- The mood and records department corresponds to the emotion and memory areas.
- Bypassing the usual switchboard corresponds to largely bypassing the thalamic relay.
- The analogy breaks down in that a telephone line is a single wire, whereas the pathway is a set of nerve fibres and relay structures.

---

## The Initiation of the Signal

Begin with where the signal starts, in the receptor cells. It begins in the nose.

- The olfactory receptor cells detect airborne chemicals dissolved in the mucus.
- Detection triggers a nerve impulse in the receptor cell.
- The axons of the receptor cells form the olfactory nerves.
- These nerves pass up through the base of the skull.

---

## The Olfactory Bulb and Tract

Next the relay and the tract carrying the signal onward. These lie beneath the frontal lobe.

- The olfactory nerves synapse in the olfactory bulb, which lies beneath the frontal lobe.
- The olfactory bulb processes the incoming signals.
- From the bulb, the signal travels along the olfactory tract toward the brain.
- The olfactory tract carries the processed signal to its destinations.

---

## The Destinations in the Brain

Finally where the signal arrives, in the emotional and memory areas. This explains smell's power over memory.

- The olfactory signal reaches areas of the brain involved in emotion and memory, part of the limbic system.
- It also reaches an olfactory area of the cortex where smell is consciously perceived.
- Because it reaches the emotional and memory areas relatively directly, smell strongly evokes emotion and memory.
- This directness distinguishes smell from the other senses.

---

## Why the Olfactory Pathway Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The olfactory pathway underlies smell and its links to memory and wellbeing. Each point below connects it to practice.

- The direct link to emotion and memory is why smells can powerfully evoke memories, relevant to wellbeing.
- Loss of smell can follow head injury damaging the pathway, relevant to some patients.
- Smell's link to appetite is relevant to nutrition in your patients.
- Understanding the pathway clarifies why smell is so tied to emotion and memory.
- Understanding it grounds your reasoning about the sense of smell.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the pathway, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to the direct line to the records and mood department.

- It is a mistake to think smell passes through the thalamus like other senses, since it largely bypasses it.
- It is a mistake to overlook the olfactory bulb's role in processing the signal.
- It is a mistake to forget the pathway's direct link to emotion and memory.
- It is a mistake to place the olfactory bulb far from the frontal lobe, since it lies beneath it.

## Key Takeaways

We have traced the olfactory pathway. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The signal begins in the olfactory receptor cells, whose axons form the olfactory nerves.
- The nerves synapse in the olfactory bulb beneath the frontal lobe.
- The olfactory tract carries the signal to areas of emotion and memory and to an olfactory cortex.
- The pathway largely bypasses the thalamic relay used by other senses.
- Its direct link to emotion and memory is why smell strongly evokes both.
