## Describe the general structures and locations of the bony and membranous labyrinths of the inner ear, and discuss the location of perilymph and endolymph

# The Bony and Membranous Labyrinths

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Describe the general structures and locations of the bony and membranous labyrinths of the inner ear, and discuss the location of perilymph and endolymph.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The inner ear is a maze of fluid-filled channels housing the organs of hearing and balance, built as a membranous tube within a bony cavity. Understanding its layout and its two fluids explains how the sensory organs are supported. Today we describe the bony and membranous labyrinths and the location of perilymph and endolymph.

- By the end of this session you will be able to describe the bony labyrinth.
- You will be able to describe the membranous labyrinth within it.
- You will be able to state where perilymph and endolymph lie.
- You will be able to connect the labyrinths to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: A Membranous Tube Suspended in a Bony Maze, With Two Fluids

The organising idea is that the inner ear is a membranous labyrinth of tubes suspended within a bony labyrinth, with endolymph inside the membranous tube and perilymph between it and the bone. A tube within a maze, bathed by two fluids. This idea of a labyrinth within a labyrinth frames the topic.

- The inner ear has a bony labyrinth and a membranous labyrinth within it.
- The bony labyrinth is a system of cavities in the skull bone.
- The membranous labyrinth is a system of tubes suspended within the bony labyrinth.
- Endolymph fills the membranous tube, and perilymph lies between it and the bone.

---

## Our Master Analogy: An Inner Tube Suspended Inside a Pipe

To keep the labyrinths concrete, picture a flexible inner tube suspended inside a rigid pipe, with fluid inside the inner tube and different fluid in the space between it and the pipe. The membranous labyrinth is the inner tube and the bony labyrinth is the pipe, and the image keeps the two fluids in view.

- The rigid pipe corresponds to the bony labyrinth.
- The flexible inner tube corresponds to the membranous labyrinth.
- The fluid inside the inner tube corresponds to the endolymph.
- The fluid between the tube and pipe corresponds to the perilymph, though the analogy breaks down in that the labyrinth is intricately shaped rather than a simple tube.

---

## The Bony Labyrinth

Begin with the outer bony maze, which houses the membranous labyrinth. It has three regions.

- The bony labyrinth is a system of cavities within the temporal bone of the skull.
- It has three regions: the cochlea for hearing, and the vestibule and semicircular canals for balance.
- Its bony walls protect the delicate structures inside.
- It is filled with the fluid perilymph.

---

## The Membranous Labyrinth

Next the inner membranous tubes, which contain the sensory organs. They follow the shape of the bony labyrinth.

- The membranous labyrinth is a system of membranous tubes and sacs suspended within the bony labyrinth.
- It follows the general shape of the bony labyrinth.
- It contains the sensory organs of hearing and balance.
- It is filled with the fluid endolymph.

---

## The Location of Perilymph and Endolymph

Finally the two fluids and where each lies, since they are easily confused. One is inside, one outside the membranous tube.

- Endolymph fills the inside of the membranous labyrinth.
- Perilymph fills the space between the membranous labyrinth and the bony labyrinth.
- The two fluids differ in composition and are kept separate.
- Movement of these fluids stimulates the sensory organs of hearing and balance.

---

## Why the Labyrinths Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The labyrinths house the balance organ central to balance rehabilitation. Each point below connects them to practice.

- The vestibule and semicircular canals house the balance organs central to the balance you assess and rehabilitate.
- Movement of endolymph stimulates the balance organs, underlying how head movement is sensed.
- Inner ear disorders, such as vertigo, arise from these structures and affect balance.
- Understanding the labyrinths clarifies how balance and hearing are supported.
- Understanding them grounds your reasoning about balance and the inner ear.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to an inner tube suspended inside a pipe.

- It is a mistake to swap the two fluids, since endolymph is inside the membranous labyrinth and perilymph outside it.
- It is a mistake to think the bony and membranous labyrinths are the same, since one is within the other.
- It is a mistake to overlook that the inner ear houses both hearing and balance organs.
- It is a mistake to forget that fluid movement stimulates the sensory organs.

## Key Takeaways

We have described the labyrinths of the inner ear. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The inner ear has a bony labyrinth and a membranous labyrinth within it.
- The bony labyrinth is cavities in the temporal bone, with the cochlea, vestibule, and semicircular canals.
- The membranous labyrinth is suspended tubes containing the sensory organs of hearing and balance.
- Endolymph fills the membranous labyrinth, and perilymph lies between it and the bone.
