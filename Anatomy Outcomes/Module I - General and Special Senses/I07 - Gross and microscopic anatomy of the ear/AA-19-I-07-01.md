## Identify the structures of the external (outer) ear and describe the structure and function of each

# The External Ear

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Identify the structures of the external (outer) ear and describe the structure and function of each.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The external ear collects sound and funnels it toward the eardrum, forming the first stage of hearing. Understanding its structures explains how sound is gathered and directed inward. Today we identify the structures of the external ear and describe the structure and function of each.

- By the end of this session you will be able to name the structures of the external ear.
- You will be able to describe the function of each.
- You will be able to explain how the external ear gathers sound.
- You will be able to connect the external ear to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: A Funnel That Collects Sound and Guides It Inward

The organising idea is that the external ear acts as a funnel, with a curved flap that collects sound and a canal that guides it to the eardrum. A sound-collecting funnel. This idea of a collecting funnel frames the topic.

- The external ear collects sound and directs it inward.
- The curved outer flap gathers sound waves.
- The ear canal guides the sound to the eardrum.
- The eardrum marks the boundary with the middle ear.

---

## Our Master Analogy: An Ear Trumpet Gathering Sound

To keep the external ear concrete, picture an old-fashioned ear trumpet, whose wide curved opening gathers sound and whose narrow tube funnels it toward the ear. The external ear is a built-in ear trumpet, and the image keeps the collecting and funnelling role in view.

- The wide curved opening corresponds to the outer flap gathering sound.
- The narrow funnelling tube corresponds to the ear canal.
- The point where the sound is delivered corresponds to the eardrum.
- The analogy breaks down in that an ear trumpet is a fixed device, whereas the external ear is living tissue that also protects the canal.

---

## The Auricle

Begin with the visible outer flap, which gathers sound. This is the auricle.

- The auricle, or pinna, is the visible curved flap of the external ear.
- It is made mostly of elastic cartilage covered by skin.
- Its curved shape collects sound waves and directs them into the ear canal.
- It also helps us judge the direction a sound comes from.

---

## The External Acoustic Meatus

Next the canal that guides sound inward. This is the external acoustic meatus.

- The external acoustic meatus is the ear canal leading from the auricle to the eardrum.
- It guides sound waves toward the eardrum.
- It contains glands that produce earwax, which protects and cleans the canal.
- Hairs and wax help keep foreign particles out.

---

## The Tympanic Membrane

Finally the eardrum, which marks the boundary of the external ear. This is the tympanic membrane.

- The tympanic membrane, or eardrum, lies at the inner end of the ear canal.
- It is a thin membrane that vibrates when sound waves strike it.
- Its vibration passes the sound on to the middle ear.
- It marks the boundary between the external and middle ear.

---

## Why the External Ear Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The external ear underlies hearing and, through the nearby balance organ, relates to balance. Each point below connects it to practice.

- The external ear gathers sound, the first stage of the hearing you may assess.
- Blockage of the ear canal, as by wax, can reduce hearing, relevant to communication with patients.
- Hearing contributes to a patient's safety and communication during rehabilitation.
- Understanding the external ear clarifies how sound is collected.
- Understanding it grounds your reasoning about hearing.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to an ear trumpet gathering sound.

- It is a mistake to think the external ear detects sound, since it only collects and guides it.
- It is a mistake to overlook the ear canal's role in funnelling sound.
- It is a mistake to place the eardrum outside the boundary of the external ear.
- It is a mistake to forget that earwax protects the canal.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Naming each structure and its function aloud is the strongest test.

- Name the structures of the external ear from memory.
- Describe the function of each.
- Explain how the external ear gathers sound.
- Explain the role of the tympanic membrane.
- Relate the external ear to a physiotherapy-relevant concern.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have described the external ear. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The external ear collects sound and directs it inward.
- The auricle gathers sound waves and helps judge their direction.
- The external acoustic meatus guides sound to the eardrum and is protected by wax.
- The tympanic membrane vibrates and passes the sound to the middle ear.
- The external ear is the first stage of hearing.
