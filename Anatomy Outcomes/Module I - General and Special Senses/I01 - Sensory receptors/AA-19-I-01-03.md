## Where We Are Going Today

Receptors can also be classified by the kind of energy or change they detect, from temperature to light to mechanical force. Learning this classification lets you match each receptor to the stimulus it senses. Today we compare and contrast the types of receptor by type of stimulus.

- By the end of this session you will be able to name the receptor types by stimulus.
- You will be able to state what each detects.
- You will be able to give an example of each.
- You will be able to connect the types to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Each Receptor Type Detects One Kind of Stimulus

The organising idea is that receptors are specialised so that each type detects one kind of stimulus, whether temperature, light, chemicals, pressure, damage, or mechanical force. One receptor type, one kind of stimulus. This idea of stimulus specialisation frames the comparison.

- Receptors can be classified by the kind of stimulus they detect.
- Each type is specialised for one kind of stimulus.
- The types include thermoreceptors, photoreceptors, chemoreceptors, baroreceptors, nociceptors, and mechanoreceptors.
- The name of each type usually indicates the stimulus it detects.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Toolkit of Single-Purpose Meters

To keep the types concrete, picture a toolkit of meters, each measuring one thing: a thermometer for temperature, a light meter for light, a chemical detector, a pressure gauge, a damage alarm, and a force sensor. The receptor types are these single-purpose meters, and the image keeps one-type-one-stimulus in view.

- The thermometer corresponds to the thermoreceptor for temperature.
- The light meter corresponds to the photoreceptor for light.
- The pressure gauge and force sensor correspond to the baroreceptor and mechanoreceptor.
- The damage alarm corresponds to the nociceptor, though the analogy breaks down in that these receptors feed one integrated nervous system rather than separate readouts.

---

## The Thermoreceptors, Photoreceptors, and Chemoreceptors

Begin with the receptors for temperature, light, and chemicals. These detect specific forms of energy or substance.

- Thermoreceptors detect changes in temperature, such as heat and cold on the skin.
- Photoreceptors detect light, and are found in the retina of the eye.
- Chemoreceptors detect chemicals, underlying taste, smell, and the monitoring of blood chemistry.
- Each is tuned to its own kind of stimulus.

---

## The Baroreceptors and Mechanoreceptors

Next the receptors for pressure and mechanical force. These detect physical deformation.

- Baroreceptors detect pressure changes, such as blood pressure in the walls of vessels.
- Mechanoreceptors detect mechanical force, such as touch, pressure, stretch, and vibration.
- Mechanoreceptors include the touch receptors of the skin and the proprioceptors of muscles and joints.
- Both respond to physical deformation of the receptor.

---

## The Nociceptors

Finally the receptors for damage, which underlie pain. These are the nociceptors.

- Nociceptors detect potentially damaging stimuli.
- They respond to extreme temperature, strong mechanical force, and certain chemicals released by damaged tissue.
- Their activation underlies the sensation of pain.
- They warn the body of actual or threatened tissue damage.

---

## Why These Types Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The receptor types underlie the sensations you assess and the pain you manage. Each point below connects a type to practice.

- Mechanoreceptors underlie the touch, pressure, and proprioception you assess and retrain.
- Nociceptors underlie the pain your patients report, central to your management.
- Thermoreceptors underlie temperature sensation you test in examination.
- Understanding that nociceptors detect threat, not simply damage, informs modern pain science.
- Understanding the types grounds your reasoning about sensation and pain.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors blur the types, and naming them keeps them distinct. When unsure, return to the toolkit of single-purpose meters.

- It is a mistake to think one receptor detects several kinds of stimulus, since each is specialised.
- It is a mistake to equate nociceptors with pain itself, since pain is produced by the nervous system's interpretation of their signals.
- It is a mistake to confuse baroreceptors and mechanoreceptors, though both respond to physical force.
- It is a mistake to overlook that proprioceptors are a kind of mechanoreceptor.

## Key Takeaways

We have compared receptors by type of stimulus. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- Receptors are classified by the kind of stimulus they detect, each specialised for one.
- Thermoreceptors detect temperature, photoreceptors light, and chemoreceptors chemicals.
- Baroreceptors detect pressure and mechanoreceptors mechanical force, including touch and proprioception.
- Nociceptors detect potentially damaging stimuli, underlying pain.
- Mechanoreceptors and nociceptors are especially central to physiotherapy.
