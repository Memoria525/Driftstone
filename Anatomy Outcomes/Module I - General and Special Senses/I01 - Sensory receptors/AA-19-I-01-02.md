# Sensory Receptors by Stimulus Origin

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Compare and contrast the three types of sensory receptors, based on their stimulus origin (exteroceptors, interoceptors [visceroceptors], and proprioceptors).

---

## Where We Are Going Today

Sensory receptors can be classified by where the stimulus they detect comes from: the outside world, the internal organs, or the body's own movement and position. Learning this classification clarifies the different streams of sensory information, including the proprioception central to movement. Today we compare and contrast the three types of receptor by stimulus origin.

- By the end of this session you will be able to name the three types by stimulus origin.
- You will be able to state what each detects.
- You will be able to give an example of each.
- You will be able to connect the types to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Receptors Classified by Where the Stimulus Comes From

The organising idea is that receptors can be grouped by the source of their stimulus, with exteroceptors detecting the outside world, interoceptors detecting the internal organs, and proprioceptors detecting the body's own position and movement. Outside, inside, and self. This idea of classification by source frames the comparison.

- Receptors can be classified by the origin of the stimulus they detect.
- Exteroceptors detect stimuli from outside the body.
- Interoceptors detect stimuli from the internal organs.
- Proprioceptors detect the body's own position and movement.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Sensors Facing Out, In, and at the Machine Itself

To keep the types concrete, picture a machine fitted with three kinds of sensor: some facing outward to monitor the environment, some facing inward to monitor the internal workings, and some monitoring the position of the machine's own moving parts. The three receptor types are these sensors, and the image keeps the source of each stimulus in view.

- The outward-facing sensors correspond to the exteroceptors.
- The inward-facing sensors correspond to the interoceptors.
- The sensors on the moving parts correspond to the proprioceptors.
- The analogy breaks down in that machine sensors are installed separately, whereas the receptor types share the same sensory pathways into the nervous system.

---

## The Exteroceptors

Begin with the receptors facing outward, detecting the environment. These are the exteroceptors.

- Exteroceptors detect stimuli from the external environment.
- They are located at or near the body surface.
- They include the receptors for touch, pressure, temperature, and the special senses.
- They inform the body about the outside world.

---

## The Interoceptors

Next the receptors facing inward, detecting the organs. These are the interoceptors.

- Interoceptors, also called visceroceptors, detect stimuli from the internal organs.
- They are located in the walls of the viscera and blood vessels.
- They detect conditions such as stretch, pressure, and the chemical state of the internal environment.
- They inform the body about its internal state, often without reaching conscious awareness.

---

## The Proprioceptors

Finally the receptors monitoring the body itself, detecting position and movement. These are the proprioceptors.

- Proprioceptors detect the position and movement of the body itself.
- They are located in muscles, tendons, and joints.
- They detect stretch, tension, and joint position.
- They inform the body about where its parts are and how they are moving.

---

## Why These Types Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The receptor types underlie sensation, internal awareness, and the proprioception central to movement. Each point below connects a type to practice.

- Proprioceptors provide the position sense central to the balance and coordination you retrain.
- Exteroceptors provide the touch and temperature sensation you assess in examination.
- Interoceptors underlie internal sensations such as visceral pain, relevant to recognising non-musculoskeletal problems.
- Loss of proprioception profoundly affects movement and balance you rehabilitate.
- Understanding the types grounds your reasoning about the different streams of sensation.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors blur the types, and naming them keeps them distinct. When unsure, return to sensors facing out, in, and at the machine.

- It is a mistake to confuse exteroceptors and interoceptors, since one faces the outside world and the other the organs.
- It is a mistake to overlook proprioceptors, which are central to movement.
- It is a mistake to think interoceptive sensation always reaches awareness, since much of it does not.
- It is a mistake to treat the types as detecting the same stimuli, since they differ by source.

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Naming each type with an example aloud is the strongest test.

- Name the three types of receptor by stimulus origin from memory.
- State what each detects.
- Give an example of each.
- Explain why proprioceptors are central to movement.
- Relate one type to a physiotherapy-relevant concern.

## Key Takeaways

We have compared receptors by stimulus origin. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- Receptors are classified by the source of their stimulus.
- Exteroceptors detect the external world at the body surface.
- Interoceptors detect the internal organs' conditions, often below awareness.
- Proprioceptors detect the body's own position and movement in muscles, tendons, and joints.
- Proprioceptors provide the position sense central to balance and coordination.
