## Identify and describe the layers of the integument (skin)

# The Layers of the Skin

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Identify and describe the layers of the integument (skin).

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The skin is built in layers, and understanding those layers is the foundation for reasoning about wounds, burns, and pressure injuries, whose severity is described by how deep they reach. Knowing what each layer is made of and does lets you interpret depth clinically. Today we identify and describe the layers of the skin.

- By the end of this session you will be able to name the two main layers of the skin.
- You will be able to describe the tissue and role of each layer.
- You will be able to place the subcutaneous layer relative to the skin.
- You will be able to connect skin layers to wound depth in practice.

---

## The Big Idea: A Protective Surface on a Supportive Base

The organising idea is that the skin pairs a thin, tough, self-renewing epithelial surface with a thicker, supportive connective tissue base, so that the barrier is backed by the structures that nourish and strengthen it. The two layers are specialised for protection and for support respectively. This pairing of surface and base runs through the topic.

- The skin consists of a superficial epidermis and a deeper dermis.
- The epidermis is a protective epithelial surface.
- The dermis is a supportive connective tissue base.
- Beneath both lies the subcutaneous layer, which is not part of the skin proper.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Tiled Roof on a Timber Frame

To keep the layers concrete, picture a roof: a surface of overlapping tiles that sheds weather and is replaced as tiles wear, resting on a timber frame that carries the load, holds the wiring and plumbing, and supports the tiles. The epidermis is the tile surface and the dermis is the frame beneath, and the image keeps surface and support together.

- The overlapping weatherproof tiles correspond to the epidermis.
- The load-bearing timber frame with its wiring and plumbing corresponds to the dermis with its vessels and nerves.
- The continual replacement of worn tiles corresponds to the constant renewal of the epidermis.
- The analogy breaks down in that a roof is repaired by builders, whereas the skin renews and repairs itself from its own living cells.

---

## The Epidermis

Begin with the epidermis, the outer layer that forms the barrier surface. It is epithelial and avascular.

- The epidermis is the superficial layer, made of keratinised stratified squamous epithelium.
- It is avascular, receiving nutrients by diffusion from the dermis beneath.
- It forms the tough, waterproof barrier that protects against abrasion, water loss, and microbes.
- It renews itself continually, replacing cells shed from its surface.

---

## The Dermis

Next the dermis, the deeper layer that supports the epidermis and does much of the skin's work. It is connective tissue and vascular.

- The dermis is the deeper layer, made of dense connective tissue rich in collagen and elastic fibres.
- It gives the skin its strength, elasticity, and toughness.
- It contains blood vessels, nerves, sensory receptors, and the roots of the appendages.
- It nourishes the overlying epidermis through its blood supply.

---

## The Subcutaneous Layer Beneath

Finally the layer beneath the skin, which supports and anchors it though it is not part of the skin proper. It is largely fat.

- The subcutaneous layer, or hypodermis, lies deep to the dermis.
- It is composed mainly of adipose and loose connective tissue.
- It anchors the skin to underlying muscle and bone while allowing movement.
- It cushions and insulates the body and stores energy.

---

## Why the Skin Layers Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The layers underlie how wounds and pressure injuries are graded and managed, which is core to skin care. Each point below connects a layer to practice.

- Wound and burn depth is described by how far it reaches through the epidermis, dermis, and beyond, guiding management.
- A wound confined to the epidermis heals quickly, whereas one reaching the dermis heals more slowly and may scar.
- The dermal blood supply is what nourishes healing and what pressure occludes to cause injury.
- Loss of subcutaneous fat over bony prominences raises pressure-injury risk.
- Understanding the layers lets you assess wound depth and reason about healing.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture of the skin, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to the tiled roof on a timber frame.

- It is a mistake to think the epidermis has its own blood supply, because it is avascular.
- It is a mistake to place the appendage roots in the epidermis, because they sit in the dermis.
- It is a mistake to count the subcutaneous layer as part of the skin, because the skin is epidermis and dermis.
- It is a mistake to overlook the dermis's role in nourishing the epidermis.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Describing each layer aloud is the strongest test.

- Name the two main layers of the skin from memory.
- Describe the tissue and role of the epidermis.
- Describe the tissue and role of the dermis.
- State what the subcutaneous layer is made of and why it is not part of the skin proper.
- Explain how the skin layers relate to wound depth.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have described the skin's layered construction. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The skin consists of a superficial epidermis and a deeper dermis.
- The epidermis is avascular keratinised stratified squamous epithelium forming the barrier.
- The dermis is dense connective tissue that supports, nourishes, and houses vessels, nerves, and appendages.
- The subcutaneous layer beneath is largely fat and anchors and cushions the skin without being part of it.
- Wound and pressure-injury depth is graded by how far it reaches through these layers.
