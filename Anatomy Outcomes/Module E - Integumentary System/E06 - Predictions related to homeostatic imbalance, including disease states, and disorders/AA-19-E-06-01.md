## List the epidermal derivatives of the integument

# The Epidermal Derivatives of the Integument

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: List the epidermal derivatives of the integument.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The skin gives rise to a set of appendages, all developing from the epidermis, that extend its functions of protection, sensation, and regulation. Listing these epidermal derivatives inventories the accessory structures you assess alongside the skin. Today we list the epidermal derivatives of the integument.

- By the end of this session you will be able to list the epidermal derivatives.
- You will be able to explain what makes them epidermal derivatives.
- You will be able to state the general role of each derivative.
- You will be able to connect the derivatives to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Appendages Grown From the Epidermis

The organising idea is that although these appendages sit within the dermis, they all develop from the epidermis growing down into the skin, which is why they are called epidermal derivatives. They extend the epidermis's protective and regulatory work. This shared epidermal origin frames the list.

- The epidermal derivatives are appendages that develop from the epidermis.
- Although they lie in the dermis, they originate from downgrowths of the epidermis.
- They extend the skin's functions of protection, sensation, and regulation.
- They include hair, nails, and the skin's glands.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Fixtures Grown Down From a Ceiling

To keep the list concrete, picture fixtures that grow downward from a ceiling into the room below, remaining part of the ceiling's material even though they hang in the room. The epidermal derivatives are like this, formed from the epidermis but seated in the dermis below. The image keeps their epidermal origin in view despite their dermal location.

- The ceiling corresponds to the epidermis.
- The fixtures growing down into the room correspond to the appendages growing into the dermis.
- Their being made of the ceiling's material corresponds to the appendages' epidermal origin.
- The analogy breaks down in that fixtures are installed, whereas the appendages develop from the living epidermis during growth.

---

## The Hair and Follicles

Begin with hair, the most widespread derivative. It grows from follicles.

- Hair is an epidermal derivative that covers most of the body surface.
- Each hair grows from a follicle, a downgrowth of the epidermis into the dermis.
- Hair contributes to protection, insulation, and sensation.
- Attached to each follicle is a small arrector pili muscle.

---

## The Nails

Next the nails, hard plates protecting the digits. They too are epidermal.

- Nails are epidermal derivatives forming hard plates over the tips of the fingers and toes.
- They are made of tightly packed keratinised cells.
- They protect the ends of the digits and aid in grip and manipulation.
- They grow from a nail matrix of dividing epidermal cells.

---

## The Glands

Finally the glands, secretory derivatives of the epidermis. Two main kinds exist.

- Sweat glands are epidermal derivatives that secrete sweat for cooling and, in places, scent.
- Sebaceous glands are epidermal derivatives that secrete oily sebum to lubricate skin and hair.
- Both develop as downgrowths of the epidermis into the dermis.
- Together with hair and nails they complete the list of epidermal derivatives.

---

## Why the Derivatives Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The epidermal derivatives carry clinical information and contribute to functions relevant to practice. Each point below connects a derivative to care.

- Sweat glands underlie thermoregulation during exercise, affecting exercise tolerance.
- The nails and skin can reveal signs of circulatory or systemic problems during examination.
- Sebaceous glands maintain skin lubrication, relevant to skin integrity in immobile patients.
- Hair distribution can reflect circulatory status in the limbs.
- Knowing the derivatives ensures you assess the whole integument, not skin alone.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors narrow the list, and naming them keeps it complete. When unsure, return to fixtures grown down from a ceiling.

- It is a mistake to think the appendages are dermal because they lie in the dermis, since they are epidermal in origin.
- It is a mistake to omit the glands from the list of derivatives.
- It is a mistake to forget that hair and nails are made of keratinised epidermal cells.
- It is a mistake to overlook the appendages during skin assessment.

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Listing the derivatives aloud is the strongest test.

- List the epidermal derivatives from memory.
- Explain what makes them epidermal derivatives despite their dermal location.

## Key Takeaways

We have listed the appendages that grow from the epidermis. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The epidermal derivatives are hair, nails, sweat glands, and sebaceous glands.
- They develop from downgrowths of the epidermis, though they lie in the dermis.
- Hair protects and senses, nails protect the digits, and the glands cool and lubricate.
- They extend the skin's functions of protection, sensation, and regulation.
- Assessing them alongside the skin ensures a complete evaluation of the integument.
