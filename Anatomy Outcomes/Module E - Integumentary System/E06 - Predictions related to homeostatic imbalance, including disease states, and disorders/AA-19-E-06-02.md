## Describe the structure, function, and location of hair

# The Structure, Function, and Location of Hair

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Describe the structure, function, and location of hair.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

Hair is a keratinised appendage growing from the skin over nearly the whole body, and it contributes quietly to protection, insulation, and sensation. Understanding its structure and function explains what it does and what its changes can signal. Today we describe the structure, function, and location of hair.

- By the end of this session you will be able to describe the structure of a hair and its follicle.
- You will be able to state the functions of hair.
- You will be able to state where hair is and is not found.
- You will be able to connect hair to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: A Keratinised Shaft Rooted in a Living Follicle

The organising idea is that a hair is a dead keratinised shaft produced by a living follicle rooted in the dermis, so that the visible part is inert while growth happens hidden below. The follicle makes the hair; the hair itself is not alive. This split between living root and dead shaft frames the topic.

- A hair is a keratinised structure produced by a follicle in the skin.
- The visible shaft is dead and keratinised, while the follicle at its root is living.
- Growth occurs at the base of the follicle, not along the shaft.
- Hair covers most of the body and serves protection, insulation, and sensation.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Growing Thread Spun From a Buried Spindle

To keep hair concrete, picture a thread continuously spun from a buried spindle: the thread emerging above ground is finished and lifeless, while all the spinning happens at the spindle below. Cut the thread and it regrows from the spindle; damage the spindle and growth stops. Hair works this way, and the image keeps the living root and dead shaft distinct.

- The buried spindle corresponds to the living follicle root that produces the hair.
- The emerging finished thread corresponds to the dead keratinised shaft.
- Regrowth after cutting corresponds to hair growing back from an intact follicle.
- The analogy breaks down in that a spindle is mechanical, whereas the follicle is living tissue with its own blood supply, nerves, and cycles of growth and rest.

---

## The Structure of Hair

Begin with structure, distinguishing the shaft, the root, and the follicle. Each part has a role.

- The hair shaft is the visible part above the skin surface, made of dead keratinised cells.
- The hair root is the part below the surface, embedded in the follicle.
- The follicle is a downgrowth of the epidermis surrounding the root, with a bulb at its base where cells divide.
- The bulb is supplied by a small vessel and is where the hair grows.

---

## The Function of Hair

Next function, which combines protection, insulation, and sensation. Hair contributes modestly but usefully.

- Hair on the scalp protects against sun and heat loss and cushions minor impacts.
- Hair in the nose and around the eyes traps particles and protects the openings.
- Body hair provides a degree of insulation.
- Each follicle is associated with nerve endings, so hair movement contributes to touch sensation.

---

## The Location of Hair

Finally location, since hair covers most but not all of the body. The exceptions are worth knowing.

- Hair is found over most of the body surface.
- It is absent from the palms of the hands and soles of the feet, which are thick skin.
- It is also absent from the lips and parts of the external genitalia.
- Its type and density vary by region, age, and individual.

---

## Why Hair Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

Hair carries clinical information and contributes to sensation relevant to assessment. Each point below connects hair to practice.

- Reduced hair on the lower limbs can be a sign of impaired circulation, relevant to vascular assessment.
- The attached arrector pili muscles produce goosebumps, part of the skin's response to cold.
- Hair follicles are a source of cells that contribute to the healing of superficial wounds.
- Changes in hair can reflect systemic or nutritional problems noticed on examination.
- Understanding hair supports thorough skin and circulatory assessment.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture of hair, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to the thread spun from a buried spindle.

- It is a mistake to think the visible hair shaft is alive, because it is dead and keratinised.
- It is a mistake to believe hair grows along its length, because growth occurs at the follicle bulb.
- It is a mistake to expect hair on the palms and soles, because they are hairless thick skin.
- It is a mistake to overlook hair's contribution to touch sensation.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Describing structure, function, and location aloud is the strongest test.

- Describe the structure of a hair and its follicle from memory.
- State the functions of hair.
- State where hair is absent on the body.
- Explain why the visible hair shaft is dead.
- Relate hair to a physiotherapy-relevant sign.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have described the structure, function, and location of hair. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- A hair is a dead keratinised shaft produced by a living follicle rooted in the dermis.
- Growth occurs at the follicle bulb, not along the shaft.
- Hair protects, insulates, and, through associated nerve endings, contributes to sensation.
- It covers most of the body but is absent from the palms, soles, lips, and parts of the genitalia.
- Hair changes can signal circulatory or systemic problems relevant to assessment.
