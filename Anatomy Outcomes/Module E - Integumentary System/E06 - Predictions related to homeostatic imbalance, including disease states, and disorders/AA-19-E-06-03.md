## Describe the structure, function and location of nails

# The Structure, Function, and Location of Nails

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Describe the structure, function and location of nails.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

Nails are hard keratinised plates protecting the tips of the digits, and they are a small but informative part of the integument. Understanding their structure and function explains how they protect and assist the hands and feet, and why their appearance can signal disease. Today we describe the structure, function, and location of nails.

- By the end of this session you will be able to describe the structure of a nail.
- You will be able to state the functions of nails.
- You will be able to state where nails are located.
- You will be able to connect nails to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Hard Keratin Plates Grown From a Living Matrix

The organising idea is that a nail is a plate of hard keratin continually produced by a living matrix at its base, so the visible nail is dead while growth is hidden beneath the skin fold behind it. The matrix makes the nail; the plate itself is inert. This split between living matrix and dead plate frames the topic.

- A nail is a plate of hard keratinised cells over the tip of a digit.
- The visible nail plate is dead, while the matrix that produces it is living.
- Growth occurs at the matrix beneath the base of the nail.
- Nails protect the digit tips and assist in grip and fine tasks.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Shield Extruded From a Hidden Press

To keep nails concrete, picture a protective shield slowly extruded from a hidden press behind a wall: the shield emerging is finished and hard, while all the forming happens at the press out of sight. Damage the press and the shield forms abnormally; leave the press intact and the shield regrows. Nails work this way, and the image keeps the hidden living matrix and the visible dead plate distinct.

- The hidden press corresponds to the living nail matrix.
- The extruded finished shield corresponds to the dead keratinised nail plate.
- Regrowth after damage corresponds to a nail regrowing from an intact matrix.
- The analogy breaks down in that a press is mechanical, whereas the matrix is living tissue whose disturbance can leave lasting marks in the nail.

---

## The Structure of a Nail

Begin with structure, naming the parts of a nail. Several regions make up the whole.

- The nail plate is the visible hard part, made of tightly packed keratinised cells.
- The nail matrix, beneath the base, contains dividing cells that produce the plate.
- The lunula is the pale crescent at the base, the visible part of the matrix.
- The nail bed lies beneath the plate, and the surrounding skin folds protect its edges.

---

## The Function of Nails

Next function, which centres on protection and manipulation. Nails aid the hands and feet.

- Nails protect the sensitive tips of the fingers and toes from injury.
- They provide a firm backing that improves the precision of touch and grip.
- Fingernails assist in fine manipulation, scratching, and picking up small objects.
- Toenails protect the toes and contribute to the mechanics of the foot.

---

## The Location of Nails

Finally location, since nails are found only at the ends of the digits. Their placement is specific.

- Nails are located on the dorsal tips of the fingers and toes.
- Each nail sits over a nail bed on the last bone of the digit.
- The nail is surrounded by folds of skin at its base and sides.
- No nails are found elsewhere on the body.

---

## Why Nails Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

Nails carry clinical information and contribute to hand and foot function relevant to practice. Each point below connects nails to care.

- Nail colour and shape can signal circulatory or systemic problems noticed during examination.
- Capillary refill assessed at the nail bed gives information about peripheral circulation.
- Toenail health affects foot mechanics and comfort in gait and standing.
- Fingernail function contributes to the fine manipulation assessed in hand rehabilitation.
- Understanding nails supports thorough circulatory and hand or foot assessment.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture of nails, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to the shield extruded from a hidden press.

- It is a mistake to think the visible nail plate is alive, because it is dead and keratinised.
- It is a mistake to believe the nail grows from its free edge, because it grows from the matrix at its base.
- It is a mistake to overlook the nail bed's role in the pink appearance and capillary refill.
- It is a mistake to forget that nail changes can reflect systemic disease.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Describing structure, function, and location aloud is the strongest test.

- Describe the structure of a nail from memory.
- State the functions of nails.
- State where nails are located.
- Explain why the visible nail plate is dead.
- Relate the nail bed to a physiotherapy-relevant assessment.

## Key Takeaways

We have described the structure, function, and location of nails. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- A nail is a plate of hard keratin produced by a living matrix at its base.
- The visible plate is dead, and growth occurs at the matrix, seen as the pale lunula.
- Nails protect the digit tips and improve grip and fine manipulation.
- They are located only on the dorsal tips of the fingers and toes.
- Nail appearance and capillary refill provide clinical information in assessment.
