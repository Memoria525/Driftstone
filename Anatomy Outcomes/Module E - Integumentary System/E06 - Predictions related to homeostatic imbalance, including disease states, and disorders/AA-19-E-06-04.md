## Describe the structure, function and location of exocrine glands of the integumentary system

# The Exocrine Glands of the Integumentary System

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Describe the structure, function and location of exocrine glands of the integumentary system.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The skin contains several exocrine glands that secrete onto its surface, and these glands underlie thermoregulation, skin lubrication, and more. Understanding their structure, function, and location explains how the skin cools, protects, and maintains itself. Today we describe the exocrine glands of the integumentary system.

- By the end of this session you will be able to name the exocrine glands of the skin.
- You will be able to describe the structure and secretion of each.
- You will be able to state where each gland is located.
- You will be able to connect the glands to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Surface-Secreting Glands for Cooling and Protection

The organising idea is that the skin's exocrine glands deliver their secretions onto the surface or into follicles to cool the body, lubricate the skin, and protect it, each gland matched to its product and place. They are the skin's built-in delivery system. This idea of surface delivery for cooling and protection frames the glands.

- The skin's exocrine glands secrete onto the surface or into hair follicles.
- Sweat glands secrete watery sweat mainly for cooling.
- Sebaceous glands secrete oily sebum for lubrication and protection.
- Each gland is matched to its secretion and its location.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Building's Sprinklers and Oiling System

To keep the glands concrete, picture a building fitted with sprinklers that release water to cool it and an oiling system that lubricates its moving parts. The sprinklers cool by releasing water to the surface, and the oiling system protects by coating parts with oil. The skin's sweat and sebaceous glands play these roles, and the image keeps cooling and lubrication distinct.

- The cooling sprinklers correspond to the sweat glands releasing watery sweat.
- The oiling system corresponds to the sebaceous glands releasing oily sebum.
- Delivery to the surface corresponds to the glands secreting onto the skin or into follicles.
- The analogy breaks down in that a building's systems are plumbed in, whereas the skin's glands are living tissue that adjusts secretion to the body's needs.

---

## The Sweat Glands

Begin with the sweat glands, which are the skin's cooling system. There are two kinds.

- Eccrine sweat glands are coiled tubular glands distributed over most of the body, opening directly onto the skin surface.
- They secrete watery sweat that cools the body by evaporation, a key part of thermoregulation.
- Apocrine sweat glands are larger, located mainly in the armpits and groin, opening into hair follicles.
- They secrete a thicker sweat that, acted on by bacteria, produces body odour, and become active after puberty.

---

## The Sebaceous Glands

Next the sebaceous glands, which lubricate and protect the skin and hair. They are usually linked to follicles.

- Sebaceous glands are usually connected to hair follicles and secrete an oily substance called sebum.
- Sebum lubricates the hair and skin and helps waterproof and protect the surface.
- It has mild antimicrobial properties that help defend the skin.
- These glands are found over most of the body but are absent from the palms and soles.

---

## The Location of the Glands

Finally a summary of where the glands are found, since location matters clinically. Distribution varies by gland.

- Eccrine sweat glands are most numerous on the palms, soles, and forehead.
- Apocrine sweat glands are concentrated in the armpits and groin.
- Sebaceous glands are numerous on the face and scalp and absent from the palms and soles.
- The distribution of each gland suits its function of cooling or lubrication.

---

## Why the Skin Glands Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The skin glands underlie thermoregulation and skin integrity, both relevant to practice. Each point below connects a gland to care.

- Eccrine sweat glands drive thermoregulation during exercise, affecting exercise tolerance and fluid balance.
- Excessive or impaired sweating affects comfort and safety during activity.
- Sebaceous secretion maintains skin lubrication, relevant to skin integrity in immobile patients.
- Blocked sebaceous glands underlie common skin conditions seen on examination.
- Understanding the glands supports reasoning about thermoregulation and skin health.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture of the skin glands, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to the sprinklers and oiling system.

- It is a mistake to think all sweat glands are the same, because eccrine and apocrine glands differ in structure and location.
- It is a mistake to believe sebaceous glands secrete watery fluid, because they secrete oily sebum.
- It is a mistake to expect sebaceous glands on the palms and soles, because they are absent there.
- It is a mistake to overlook the role of eccrine sweat in thermoregulation.

## Key Takeaways

We have described the skin's exocrine glands. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The skin's exocrine glands are the eccrine and apocrine sweat glands and the sebaceous glands.
- Eccrine sweat glands cover the body and cool it by evaporation, driving thermoregulation.
- Apocrine sweat glands, in the armpits and groin, produce a thicker sweat linked to body odour.
- Sebaceous glands secrete oily sebum that lubricates and protects the skin and hair.
- The glands underlie thermoregulation and skin integrity relevant to practice.
