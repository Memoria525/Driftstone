## Trace the path of blood through the right and left sides of the heart, including its passage through the heart valves

# The Path of Blood Through the Heart

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Trace the path of blood through the right and left sides of the heart, including its passage through the heart valves.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

Blood flows through the heart in a fixed one-way sequence, through the right side to the lungs and the left side to the body, passing through valves that keep it moving forward. Tracing this path explains how the heart drives the two circulatory loops. Today we trace the path of blood through the right and left sides of the heart.

- By the end of this session you will be able to trace blood through the right side of the heart.
- You will be able to trace blood through the left side of the heart.
- You will be able to name the valves blood passes through.
- You will be able to connect the path to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: One-Way Flow Through the Right Side to the Lungs and the Left Side to the Body

The organising idea is that blood flows one way through the heart, entering the right side to be sent to the lungs and returning to the left side to be sent to the body, passing through valves that prevent backflow. Right side to lungs, left side to body. This idea of one-way flow through two sides frames the topic.

- Blood flows one way through the heart.
- The right side receives blood from the body and sends it to the lungs.
- The left side receives blood from the lungs and sends it to the body.
- Valves between and out of the chambers keep the flow moving forward.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Two-Stage Pump With One-Way Gates

To keep the path concrete, picture a two-stage pump where fluid enters the first stage, is sent to be refreshed, returns to the second stage, and is sent onward, passing through one-way gates that stop it flowing back. The heart is this two-stage pump, and the image keeps the one-way sequence in view.

- The first stage sending fluid to be refreshed corresponds to the right side sending blood to the lungs.
- The second stage sending it onward corresponds to the left side sending blood to the body.
- The one-way gates correspond to the heart valves.
- The analogy breaks down in that a pump's gates are mechanical, whereas the heart valves are moved by pressure.

---

## The Path Through the Right Side

Begin with the right side, which sends blood to the lungs. It receives deoxygenated blood.

- Deoxygenated blood returns from the body into the right atrium through the great veins.
- It passes through the right atrioventricular valve into the right ventricle.
- The right ventricle contracts and pushes the blood through the pulmonary semilunar valve.
- The blood enters the pulmonary arteries and travels to the lungs.

---

## The Path Through the Left Side

Next the left side, which sends blood to the body. It receives oxygenated blood.

- Oxygenated blood returns from the lungs into the left atrium through the pulmonary veins.
- It passes through the left atrioventricular valve into the left ventricle.
- The left ventricle contracts and pushes the blood through the aortic semilunar valve.
- The blood enters the aorta and travels to the body.

---

## The Valves Along the Path

Finally the valves, which keep the flow one way. Four valves are passed.

- The right atrioventricular valve lies between the right atrium and right ventricle.
- The pulmonary semilunar valve lies at the exit of the right ventricle.
- The left atrioventricular valve lies between the left atrium and left ventricle.
- The aortic semilunar valve lies at the exit of the left ventricle.

---

## Why This Path Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The path of blood underlies heart function and its response to exercise. Each point below connects it to practice.

- The heart drives the two circulatory loops, central to oxygen delivery and exercise tolerance.
- Valve conditions disrupt this flow, impairing the heart's pumping in patients you rehabilitate.
- Understanding the path clarifies how the heart oxygenates and distributes blood.
- The heart's output during exercise depends on this flow.
- Understanding the path grounds your reasoning about heart function.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the path, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to a two-stage pump with one-way gates.

- It is a mistake to think the right side sends blood to the body, since it sends blood to the lungs.
- It is a mistake to confuse which side receives oxygenated blood, since the left side does.
- It is a mistake to overlook the valves that keep the flow one way.
- It is a mistake to reverse the order of the chambers and valves.

## Key Takeaways

We have traced blood through the heart. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- Blood flows one way through the heart's two sides.
- Deoxygenated blood enters the right atrium, passes the right atrioventricular valve to the right ventricle, and the pulmonary valve to the lungs.
- Oxygenated blood enters the left atrium, passes the left atrioventricular valve to the left ventricle, and the aortic valve to the body.
- Four valves keep the flow moving forward.
- The heart drives the two circulatory loops central to oxygen delivery.
