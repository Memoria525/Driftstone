# The Timing of the Heart Valves and Heart Sounds

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Describe the general timing of the opening and closing of the heart valves relative to blood flow through the heart, along with the related heart sounds.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The heart valves open and close in a precise sequence that keeps blood flowing forward, and their closing produces the familiar heart sounds. Understanding the timing explains the "lub-dub" you hear and how it relates to the heartbeat. Today we describe the timing of the heart valves and the related heart sounds.

- By the end of this session you will be able to describe when the valves open and close.
- You will be able to relate valve timing to the phases of the heartbeat.
- You will be able to explain what produces the heart sounds.
- You will be able to connect this to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Valves Open and Close by Pressure to Keep Blood Moving Forward

The organising idea is that the valves open and close in response to pressure differences, opening to let blood pass forward and closing to prevent backflow, and their closing produces the heart sounds. Pressure-driven valves that make the heart sounds. This idea of pressure-timed valves frames the topic.

- The valves open and close in response to pressure differences.
- They open to let blood flow forward and close to prevent backflow.
- The atrioventricular valves and the semilunar valves close at different points in the cycle.
- The closing of the valves produces the heart sounds.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Automatic Doors That Swing Open and Slam Shut

To keep the timing concrete, picture automatic doors that swing open when pressure pushes from behind and slam shut when pressure builds in front, the slam making an audible sound. The heart valves work this way, and their closing makes the heart sounds. The image keeps the pressure-driven timing in view.

- The doors swinging open corresponds to the valves opening to let blood pass.
- The doors slamming shut corresponds to the valves closing to prevent backflow.
- The audible slam corresponds to the heart sounds.
- The analogy breaks down in that automatic doors are powered, whereas the valves are moved passively by pressure.

---

## The Timing of Valve Opening and Closing

Begin with when the valves open and close, tied to the heartbeat. Their timing keeps blood moving forward.

- When the ventricles relax in diastole, the atrioventricular valves open and blood flows from the atria into the ventricles.
- When the ventricles contract in systole, the atrioventricular valves close to prevent backflow into the atria.
- As the pressure rises further, the semilunar valves open and blood is ejected into the arteries.
- When the ventricles relax again, the semilunar valves close to prevent backflow into the ventricles.

---

## The First Heart Sound

Next the first heart sound, produced early in the cycle. It is the closing of the atrioventricular valves.

- The first heart sound, often described as "lub," is produced by the closing of the atrioventricular valves.
- This happens at the start of ventricular systole.
- It marks the beginning of the ventricles' contraction.
- It is the louder and longer of the two sounds.

---

## The Second Heart Sound

Finally the second heart sound, produced later in the cycle. It is the closing of the semilunar valves.

- The second heart sound, often described as "dub," is produced by the closing of the semilunar valves.
- This happens at the start of ventricular diastole.
- It marks the end of the ventricles' contraction.
- It is shorter and sharper than the first sound.

---

## Why Valve Timing and Heart Sounds Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The valve timing and heart sounds underlie the assessment of heart function relevant to your patients. Each point below connects them to practice.

- The heart sounds reflect the valves closing, and abnormal sounds can signal valve problems.
- Understanding the timing clarifies how the heartbeat is organised.
- Heart rate, heard as these sounds, is monitored during exercise you supervise.
- Valve conditions affect exercise tolerance in patients you rehabilitate.
- Understanding valve timing grounds your reasoning about heart function.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to automatic doors that swing open and slam shut.

- It is a mistake to think the heart sounds are made by blood, since they are made by the valves closing.
- It is a mistake to swap the two sounds, since the first is the atrioventricular valves and the second the semilunar valves.
- It is a mistake to believe the valves open and close actively, since they are moved by pressure.
- It is a mistake to overlook that valve timing keeps blood moving forward.

## Key Takeaways

We have described the timing of the valves and the heart sounds. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The valves open and close by pressure differences to keep blood moving forward.
- The atrioventricular valves open in diastole and close at the start of systole.
- The semilunar valves open during systole and close at the start of diastole.
- The first heart sound is the atrioventricular valves closing, and the second is the semilunar valves closing.
- Abnormal heart sounds can signal valve problems.
