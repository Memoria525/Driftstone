## Define the terms artery, capillary, and vein

# Defining Artery, Capillary, and Vein

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Define the terms artery, capillary, and vein.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The three basic kinds of blood vessel each play a distinct role in the circuit: carrying blood away from the heart, exchanging it with the tissues, and returning it. Defining them frames the whole study of blood vessels. Today we define the terms artery, capillary, and vein.

- By the end of this session you will be able to define artery, capillary, and vein.
- You will be able to state the role of each in the circuit.
- You will be able to explain what distinguishes them.
- You will be able to connect the terms to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Vessels Defined by Their Place in the Circuit

The organising idea is that the three vessel types are defined by their place in the circuit, with arteries carrying blood away from the heart, capillaries exchanging materials with the tissues, and veins returning blood to the heart. Away, exchange, and back. This idea of vessels defined by role frames the definitions.

- Arteries carry blood away from the heart.
- Capillaries exchange materials between the blood and the tissues.
- Veins carry blood back to the heart.
- The three are defined by their place and role in the circuit.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Outbound Roads, Delivery Addresses, and Inbound Roads

To keep the vessels concrete, picture a delivery network with outbound roads carrying goods away from the depot, individual addresses where deliveries are actually made, and inbound roads carrying vehicles back. Arteries are the outbound roads, capillaries the addresses, and veins the inbound roads, and the image keeps the three roles in view.

- The outbound roads correspond to the arteries carrying blood away from the heart.
- The delivery addresses correspond to the capillaries where exchange occurs.
- The inbound roads correspond to the veins carrying blood back.
- The analogy breaks down in that roads are fixed, whereas vessels can widen and narrow to change flow.

---

## Defining the Artery

Begin with the artery, which carries blood away from the heart. It is defined by direction.

- An artery is a blood vessel that carries blood away from the heart.
- Arteries carry blood under high pressure from the heart's pumping.
- They branch into smaller and smaller vessels as they approach the tissues.
- Most arteries carry oxygenated blood, though the pulmonary arteries are an exception.

---

## Defining the Capillary

Next the capillary, where exchange happens. It is defined by its role.

- A capillary is the smallest blood vessel, where exchange occurs.
- Capillaries have very thin walls, one cell thick.
- Across their thin walls, oxygen, nutrients, and wastes are exchanged with the tissues.
- They connect the smallest arteries to the smallest veins.

---

## Defining the Vein

Finally the vein, which carries blood back to the heart. It is defined by direction.

- A vein is a blood vessel that carries blood back toward the heart.
- Veins carry blood under lower pressure than arteries.
- They merge into larger and larger vessels as they approach the heart.
- Most veins carry deoxygenated blood, though the pulmonary veins are an exception.

---

## Why These Terms Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The three vessel types underlie circulation and its assessment relevant to your patients. Each point below connects the terms to practice.

- Arteries carry oxygen to the muscles, underlying exercise tolerance you assess.
- Capillary exchange is where oxygen and nutrients actually reach the tissues.
- Veins return blood, and their function is relevant to swelling and circulation in the limbs.
- Understanding the three clarifies how blood circulates.
- Understanding the terms grounds your reasoning about circulation.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors blur the terms, and naming them keeps them distinct. When unsure, return to outbound roads, addresses, and inbound roads.

- It is a mistake to define arteries by carrying oxygenated blood, since they are defined by carrying blood away from the heart.
- It is a mistake to think veins always carry deoxygenated blood, since the pulmonary veins carry oxygenated blood.
- It is a mistake to overlook that capillaries are where exchange occurs.
- It is a mistake to confuse the direction of arteries and veins.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Defining each term aloud is the strongest test.

- Define artery, capillary, and vein from memory.
- State the role of each in the circuit.
- Explain what distinguishes them.
- Explain the exceptions among the pulmonary vessels.
- Relate one term to a physiotherapy-relevant concern.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have defined the three vessel types. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- An artery carries blood away from the heart, usually oxygenated.
- A capillary is the smallest vessel, where exchange with the tissues occurs.
- A vein carries blood back toward the heart, usually deoxygenated.
- The pulmonary vessels reverse the usual oxygenation pattern.
- The three are defined by their place and role in the circuit.
