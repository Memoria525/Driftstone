# The Internal Structures of the Heart

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Identify and describe the structure and function of the primary internal structures of the heart, including chambers, septa, valves, papillary muscles, chordae tendineae, fibrous skeleton, and venous and arterial openings.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

Inside the heart lie the chambers, the walls between them, and the valves that keep blood flowing one way, along with the structures that anchor and support them. Understanding these internal structures explains how the heart directs blood in a single direction. Today we identify and describe the internal structures of the heart.

- By the end of this session you will be able to name the internal structures of the heart.
- You will be able to describe the septa and valves.
- You will be able to describe the papillary muscles, chordae tendineae, and fibrous skeleton.
- You will be able to connect these structures to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: One-Way Flow Enforced by Valves and Their Supports

The organising idea is that the heart's internal structures work together to enforce one-way blood flow, with septa separating the sides, valves preventing backflow, and supporting structures holding the valves in place. One-way flow enforced by valves. This idea of enforced one-way flow frames the topic.

- The heart's chambers are separated by walls called septa.
- Valves between and out of the chambers keep blood flowing one way.
- Papillary muscles and chordae tendineae anchor some valves.
- A fibrous skeleton supports the valves and separates the atria from the ventricles.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Pump With One-Way Valves and Anchored Flaps

To keep the structures concrete, picture a pump with dividing walls, one-way valves that let fluid pass in only one direction, and flaps anchored by cords so they cannot flip backward. The heart's internal structures are these walls, valves, and anchored flaps, and the image keeps the one-way flow in view.

- The dividing walls correspond to the septa.
- The one-way valves correspond to the heart valves.
- The flaps anchored by cords correspond to the valve cusps held by chordae tendineae and papillary muscles.
- The analogy breaks down in that a pump's valves are mechanical, whereas the heart's valves are living tissue moved by pressure.

---

## The Chambers and Septa

Begin with the chambers and the walls between them. The septa separate the two sides.

- The heart has four chambers, two atria and two ventricles.
- The interatrial septum separates the two atria.
- The interventricular septum separates the two ventricles.
- The septa keep the blood of the right and left sides separate.

---

## The Valves and Their Anchors

Next the valves and the structures that anchor them. The valves prevent backflow.

- The atrioventricular valves lie between the atria and ventricles and prevent backflow into the atria.
- The semilunar valves lie at the exits of the ventricles and prevent backflow into the ventricles.
- The papillary muscles are muscular projections in the ventricles.
- The chordae tendineae are cords connecting the papillary muscles to the atrioventricular valve cusps, holding them closed against high pressure.

---

## The Fibrous Skeleton and Openings

Finally the supporting skeleton and the vessel openings. These support the valves and admit and expel blood.

- The fibrous skeleton is a framework of dense connective tissue around the valves.
- It supports the valves and separates the atria from the ventricles electrically and structurally.
- Venous openings admit blood into the atria from the great veins.
- Arterial openings, guarded by the semilunar valves, let blood leave the ventricles into the great arteries.

---

## Why These Structures Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The internal structures underlie heart function and valve conditions relevant to your patients. Each point below connects them to practice.

- The valves keep blood flowing one way, and their failure, as in valve disease, impairs the heart's pumping.
- The chordae tendineae hold the valves closed, and their damage causes valve leakage.
- The fibrous skeleton supports the valves and separates the chambers electrically.
- Valve conditions affect exercise tolerance in patients you rehabilitate.
- Understanding the structures grounds your reasoning about heart function.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to a pump with one-way valves and anchored flaps.

- It is a mistake to think the valves work actively, since they are moved passively by pressure.
- It is a mistake to overlook the chordae tendineae and papillary muscles anchoring the valves.
- It is a mistake to confuse the atrioventricular and semilunar valves.
- It is a mistake to forget the fibrous skeleton's role in supporting the valves.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Naming each structure and its role aloud is the strongest test.

- Name the internal structures of the heart from memory.
- Describe the septa and valves.
- Describe the papillary muscles, chordae tendineae, and fibrous skeleton.
- Explain how the chordae tendineae keep the valves closed.
- Relate one structure to a physiotherapy-relevant concern.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have described the internal structures of the heart. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The heart's chambers are separated by the interatrial and interventricular septa.
- Atrioventricular valves and semilunar valves keep blood flowing one way.
- Papillary muscles and chordae tendineae anchor the atrioventricular valves against high pressure.
- The fibrous skeleton supports the valves and separates the atria from the ventricles.
- Together these structures enforce one-way blood flow through the heart.
