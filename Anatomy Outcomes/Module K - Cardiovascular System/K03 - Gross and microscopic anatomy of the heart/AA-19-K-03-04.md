# The External Surface of the Heart

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: On the external surface of the heart identify the 4 chambers, the coronary (atrioventricular) sulcus, anterior interventricular sulcus, posterior interventricular sulcus, apex and base.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The outside of the heart shows the four chambers and a set of grooves that mark the boundaries between them and carry the vessels that supply the heart. Learning these external features lets you read the surface of the heart. Today we identify the external features of the heart.

- By the end of this session you will be able to identify the four chambers on the surface.
- You will be able to identify the sulci and what they mark.
- You will be able to identify the apex and base.
- You will be able to connect the external features to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Chambers Marked Off by Grooves That Carry the Heart's Vessels

The organising idea is that the heart's external surface shows its four chambers separated by grooves, or sulci, that mark the internal boundaries and carry the coronary vessels. Chambers divided by vessel-carrying grooves. This idea of surface grooves frames the topic.

- The external surface of the heart shows its four chambers.
- Grooves called sulci mark the boundaries between the chambers.
- The sulci carry the coronary vessels that supply the heart.
- The heart has a broad base above and a pointed apex below.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Panels on a Casing Divided by Seams Carrying Wires

To keep the surface concrete, picture a machine casing divided into panels by seams, with the seams carrying the wiring that supplies the machine. The heart's chambers are the panels and the sulci are the seams carrying vessels, and the image keeps the surface layout in view.

- The panels correspond to the chambers on the surface.
- The seams dividing them correspond to the sulci.
- The wiring in the seams corresponds to the coronary vessels.
- The analogy breaks down in that a casing is rigid, whereas the heart's surface moves with each beat.

---

## The Four Chambers and the Base and Apex

Begin with the chambers and the overall orientation. Two atria sit above two ventricles.

- The two atria form the upper part of the heart's surface.
- The two ventricles form the larger lower part.
- The base is the broad upper part where the great vessels attach.
- The apex is the pointed lower tip, toward the lower left.

---

## The Coronary Sulcus

Next the groove between the atria and ventricles. This is the coronary sulcus.

- The coronary sulcus, also called the atrioventricular sulcus, encircles the heart between the atria and ventricles.
- It marks the boundary between the upper atria and the lower ventricles.
- It carries major coronary vessels around the heart.
- It separates the receiving chambers above from the pumping chambers below.

---

## The Interventricular Sulci

Finally the grooves between the ventricles, front and back. These are the interventricular sulci.

- The anterior interventricular sulcus runs down the front of the heart between the two ventricles.
- The posterior interventricular sulcus runs down the back between the two ventricles.
- Both mark the boundary between the right and left ventricles.
- Both carry coronary vessels that supply the ventricles.

---

## Why the External Features Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The external features underlie the heart's blood supply and examination. Each point below connects them to practice.

- The sulci carry the coronary vessels whose blockage causes heart attacks in patients you rehabilitate.
- The apex is where the heartbeat is felt, relevant to assessing the heart.
- Understanding the surface layout clarifies how the heart is organised and supplied.
- The coronary circulation in the sulci is central to cardiac conditions.
- Understanding the features grounds your reasoning about the heart.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to panels divided by seams carrying wires.

- It is a mistake to think the sulci are just grooves, since they carry the coronary vessels.
- It is a mistake to confuse the coronary sulcus with the interventricular sulci, since one separates atria from ventricles and the others separate the ventricles.
- It is a mistake to place the apex at the top, since it is the pointed lower tip.
- It is a mistake to overlook that the base is where the great vessels attach.

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Identifying the features aloud is the strongest test.

- Identify the four chambers on the surface from memory.
- Identify the sulci and what they mark.
- Identify the apex and base.
- Explain what the sulci carry.
- Relate one external feature to a physiotherapy-relevant concern.

## Key Takeaways

We have described the external surface of the heart. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The surface shows two atria above and two ventricles below.
- The coronary sulcus separates the atria from the ventricles and carries coronary vessels.
- The anterior and posterior interventricular sulci separate the two ventricles and carry coronary vessels.
- The base is the broad upper part where the great vessels attach, and the apex is the pointed lower tip.
- The sulci carry the coronary vessels whose blockage causes heart attacks.
