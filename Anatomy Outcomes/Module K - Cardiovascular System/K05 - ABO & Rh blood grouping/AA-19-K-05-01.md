# The Conduction System of the Heart

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Identify and describe the parts of the conduction system in the order of nerve impulse transmission, and explain how the system functions.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The heart generates and coordinates its own beat through a built-in electrical conduction system, so that the chambers contract in the right order. Understanding it explains how the heartbeat is timed and why disturbances cause abnormal rhythms. Today we identify and describe the parts of the conduction system in order and explain how it functions.

- By the end of this session you will be able to name the parts of the conduction system in order.
- You will be able to describe the role of each part.
- You will be able to explain how the system coordinates the heartbeat.
- You will be able to connect the conduction system to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: A Built-In Electrical Circuit That Times the Heartbeat

The organising idea is that the heart has its own electrical conduction system that generates a signal and passes it through the heart in a set sequence, so that the atria and then the ventricles contract in the right order. A built-in electrical timer. This idea of a self-timing circuit frames the topic.

- The heart has a built-in electrical conduction system.
- It generates the signal that triggers each heartbeat.
- It passes the signal through the heart in a set sequence.
- This makes the atria and then the ventricles contract in the right order.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Relay of Signal Stations Firing in Sequence

To keep the system concrete, picture a relay of signal stations, one generating a pulse and passing it to the next in a fixed order, so that the whole line responds in a coordinated sequence. The conduction system is this relay, and the image keeps the ordered firing in view.

- The station generating the pulse corresponds to the pacemaker that starts the beat.
- The pulse passing to the next stations corresponds to the signal spreading through the system.
- The coordinated sequence corresponds to the ordered contraction of the chambers.
- The analogy breaks down in that signal stations are wired by engineers, whereas the conduction system is specialised heart tissue that generates its own signal.

---

## The Pacemaker and Atrial Spread

Begin with where the signal starts and spreads through the atria. The pacemaker fires first.

- The sinoatrial node, in the wall of the right atrium, is the natural pacemaker that starts each beat.
- It generates the signal that triggers the heartbeat.
- The signal spreads across the atria, causing them to contract.
- The signal then reaches the atrioventricular node.

---

## The Delay and Passage to the Ventricles

Next the delay and the route to the ventricles. A brief delay lets the atria finish contracting.

- The atrioventricular node, between the atria and ventricles, briefly delays the signal.
- This delay lets the atria finish contracting and filling the ventricles before the ventricles contract.
- The signal then passes into a bundle of conducting fibres that runs into the ventricular septum.
- This bundle divides into branches running down the two sides of the septum.

---

## The Spread Through the Ventricles

Finally the spread through the ventricles, which triggers their contraction. Fine fibres distribute the signal.

- The bundle branches carry the signal down toward the apex of the heart.
- From there, fine conducting fibres spread the signal through the ventricle walls.
- This makes the ventricles contract from the apex upward, pushing blood toward the arteries.
- The coordinated spread ensures the ventricles contract effectively.

---

## Why the Conduction System Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The conduction system underlies the heartbeat and its disturbances relevant to your patients. Each point below connects it to practice.

- The conduction system times the heartbeat, and its disturbances cause the abnormal rhythms you may encounter.
- The heart rate set by the pacemaker is monitored during exercise you supervise.
- Understanding the system clarifies how the heartbeat is coordinated.
- Rhythm problems affect exercise tolerance and safety in cardiac rehabilitation.
- Understanding it grounds your reasoning about the heartbeat.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to a relay of signal stations firing in sequence.

- It is a mistake to think the heartbeat is triggered from outside, since the conduction system generates it within the heart.
- It is a mistake to overlook the delay at the atrioventricular node, which coordinates atrial and ventricular contraction.
- It is a mistake to muddle the order of the parts.
- It is a mistake to forget that the ventricles contract from the apex upward.

## Key Takeaways

We have described the conduction system. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The heart has a built-in electrical conduction system that times the heartbeat.
- The sinoatrial node is the pacemaker that starts each beat, spreading the signal across the atria.
- The atrioventricular node delays the signal so the atria finish contracting first.
- The signal then passes through the bundle and its branches to fine fibres that spread it through the ventricles.
- The ventricles contract from the apex upward, and disturbances cause abnormal rhythms.
