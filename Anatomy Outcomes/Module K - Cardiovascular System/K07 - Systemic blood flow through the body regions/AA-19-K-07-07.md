## Trace the blood flow to, through, and from the gastrointestinal tract and accessory digestive organs

# Blood Flow to, through, and from the Digestive Organs

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Trace the blood flow to, through, and from the gastrointestinal tract and accessory digestive organs.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The digestive organs have an unusual circulation: their blood, rich in absorbed nutrients, is routed to the liver before returning to the heart. Tracing this route explains how nutrients are processed and introduces the hepatic portal system. Today we trace the blood flow to, through, and from the gastrointestinal tract and accessory digestive organs.

- By the end of this session you will be able to trace arterial blood to the digestive organs.
- You will be able to describe exchange and absorption in the gut.
- You will be able to trace venous blood to the liver and beyond.
- You will be able to connect this route to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Nutrient-Rich Blood Routed to the Liver Before the Heart

The organising idea is that the digestive organs are supplied by branches of the aorta, and their nutrient-rich venous blood is routed through the hepatic portal system to the liver for processing before it returns to the heart. Gut to liver to heart. This idea of a liver detour frames the topic.

- The digestive organs are supplied by branches of the abdominal aorta.
- Their blood absorbs nutrients from digested food.
- This nutrient-rich blood drains into the hepatic portal system to the liver.
- After the liver processes it, the blood returns toward the heart.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Deliveries Routed Through a Processing Plant

To keep the route concrete, picture goods collected from many shops and routed through a central processing plant for checking and treatment before being sent on to the main depot. The gut's nutrient-rich blood is routed through the liver before returning to the heart, and the image keeps the processing detour in view.

- The goods collected from shops correspond to the nutrient-rich blood from the gut.
- The processing plant corresponds to the liver.
- The onward journey to the depot corresponds to the blood returning to the heart.
- The analogy breaks down in that goods are chosen for processing, whereas all the gut's blood is routed through the liver by the vessels.

---

## Tracing the Supply

Begin with the arterial supply, from the aorta. Several branches serve the gut.

- The abdominal aorta gives off branches that supply the digestive organs.
- These branches supply the stomach, intestines, and accessory organs such as the pancreas and spleen.
- The arteries branch into arterioles and capillaries within the walls of the organs.
- The liver also receives its own arterial supply for its oxygen.

---

## Absorption and Exchange in the Gut

Next what happens in the gut, the point of the route. Nutrients are absorbed here.

- In the capillaries of the gut wall, oxygen and nutrients are exchanged.
- Nutrients from digested food are absorbed into the blood.
- This makes the venous blood leaving the gut rich in nutrients.
- The blood also picks up carbon dioxide and wastes.

---

## Tracing the Drainage to the Liver and Beyond

Finally the venous drainage, through the portal system to the liver. This is the distinctive part.

- The nutrient-rich blood from the gut drains into the hepatic portal vein.
- The hepatic portal vein carries it to the liver, a second capillary bed.
- The liver processes the nutrients and removes harmful substances.
- The blood then leaves the liver through the hepatic veins into the inferior vena cava, returning to the heart.

---

## Why This Route Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The digestive circulation underlies nutrient processing and metabolism relevant to your patients. Each point below connects it to practice.

- The routing of nutrients to the liver underlies metabolism and nutrition relevant to recovery.
- During exercise, blood is redirected away from the gut toward the muscles.
- Understanding the route clarifies how nutrients are processed.
- Liver function affects metabolism and the handling of substances in your patients.
- Understanding the route grounds your reasoning about nutrient circulation.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the route, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to deliveries routed through a processing plant.

- It is a mistake to think the gut's blood returns directly to the heart, since it passes through the liver first.
- It is a mistake to overlook the hepatic portal system as a second capillary bed.
- It is a mistake to forget that the liver also has its own arterial supply.
- It is a mistake to think exchange and absorption occur anywhere but the capillaries.

## Key Takeaways

We have traced blood to, through, and from the digestive organs. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The digestive organs are supplied by branches of the abdominal aorta.
- Their venous blood absorbs nutrients and drains into the hepatic portal vein.
- The hepatic portal vein carries the blood to the liver for processing.
- The blood then leaves the liver through the hepatic veins into the inferior vena cava.
- During exercise, blood is redirected away from the gut toward the muscles.
