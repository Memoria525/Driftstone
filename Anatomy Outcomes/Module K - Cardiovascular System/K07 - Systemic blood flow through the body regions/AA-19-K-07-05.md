## Trace the blood flow to, through, and from the abdominal wall

# Blood Flow to, through, and from the Abdominal Wall

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Trace the blood flow to, through, and from the abdominal wall.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The abdominal wall, like the thoracic wall, is supplied from more than one direction and drained by matching veins. Tracing this route explains how the trunk wall is supplied and how it forms collateral connections. Today we trace the blood flow to, through, and from the abdominal wall.

- By the end of this session you will be able to trace arterial blood to the abdominal wall.
- You will be able to describe exchange in the wall.
- You will be able to trace venous blood from the wall.
- You will be able to connect this route to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Supply from Above and Below, Meeting in the Wall

The organising idea is that the abdominal wall is supplied from above by vessels continuing from the chest and from below by vessels from the pelvis, meeting in the wall to form a continuous supply. Supply from above and below. This idea of a two-ended supply frames the topic.

- The abdominal wall is supplied from more than one direction.
- Vessels from above continue down from the chest wall.
- Vessels from below ascend from the pelvic arteries.
- The two meet in the wall, forming a continuous supply.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Wall Supplied from the Roof and the Foundation

To keep the route concrete, picture a wall fed by supply lines running down from the roof and up from the foundation, meeting in the middle so the whole wall is supplied. The abdominal wall is supplied from above and below this way, and the image keeps the two-ended supply in view.

- The line from the roof corresponds to the vessels descending from the chest wall.
- The line from the foundation corresponds to the vessels ascending from the pelvis.
- The meeting in the middle corresponds to the vessels connecting in the wall.
- The analogy breaks down in that a building's lines are fixed, whereas the connecting vessels can enlarge to carry more blood.

---

## Tracing the Supply

Begin with the arterial supply, from above and below. Vessels descend and ascend into the wall.

- The upper abdominal wall is supplied by vessels continuing down from the internal thoracic artery of the chest.
- These descend behind the wall toward the navel.
- The lower abdominal wall is supplied by vessels arising from the arteries of the pelvis.
- These ascend behind the wall to meet the descending vessels.

---

## Exchange in the Wall

Next what happens in the wall, the point of the route. Exchange occurs in the capillaries.

- The arteries branch into arterioles and capillaries within the muscles and skin of the wall.
- In the capillaries, oxygen and nutrients pass to the tissues.
- Carbon dioxide and wastes pass into the blood.
- The deoxygenated blood collects into venules for the return journey.

---

## Tracing the Drainage

Finally the venous drainage, which mirrors the supply. Veins carry blood up and down.

- The veins of the upper abdominal wall drain upward toward the veins of the chest.
- The veins of the lower abdominal wall drain downward toward the veins of the pelvis.
- These veins ultimately drain toward the superior and inferior vena cavae.
- The venous drainage mirrors the two-ended arterial supply.

---

## Why This Route Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The abdominal wall route underlies the trunk wall supply relevant to your patients. Each point below connects it to practice.

- The abdominal wall muscles are part of the trunk you assess and rehabilitate.
- The connecting vessels can form collateral routes when major veins are obstructed.
- Understanding the route clarifies how the trunk wall is supplied.
- The wall's circulation is relevant to healing after abdominal surgery.
- Understanding the route grounds your reasoning about trunk circulation.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the route, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to a wall supplied from the roof and foundation.

- It is a mistake to think the abdominal wall is supplied from only one direction, since it is supplied from above and below.
- It is a mistake to overlook that the vessels connect within the wall.
- It is a mistake to forget that exchange occurs in the capillaries.
- It is a mistake to treat the venous drainage as unrelated to the arterial supply.

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Tracing the route aloud is the strongest test.

- Trace arterial blood to the abdominal wall from memory.
- Describe exchange in the wall.
- Trace venous blood from the wall.
- Explain how the wall is supplied from two directions.
- Relate the route to a physiotherapy-relevant concern.

## Key Takeaways

We have traced blood to, through, and from the abdominal wall. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The abdominal wall is supplied from above by vessels from the chest and from below by vessels from the pelvis.
- The two supplies meet in the wall, forming a continuous supply.
- Exchange occurs in the capillaries of the wall's muscles and skin.
- The veins mirror the supply, draining upward and downward toward the vena cavae.
- The connecting vessels can form collateral routes when major veins are obstructed.
