# Blood Flow to, through, and from the Pelvic Organs

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Trace the blood flow to, through, and from the pelvic organs (e.g., urinary bladder and uterus).

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The organs within the pelvis, such as the bladder and uterus, are supplied by branches of the pelvic arteries and drained by matching veins. Tracing this route explains how the pelvic organs are supplied, relevant to pelvic-health practice. Today we trace the blood flow to, through, and from the pelvic organs.

- By the end of this session you will be able to trace arterial blood to the pelvic organs.
- You will be able to describe exchange in the pelvic organs.
- You will be able to trace venous blood from them.
- You will be able to connect this route to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Branches of the Pelvic Arteries Supply the Pelvic Organs, Draining to the Inferior Vena Cava

The organising idea is that the pelvic organs are supplied by branches of the internal iliac arteries and drain through pelvic veins toward the inferior vena cava. Pelvic arteries in, pelvic veins out. This idea of a pelvic supply frames the topic.

- The pelvic organs are supplied by branches of the internal iliac arteries.
- These supply the bladder, the uterus, and other pelvic organs.
- Exchange occurs in the capillaries of these organs.
- The blood drains through pelvic veins toward the inferior vena cava.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Local Supply Grid for a District

To keep the route concrete, picture a local supply grid branching to serve the buildings of one district, with a matching drainage network carrying the used supply back to the main line. The internal iliac arteries supply the pelvic organs and the pelvic veins drain them, and the image keeps the local supply and drainage in view.

- The local supply grid corresponds to the internal iliac arteries.
- The buildings served correspond to the pelvic organs.
- The drainage network corresponds to the pelvic veins.
- The analogy breaks down in that a grid is fixed, whereas the vessels adjust flow to the organs' needs.

---

## Tracing the Supply

Begin with the arterial supply, from the internal iliac arteries. Branches serve the organs.

- The aorta divides into two common iliac arteries, which each divide into internal and external iliac arteries.
- The internal iliac arteries supply the pelvic organs.
- Their branches supply the bladder, the uterus in the female, and other pelvic structures.
- The arteries branch into arterioles and capillaries within the organs.

---

## Exchange in the Pelvic Organs

Next what happens in the organs, the point of the route. Exchange occurs in the capillaries.

- In the capillaries of the pelvic organs, oxygen and nutrients pass to the tissues.
- Carbon dioxide and wastes pass into the blood.
- The uterus receives an especially rich supply, which increases greatly in pregnancy.
- The deoxygenated blood collects into venules for the return journey.

---

## Tracing the Drainage

Finally the venous drainage, toward the inferior vena cava. Pelvic veins carry the blood back.

- The veins of the pelvic organs drain into the internal iliac veins.
- The internal iliac veins join the external iliac veins to form the common iliac veins.
- The common iliac veins join to form the inferior vena cava.
- The blood then returns up to the heart.

## Why This Route Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The pelvic circulation underlies pelvic organ function relevant to pelvic-health practice. Each point below connects it to practice.

- The pelvic organs, including the bladder and uterus, are central to pelvic-health physiotherapy.
- The uterus's rich supply increases in pregnancy, relevant to obstetric care.
- Understanding the route clarifies how the pelvic organs are supplied.
- Pelvic circulation is relevant to healing after pelvic surgery or childbirth.
- Understanding the route grounds your reasoning about pelvic circulation.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the route, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to a local supply grid for a district.

- It is a mistake to confuse the internal and external iliac arteries, since one supplies the pelvic organs and the other the leg.
- It is a mistake to overlook the uterus's rich supply that increases in pregnancy.
- It is a mistake to forget that exchange occurs in the capillaries.
- It is a mistake to route the pelvic blood anywhere but toward the inferior vena cava.

- These supply the bladder, the uterus, and other pelvic structures.
