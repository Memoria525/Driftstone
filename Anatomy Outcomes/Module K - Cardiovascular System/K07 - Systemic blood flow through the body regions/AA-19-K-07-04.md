## Trace the blood flow to, through, and from the thoracic wall

# Blood Flow to, through, and from the Thoracic Wall

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Trace the blood flow to, through, and from the thoracic wall.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

This objective is deceptively practical. Tracing a vascular route is exactly the skill you will draw on when you reason about why a rib fracture can bleed dangerously, why an intercostal drain is inserted at one edge of a space and not the other, and why the chest wall tolerates a surgeon harvesting one of its arteries for a bypass graft. The anatomy is only worth memorising if it becomes a route you can walk in your head, so today we will walk it deliberately, in the direction blood actually travels.

- By the end of this session you will trace arterial blood from the heart to any given intercostal space using the correct named vessels.
- You will describe what happens as blood passes through the microcirculation of the wall tissues.
- You will trace venous blood from the wall back to the heart, including the azygos system and its left-right asymmetry.
- You will apply this route to explain several clinical situations that physiotherapists genuinely encounter.

---

## The Big Idea: One Loop, Three Verbs

The wording of the objective is doing real work, so let us honour it. The words "to," "through," and "from" name the three phases of a single closed circuit, and if you keep them separate in your mind the whole topic organises itself. Blood is delivered *to* the wall under high pressure through arteries, it passes *through* the wall's capillary beds where the actual business of exchange happens, and it is collected *from* the wall under low pressure through veins that funnel it back toward the heart.

- The "to" phase is arterial supply, which arrives from two distinct directions rather than one.
- The "through" phase is capillary exchange, which is the only phase where oxygen, nutrients, and waste actually cross between blood and tissue.
- The "from" phase is venous drainage, which converges on one great collecting vessel on the right side of the body.
- Holding these three verbs apart is the difference between reciting vessel names and genuinely tracing a route.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Delivery-and-Collection Network

To keep the route concrete we will treat the circulation as a logistics network run from a central depot. The heart is that depot, dispatching goods out along progressively smaller roads and receiving them back along progressively larger ones. This analogy is useful precisely because it forces us to distinguish distribution, delivery, and collection, which are the same three phases the objective asks about.

- The heart is the central depot from which everything is dispatched and to which everything returns.
- Arteries are the outbound roads, starting as motorways and narrowing to local streets as they approach their destinations.
- Capillaries are the individual addresses, and they are the only point at which a delivery is actually made and a return parcel is actually collected.
- Veins are the inbound roads, merging from local collection routes into major trunk lines heading back to the depot.
- A recurring feature of a good network is redundancy, where an address can be reached by more than one route, and we will see the thoracic wall is built exactly this way.

---

## Orientation: Two Front Doors and Two Back Doors

Before tracing details, fix the overall shape in mind, because the thoracic wall is unusual in being served from two directions at once. Arterial blood reaches it both from the front, via vessels derived from the subclavian artery, and from the back, via vessels derived from the thoracic aorta. Venous blood likewise leaves by two routes, one anterior and one posterior. Everything that follows is simply detail hung on this frame.

- Anterior arterial supply arrives chiefly from the internal thoracic artery, a branch of the subclavian artery.
- Posterior arterial supply arrives chiefly from the posterior intercostal arteries of the thoracic aorta.
- Anterior venous drainage leaves via the internal thoracic veins toward the brachiocephalic veins.
- Posterior venous drainage leaves via the azygos system of veins toward the superior vena cava.

---

## Tracing "To," Part 1: Leaving the Depot

Every arterial journey to the thoracic wall begins in the same place, so we start at the left ventricle and follow the blood as far as the two dispatch routes that matter for the wall. Getting these first few branches right is what lets you connect any intercostal space back to the heart in a single unbroken chain.

- Oxygenated blood is ejected from the left ventricle into the ascending aorta and passes into the arch of the aorta.
- The arch gives off, in order, the brachiocephalic trunk, the left common carotid artery, and the left subclavian artery.
- The brachiocephalic trunk divides into the right subclavian artery and the right common carotid artery, so both subclavian arteries are now accounted for.
- Beyond the arch the aorta continues as the descending thoracic aorta against the vertebral column, which is the second dispatch route.
- From here, two roads serve the wall, namely the subclavian arteries anteriorly and the thoracic aorta posteriorly.

---

## Tracing "To," Part 2: The Anterior Route

The anterior supply is the more elegant of the two, because a single artery descends the inside of the front chest wall and sends branches into one space after another. This is also the vessel cardiac surgeons prize, so knowing its course pays off clinically as well as in the exam.

- The internal thoracic artery arises from the subclavian artery and descends just lateral to the sternum on the internal surface of the anterior wall.
- Along its course it gives anterior intercostal arteries that run forward into the upper intercostal spaces, typically the first six.
- It also gives perforating branches that pass through the wall to supply the overlying muscle, skin, and, in the female, the breast.
- Low in the thorax the internal thoracic artery ends by dividing into the musculophrenic artery, which supplies anterior intercostal branches to the next few spaces, and the superior epigastric artery, which continues into the anterior abdominal wall.

---

## Tracing "To," Part 3: The Posterior Route

The back of the wall is supplied more directly, straight from the aorta lying against the spine, with the very top two spaces handled by a small vessel from the subclavian side instead. Because the aorta sits slightly left of the midline, there is a neat asymmetry worth noticing here.

- The descending thoracic aorta gives paired posterior intercostal arteries that supply most of the spaces, typically the third down to the eleventh.
- A subcostal artery, the pair below the twelfth rib, follows the same pattern at the very bottom.
- The first and second spaces are supplied instead by the supreme intercostal artery, which descends from the costocervical trunk of the subclavian artery.
- Because the aorta lies to the left of the midline, the right posterior intercostal arteries are longer and must cross the front of the vertebral bodies to reach the right-sided spaces.

---

## Tracing "To," Part 4: The Ring and the Bundle

Now the two routes meet, and this is the single most clinically important idea in the arterial half of the topic. In each intercostal space the anterior and posterior supplies join, so that every space is fed from both ends like a street reachable from either direction. This redundancy is what allows collateral circulation to develop, and the exact packing of the vessels within the space is what makes chest procedures safe or unsafe.

- In each space the anterior intercostal artery and the posterior intercostal artery anastomose, forming a continuous arterial ring around the thorax at that level.
- This anastomosis means blood can still reach a space from one direction if the other is obstructed, which is the basis of collateral circulation.
- Within the space the main neurovascular bundle runs in the costal groove near the lower border of each rib, ordered from top to bottom as vein, artery, and nerve, a sequence remembered as VAN.
- The bundle runs between the internal intercostal and innermost intercostal muscles, which is why a needle or drain is passed close to the upper border of the lower rib to stay clear of the main bundle above.

---

## Tracing "Through": The Point of the Whole Journey

Everything so far has been transport, and none of it matters until we reach the only phase where blood does its actual work. In the microcirculation of the wall the roads finally reach their addresses, deliveries are made, and return parcels are collected. This is where oxygen leaves the blood and carbon dioxide joins it.

- The intercostal and internal thoracic arteries branch into arterioles that regulate flow into the tissues.
- The arterioles feed capillary beds within the skin, the intercostal and pectoral muscles, the periosteum of the ribs, and the parietal pleura lining the inner wall.
- Across the thin capillary walls, oxygen and nutrients pass to the tissues while carbon dioxide and metabolic waste pass into the blood.
- The now-deoxygenated blood collects into venules, which are the first inbound roads of the return journey.

---

## Tracing "From," Part 1: The Anterior Return

The venous return mirrors the arterial supply in having an anterior and a posterior route, and the anterior route is the simpler of the two. It essentially retraces the internal thoracic artery in reverse, heading up toward the great veins at the root of the neck.

- Anterior intercostal veins from the upper spaces drain into the internal thoracic veins that accompany the artery of the same name.
- The internal thoracic veins ascend and empty into the brachiocephalic veins.
- The brachiocephalic veins unite to form the superior vena cava, so the anterior route reaches the heart by this path.
- This anterior return handles only part of the wall, and the larger share travels by the posterior route we consider next.

---

## Tracing "From," Part 2: The Azygos System

The posterior venous drainage is the part students find hardest, because it is asymmetrical and its naming is unfamiliar. The key is to realise that almost everything eventually funnels onto one vessel, the azygos vein, which runs up the right side of the vertebral column. The left side simply has to cross the midline to reach it.

- On the right, most posterior intercostal veins drain directly into the azygos vein, with the upper spaces joining via the right superior intercostal vein.
- On the left, the lower posterior intercostal veins drain into the hemiazygos vein and the middle spaces into the accessory hemiazygos vein.
- Both the hemiazygos and accessory hemiazygos veins cross the midline behind the aorta and oesophagus to empty into the azygos vein.
- The very highest spaces on each side drain upward into the brachiocephalic veins rather than into the azygos system, via the superior intercostal veins.
- The exact space each vein serves varies between individuals, so it is the pattern of right-sided collection and leftward crossover that you should carry, not rigid numbers.

---

## Tracing "From," Part 3: Closing the Loop

The return journey ends where the outward journey began, at the depot. Following the azygos vein to its termination completes the circuit and lets you state the whole route in one breath, which is exactly what the objective asks for.

- The azygos vein ascends on the right of the vertebral column and arches forward over the root of the right lung.
- It empties into the superior vena cava, joining the blood that returned by the anterior and brachiocephalic routes.
- The superior vena cava delivers this blood into the right atrium, and the wall's circuit is complete.
- From the right atrium the blood passes to the right ventricle and through the pulmonary circulation to be reoxygenated before the whole loop can begin again.

---

## Why This Route Matters in Practice

The payoff of tracing the route carefully is that several clinical situations you will meet stop being facts to memorise and become predictable consequences of the anatomy. Each of the following follows directly from the path we have just walked.

- Because the main neurovascular bundle sits in the costal groove of the rib above each space, an intercostal drain is inserted just over the top of the rib below, and getting this wrong risks the vessel and nerve.
- Because the intercostal arteries carry systemic arterial pressure, a displaced rib fracture that tears one can produce a significant haemothorax.
- Because the internal thoracic artery has robust collateral partners, a cardiac surgeon can harvest it as a bypass graft without starving the chest wall, which is relevant to the patients you rehabilitate after surgery.
- Because the anastomotic ring can enlarge over time, a coarctation of the aorta drives blood through hypertrophied intercostal arteries and can erode the ribs, producing the notching seen on a chest radiograph.
- Because the azygos system connects the superior and inferior venous territories, it can act as a collateral bypass when a major central vein is obstructed.

---

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

A few predictable errors trip students on this objective, and naming them now makes them easier to sidestep. If you notice yourself doing any of these, return to the two-front-doors frame and the three verbs.

- It is a mistake to forget that the wall is supplied from two directions, because omitting either the internal thoracic or the aortic route leaves your trace incomplete.
- It is a mistake to treat the venous side as a simple mirror of the arterial side, because the azygos system is asymmetrical in a way the arteries are not.
- It is a mistake to memorise fixed space numbers as if they were universal, because the boundaries between vessels vary and the pattern is what matters.
- It is a mistake to skip the "through" phase, because exchange at the capillaries is the entire biological purpose of the route.

---

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Tracing is a skill, and skills consolidate through retrieval rather than rereading, so use these prompts to walk the route yourself with the slides closed. Saying the route aloud to a colleague is the strongest test, because any gap in the chain will reveal itself the moment you have to speak it.

- From memory, trace arterial blood from the left ventricle to a fifth left intercostal space, naming every vessel in order.
- Explain in one sentence what happens as that blood passes through the capillary bed of the intercostal muscle.
- Trace the venous return from a ninth left intercostal space all the way to the right atrium, including any midline crossover.
- Explain to a peer why an intercostal drain is inserted at the lower part of a space rather than the upper part.
- State how the anastomotic ring accounts for rib notching in coarctation of the aorta.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have walked one closed loop in three phases, and if the detail fades, these anchors will let you reconstruct the rest. The objective asks you to trace a route, so the final test of your learning is simply whether you can narrate that route without stopping.

- Blood reaches the thoracic wall from two directions, anteriorly from the internal thoracic artery and posteriorly from the thoracic aorta, and these meet as an anastomotic ring in each space.
- The main neurovascular bundle lies in the costal groove near the lower border of each rib, ordered vein, artery, nerve, with direct consequences for chest procedures.
- Exchange happens only in the capillary beds of the wall, which is the true purpose of the whole route.
- Blood leaves the wall anteriorly via the internal thoracic veins and posteriorly via the azygos system, with the left side crossing the midline to reach the azygos vein on the right.
- The entire return converges on the superior vena cava and the right atrium, closing a single loop that you should be able to trace unbroken from heart to wall and back.
