## Trace the blood flow to, through, and from the head and neck

# Blood Flow to, through, and from the Head and Neck

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Trace the blood flow to, through, and from the head and neck.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The head and neck, including the brain, receive a rich blood supply and drain it back through the neck. Tracing this route explains how the brain is supplied and why neck vessel disease can cause strokes. Today we trace the blood flow to, through, and from the head and neck.

- By the end of this session you will be able to trace arterial blood to the head and neck.
- You will be able to describe exchange in the head and neck.
- You will be able to trace venous blood from the head and neck.
- You will be able to connect this route to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Supply from the Carotid and Vertebral Arteries, Drainage by the Jugular Veins

The organising idea is that the head and neck are supplied mainly by the carotid and vertebral arteries and drained mainly by the jugular veins, forming one closed route to and from the head. Carotids and vertebrals in, jugulars out. This idea of a head-and-neck route frames the topic.

- Blood reaches the head and neck mainly through the carotid and vertebral arteries.
- It exchanges materials in the tissues of the head and neck, including the brain.
- It drains back mainly through the jugular veins.
- The route forms a closed circuit to and from the head.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Supply Lines Up a Tower and Drains Back Down

To keep the route concrete, picture supply lines running up a tower to serve its upper floors and drains carrying the used supply back down. The carotid and vertebral arteries run up to the head and the jugular veins drain back down, and the image keeps the up-and-down route in view.

- The supply lines up the tower correspond to the carotid and vertebral arteries.
- The service of the upper floors corresponds to exchange in the head and neck.
- The drains back down correspond to the jugular veins.
- The analogy breaks down in that a tower's plumbing is fixed, whereas the arteries adjust flow to the brain's needs.

---

## Tracing the Supply

Begin with the arterial supply, from the arch to the head. Two sets of arteries supply the head.

- The common carotid arteries ascend the neck and divide into internal and external carotid arteries.
- The internal carotid arteries supply much of the brain, and the external carotid arteries supply the face and scalp.
- The vertebral arteries, arising from the subclavian arteries, ascend through the neck bones to supply the back of the brain.
- Together the carotid and vertebral arteries supply the whole head and neck.

---

## Exchange in the Head and Neck

Next what happens in the tissues, the point of the route. Exchange occurs in the capillaries.

- The arteries branch into arterioles and then capillaries within the tissues of the head and neck.
- In the capillaries, oxygen and nutrients pass to the tissues, including the brain.
- Carbon dioxide and wastes pass into the blood.
- The now-deoxygenated blood collects into venules for the return journey.

---

## Tracing the Drainage

Finally the venous drainage, back to the great veins. The jugular veins carry blood down.

- The internal jugular veins drain blood from the brain and much of the head.
- The external jugular veins drain the more superficial parts of the head and neck.
- The jugular veins join the veins from the arms to form the great veins entering the superior vena cava.
- The blood then returns to the right atrium.

---

## Why This Route Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The head and neck route underlies the brain's supply and stroke risk relevant to your patients. Each point below connects it to practice.

- The carotid arteries supply the brain, and their disease can cause strokes in patients you rehabilitate.
- The pulse in the carotid artery is felt in the neck during assessment.
- Understanding the route clarifies how the brain is supplied and drained.
- Neck vessel problems affect the brain, relevant to neurological conditions.
- Understanding the route grounds your reasoning about the brain's circulation.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the route, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to supply lines up a tower and drains back down.

- It is a mistake to think only the carotid arteries supply the brain, since the vertebral arteries supply the back of it.
- It is a mistake to confuse the internal and external carotid arteries, since one supplies the brain and the other the face.
- It is a mistake to overlook the jugular veins draining the head.
- It is a mistake to forget that exchange occurs in the capillaries.

## Key Takeaways

We have traced blood to, through, and from the head and neck. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The carotid and vertebral arteries supply the head and neck.
- The internal carotid and vertebral arteries supply the brain, and the external carotid supplies the face and scalp.
- Exchange occurs in the capillaries of the head and neck tissues.
- The jugular veins drain the head and neck back toward the superior vena cava.
- Carotid artery disease can cause strokes, central to some patients.
