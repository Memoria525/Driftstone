## Trace the blood flow to, through, and from the upper limb

# Blood Flow to, through, and from the Upper Limb

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Trace the blood flow to, through, and from the upper limb.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The arm receives a single main artery that changes name along its length and is drained by both deep and superficial veins. Tracing this route explains the pulses you feel and the veins used for blood samples. Today we trace the blood flow to, through, and from the upper limb.

- By the end of this session you will be able to trace arterial blood down the arm.
- You will be able to describe exchange in the arm.
- You will be able to trace venous blood back from the arm.
- You will be able to connect this route to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: One Main Artery Down the Arm, Drained by Deep and Superficial Veins

The organising idea is that the upper limb is supplied by one main artery that changes name from the subclavian to the axillary to the brachial and then divides at the elbow, and is drained by deep veins alongside the arteries and superficial veins in the skin. One main artery, two sets of veins. This idea of a single supply and dual drainage frames the topic.

- The upper limb is supplied by one main artery that changes name along its course.
- It runs from the subclavian artery through the arm to the hand.
- Exchange occurs in the capillaries of the arm.
- The arm is drained by deep veins alongside the arteries and superficial veins in the skin.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Single Trunk Road With Named Sections and Two Return Routes

To keep the route concrete, picture a single trunk road that takes a new name in each district as it runs down a peninsula, served by a deep return route alongside it and a separate surface route. The arm's artery changes name along its course, and the arm has deep and superficial veins, and the image keeps the single supply and dual drainage in view.

- The single trunk road with named sections corresponds to the artery changing name along the arm.
- The deep return route alongside corresponds to the deep veins with the arteries.
- The separate surface route corresponds to the superficial veins.
- The analogy breaks down in that a road is fixed, whereas the vessels adjust flow to the arm's needs.

---

## Tracing the Supply

Begin with the arterial supply, down the arm. The main artery changes name.

- The subclavian artery becomes the axillary artery as it passes into the armpit.
- The axillary artery becomes the brachial artery in the upper arm.
- The brachial artery runs down the arm and divides at the elbow into the radial and ulnar arteries.
- The radial and ulnar arteries supply the forearm and hand.

---

## Exchange in the Arm

Next what happens in the arm, the point of the route. Exchange occurs in the capillaries.

- The arteries branch into arterioles and capillaries within the muscles and skin of the arm.
- In the capillaries, oxygen and nutrients pass to the tissues.
- Carbon dioxide and wastes pass into the blood.
- The deoxygenated blood collects into venules for the return journey.

---

## Tracing the Drainage

Finally the venous drainage, back toward the heart. Deep and superficial veins carry the blood.

- Deep veins run alongside the arteries and carry much of the blood back.
- Superficial veins run in the skin of the arm and are visible on the surface.
- The deep and superficial veins join and drain into the axillary and then the subclavian vein.
- The subclavian vein carries the blood toward the superior vena cava.

---

## Why This Route Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The upper limb circulation underlies the arm you assess and rehabilitate. Each point below connects it to practice.

- The pulse in the radial and brachial arteries is felt during assessment of the arm.
- The superficial veins of the arm are used for blood samples and drips in your patients.
- Understanding the route clarifies how the arm is supplied and drained.
- Muscle contraction aids the venous return from the arm, relevant to movement and swelling.
- Understanding the route grounds your reasoning about arm circulation.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the route, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to a single trunk road with named sections.

- It is a mistake to think there are several main arteries in the arm, since one main artery changes name along its course.
- It is a mistake to overlook that the arm has both deep and superficial veins.
- It is a mistake to forget that the brachial artery divides at the elbow.
- It is a mistake to think exchange occurs anywhere but the capillaries.

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Tracing the route aloud is the strongest test.

- Trace arterial blood down the arm from memory.
- Describe exchange in the arm.

## Key Takeaways

We have traced blood to, through, and from the upper limb. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- One main artery supplies the arm, changing name from subclavian to axillary to brachial.
- The brachial artery divides at the elbow into the radial and ulnar arteries.
- Exchange occurs in the capillaries of the arm.
- Deep veins alongside the arteries and superficial veins in the skin drain the arm.
- The blood drains through the axillary and subclavian veins toward the superior vena cava.
