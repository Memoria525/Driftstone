## Describe the role of the placenta, umbilical vessels, ductus venosus, foramen ovale, and ductus arteriosus in fetal circulation

# The Special Structures of Fetal Circulation

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Describe the role of the placenta, umbilical vessels, ductus venosus, foramen ovale, and ductus arteriosus in fetal circulation.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

Before birth, the fetus gets its oxygen from the placenta, not the lungs, so its circulation has special structures that route blood around the lungs and to and from the placenta. Understanding them explains how the fetus is supplied and prepares you for the changes at birth. Today we describe the roles of the placenta, umbilical vessels, ductus venosus, foramen ovale, and ductus arteriosus.

- By the end of this session you will be able to describe the role of the placenta and umbilical vessels.
- You will be able to describe the ductus venosus.
- You will be able to describe the foramen ovale and ductus arteriosus.
- You will be able to connect fetal circulation to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: Special Structures That Use the Placenta and Bypass the Lungs

The organising idea is that fetal circulation uses the placenta for oxygen and has special structures that route blood to and from the placenta and bypass the non-functioning lungs. Placenta for oxygen, shortcuts past the lungs. This idea of placental supply with lung bypasses frames the topic.

- The fetus gets oxygen from the placenta, not the lungs.
- Umbilical vessels carry blood to and from the placenta.
- The ductus venosus routes blood past the liver.
- The foramen ovale and ductus arteriosus route blood past the lungs.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Temporary Bypass Pipes on a System Not Yet in Use

To keep the structures concrete, picture a building drawing its supply from an external source while its own internal plant is not yet running, with temporary bypass pipes routing flow around the idle plant. The fetus uses the placenta and bypasses the idle lungs this way, and the image keeps the temporary bypasses in view.

- The external supply source corresponds to the placenta providing oxygen.
- The idle internal plant corresponds to the non-functioning lungs.
- The temporary bypass pipes correspond to the foramen ovale and ductus arteriosus.
- The analogy breaks down in that bypass pipes are removed by hand, whereas the fetal structures close on their own after birth.

---

## The Placenta and Umbilical Vessels

Begin with the source of oxygen and the vessels to it. The placenta supplies the fetus.

- The placenta is where the fetal blood picks up oxygen and nutrients from the mother.
- The umbilical vein carries oxygen-rich blood from the placenta to the fetus.
- The umbilical arteries carry oxygen-poor blood from the fetus back to the placenta.
- The placenta replaces the role the lungs and gut will take after birth.

---

## The Ductus Venosus

Next the structure that bypasses the liver. This is the ductus venosus.

- The ductus venosus is a vessel that routes much of the blood from the umbilical vein past the liver.
- It carries the oxygen-rich blood toward the fetal heart.
- This lets the oxygen-rich blood reach the heart quickly without passing fully through the liver.
- It is a temporary fetal structure.

---

## The Foramen Ovale and Ductus Arteriosus

Finally the two structures that bypass the lungs. These route blood around the idle lungs.

- The foramen ovale is an opening between the two atria that lets blood pass from the right atrium to the left, bypassing the lungs.
- The ductus arteriosus is a vessel connecting the pulmonary artery to the aorta, routing blood past the lungs.
- Both send blood around the non-functioning fetal lungs.
- They ensure oxygen-rich blood reaches the body rather than the idle lungs.

---

## Why Fetal Circulation Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

Fetal circulation underlies development and congenital conditions relevant to paediatric practice. Each point below connects it to practice.

- The special structures normally close after birth, and their failure to close causes congenital heart conditions.
- Understanding fetal circulation grounds the study of paediatric cardiac conditions.
- The changes at birth are essential to understanding newborn circulation.
- Congenital heart conditions affect children you may treat.
- Understanding fetal circulation grounds your reasoning about development.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to temporary bypass pipes on an idle plant.

- It is a mistake to think the fetus gets oxygen from its lungs, since it gets it from the placenta.
- It is a mistake to confuse the umbilical vein and arteries, since the vein carries oxygen-rich blood.
- It is a mistake to overlook that the foramen ovale and ductus arteriosus bypass the lungs.
- It is a mistake to think these structures are permanent, since they normally close after birth.

## Key Takeaways

We have described the special structures of fetal circulation. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- The fetus gets oxygen from the placenta, not the lungs.
- The umbilical vein carries oxygen-rich blood to the fetus and the umbilical arteries carry it back.
- The ductus venosus routes blood past the liver.
- The foramen ovale and ductus arteriosus route blood past the non-functioning lungs.
- These structures normally close after birth, and their failure causes congenital conditions.
