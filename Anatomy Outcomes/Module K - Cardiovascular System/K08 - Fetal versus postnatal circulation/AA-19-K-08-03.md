# The Changes in Fetal Circulation at Birth

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Describe the changes in major fetal cardiovascular structures that typically occur beginning at birth, and the ultimate postnatal remnants of these structures.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

At birth, the newborn takes its first breath and its circulation switches from relying on the placenta to using its own lungs, so the special fetal structures close and leave behind small remnants. Understanding these changes explains how the newborn circulation is established. Today we describe the changes in the major fetal structures at birth and their remnants.

- By the end of this session you will be able to describe why the fetal structures close at birth.
- You will be able to describe the change in each structure.
- You will be able to name the remnant of each.
- You will be able to connect these changes to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: The Lungs Take Over, So the Bypasses Close and Leave Remnants

The organising idea is that at birth the lungs begin to work and the placenta is lost, so the fetal bypass structures are no longer needed, close over, and leave small remnants. Lungs take over, bypasses close. This idea of a switch to the lungs frames the topic.

- At birth the lungs begin to function and the placenta is lost.
- The fetal structures that bypassed the lungs and served the placenta are no longer needed.
- They close over in the days to weeks after birth.
- Each leaves a small remnant in the adult.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Decommissioning Temporary Bypasses When the Plant Starts

To keep the changes concrete, picture a building whose own plant finally starts running, so the temporary external supply is disconnected and the bypass pipes are sealed, leaving only capped stubs behind. The newborn's lungs start, the placenta is disconnected, and the bypasses seal into remnants, and the image keeps the decommissioning in view.

- The plant starting corresponds to the lungs beginning to function.
- Disconnecting the external supply corresponds to losing the placenta.
- Sealing the bypass pipes into capped stubs corresponds to the structures closing into remnants.
- The analogy breaks down in that pipes are sealed by workers, whereas the fetal structures close through the body's own responses.

---

## Why the Structures Close

Begin with why the change happens, since it drives everything. The lungs and the loss of the placenta trigger it.

- At birth, the newborn takes its first breaths and the lungs expand and begin to work.
- Blood now flows through the lungs to be oxygenated, so the lung bypasses are no longer needed.
- The placenta is lost when the umbilical cord is cut, so the placental vessels are no longer needed.
- These changes trigger the closure of the fetal structures.

---

## The Changes in Each Structure

Next the change in each structure, since each closes in its own way. The bypasses and placental vessels shut down.

- The foramen ovale closes as the pressure in the left atrium rises, sealing the opening between the atria.
- The ductus arteriosus constricts and closes, so blood no longer bypasses the lungs.
- The ductus venosus closes, so blood no longer bypasses the liver.
- The umbilical vessels close, no longer needed once the placenta is lost.

---

## The Remnants

Finally the remnants each leaves, since they persist in the adult. Each becomes a small structure or ligament.

- The closed foramen ovale leaves a small depression in the wall between the atria.
- The closed ductus arteriosus becomes a small ligament.
- The closed ductus venosus becomes a small ligament in the liver.
- The closed umbilical vessels become ligaments in the abdominal wall and around the bladder.

---

## Why These Changes Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The changes at birth underlie newborn circulation and congenital conditions relevant to paediatric practice. Each point below connects them to practice.

- Failure of these structures to close causes congenital heart conditions in children you may treat.
- Understanding the changes grounds the study of newborn and paediatric circulation.
- The switch to the lungs establishes the adult pattern of circulation.
- Congenital conditions from incomplete closure affect exercise tolerance in children.
- Understanding the changes grounds your reasoning about newborn circulation.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort the picture, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to decommissioning temporary bypasses when the plant starts.

- It is a mistake to think the fetal structures disappear entirely, since each leaves a remnant.
- It is a mistake to overlook that the lungs beginning to work triggers the changes.
- It is a mistake to forget that failure to close causes congenital conditions.
- It is a mistake to think the changes are instant, since some take days to weeks.

## Key Takeaways

We have described the changes in fetal circulation at birth. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- At birth the lungs begin to work and the placenta is lost, so the fetal bypasses are no longer needed.
- The foramen ovale and ductus arteriosus close, so blood no longer bypasses the lungs.
- The ductus venosus and umbilical vessels close as well.
- Each structure leaves a small remnant, such as a depression in the atrial wall or a ligament.
- Failure of these structures to close causes congenital heart conditions.
