## Identify the cells of nervous tissue

# The Cells of Nervous Tissue

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Identify the cells of nervous tissue.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

Nervous tissue governs the control of movement and the experience of sensation and pain, so identifying its cells is foundational for a physiotherapist. The tissue contains two broad families of cell, the signalling neurons and their supporting glia. Today we identify the cells of nervous tissue and what each contributes.

- By the end of this session you will be able to name the two families of nervous tissue cell.
- You will be able to identify the neuron and describe its basic parts.
- You will be able to name the main types of glial cell.
- You will be able to explain why both families are needed for nervous tissue to work.

---

## The Big Idea: Signalling Cells Supported by a Larger Cast

The organising idea is that nervous tissue is built from neurons, the cells that actually signal, and a larger number of supporting glial cells that make signalling possible. The neurons get the attention, but they cannot function without the glia. Recognising this partnership frames the whole tissue.

- Nervous tissue contains two families of cell, neurons and glial cells.
- Neurons are the excitable cells that generate and transmit signals.
- Glial cells support, protect, and maintain the neurons.
- Both families are essential, and glia actually outnumber neurons.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Performers and Stage Crew

To keep the two families concrete, picture a theatre where the performers deliver the show while a larger stage crew builds the set, runs the lighting, and keeps everything working behind the scenes. The show fails without either. Neurons are the performers and glia are the crew, and the image keeps their partnership in view.

- The performers delivering the show correspond to the signalling neurons.
- The larger stage crew correspond to the supporting glial cells.
- The crew enabling the performance corresponds to glia enabling neuronal signalling.
- The analogy breaks down in that a stage crew merely supports the performers, whereas glia also actively shape signalling, insulate connections, and defend the tissue.

---

## The Neuron

Begin with the neuron, the signalling cell that defines the tissue's function. It has a characteristic three-part structure.

- A neuron is the excitable cell that generates and conducts electrical signals.
- Its cell body contains the nucleus and most organelles and integrates incoming signals.
- Its dendrites are branching extensions that receive signals from other cells.
- Its axon is a long extension that conducts the signal away toward other cells.

---

## The Glial Cells of the Central Nervous System

Next the glia of the brain and spinal cord, which support the neurons there. Several types share the work.

- Astrocytes support neurons, regulate their chemical environment, and contribute to the blood-brain barrier.
- Oligodendrocytes wrap axons in insulating myelin within the central nervous system.
- Microglia act as the immune defence of the nervous tissue, clearing debris and pathogens.
- Ependymal cells line the fluid-filled spaces and help circulate cerebrospinal fluid.

---

## The Glial Cells of the Peripheral Nervous System

Finally the glia of the peripheral nerves, which support neurons outside the brain and cord. Two types are key.

- Schwann cells wrap peripheral axons in myelin, insulating them for faster conduction.
- Schwann cells also guide the regrowth of peripheral axons after injury.
- Satellite cells surround and support neuron cell bodies in peripheral ganglia.
- The peripheral glia are central to why peripheral nerves can regenerate while central ones largely cannot.

---

## Why Identifying These Cells Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

The cells of nervous tissue underlie movement control, sensation, and nerve recovery, all central to practice. Each point below connects a cell to care.

- Neurons are the cells whose signalling produces the movement and sensation you assess and rehabilitate.
- Myelinating glia determine conduction speed, and their loss underlies conditions such as demyelinating disease.
- Schwann cells guide peripheral nerve regeneration, explaining recovery after peripheral nerve injury.
- Microglial activity in inflammation is relevant to the nervous system's response to injury and pain.
- Understanding these cells grounds your reasoning in neurological rehabilitation.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors cloud the picture of nervous tissue, and naming them keeps it accurate. When unsure, return to performers and stage crew.

- It is a mistake to think nervous tissue is only neurons, because glia outnumber them and are essential.
- It is a mistake to treat glia as passive packing, because they insulate, defend, and regulate.
- It is a mistake to assume the same glia myelinate everywhere, because oligodendrocytes serve the centre and Schwann cells the periphery.
- It is a mistake to overlook Schwann cells, because they explain peripheral nerve regeneration.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Naming each cell and its role aloud is the strongest test.

- Name the two families of nervous tissue cell from memory.
- Describe the three basic parts of a neuron.
- Name the central glial cells and the role of each.
- Name the peripheral glial cells and the role of each.
- Explain why peripheral nerves regenerate better than central ones.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have identified the cells that make up nervous tissue. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- Nervous tissue contains signalling neurons and supporting glial cells, with glia outnumbering neurons.
- A neuron has a cell body, dendrites that receive, and an axon that conducts signals away.
- Central glia are astrocytes, oligodendrocytes, microglia, and ependymal cells.
- Peripheral glia are Schwann cells, which myelinate and guide regeneration, and satellite cells.
- Both families are essential, and the glia underlie conduction speed and nerve recovery.
