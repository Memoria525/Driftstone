## Classify different types of epithelial tissues based on structural characteristics

# Classifying Epithelial Tissues by Structure

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Classify different types of epithelial tissues based on structural characteristics.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The many kinds of epithelium can seem an overwhelming list, but they are named by a simple two-part system that describes their structure. Once you know the system, you can name almost any epithelium from what you see, rather than memorising each. Today we learn the classification of epithelia by the number of cell layers and the shape of the surface cells.

- By the end of this session you will be able to state the two structural features used to classify epithelia.
- You will be able to classify epithelia by the number of layers.
- You will be able to classify epithelia by cell shape.
- You will be able to combine the two to name an epithelium from its structure.

---

## The Big Idea: A Two-Word Naming System

The organising idea is that every epithelium's name is built from two structural descriptions, one giving the number of cell layers and one giving the shape of the surface cells. Combining these two words names the tissue and, better still, predicts its function. Learning the naming system replaces memorising a long list.

- Epithelia are classified by two structural features combined into a name.
- The first feature is the number of cell layers.
- The second feature is the shape of the surface cells.
- Combining the two gives both the name and a clue to the function.

---

## Our Master Analogy: Describing a Building by Storeys and Roof Shape

To keep the system concrete, picture describing buildings by two features: how many storeys they have and the shape of their rooftop. A single-storey flat-roofed building differs from a multi-storey pitched-roof one, and the two descriptors together identify the type. Epithelia are named the same way, by layers and cell shape, and the building image keeps the two-part system in view.

- The number of storeys corresponds to the number of cell layers.
- The rooftop shape corresponds to the shape of the surface cells.
- Combining the two descriptors names the building, as it names the epithelium.
- The analogy breaks down in that a building's storeys are identical in purpose, whereas an epithelium's layers may differ, and only the surface cell shape is used for naming.

---

## Classifying by the Number of Layers

Begin with the first descriptor, the number of layers, because it distinguishes the two broad families. Layers relate to whether the tissue protects or exchanges.

- A simple epithelium has a single layer of cells, all resting on the basement membrane.
- A stratified epithelium has two or more layers of cells stacked on one another.
- Simple epithelia suit functions requiring thinness, such as absorption and diffusion.
- Stratified epithelia suit functions requiring protection, since extra layers resist wear.

---

## Classifying by Cell Shape

Next the second descriptor, the shape of the surface cells, which relates to the tissue's specific role. Three basic shapes are recognised.

- Squamous cells are flat and scale-like, suited to rapid diffusion and smooth surfaces.
- Cuboidal cells are roughly cube-shaped, suited to secretion and absorption.
- Columnar cells are tall and column-like, suited to absorption and secretion where more cell machinery is needed.
- In stratified epithelia, the shape used for naming is that of the surface cells, not the deeper ones.

---

## Combining the Two and Special Cases

Now combine the descriptors to name a tissue, and note a couple of special arrangements. The combination is the practical skill.

- Combining layer and shape gives names such as simple squamous, simple cuboidal, and stratified squamous.
- Pseudostratified epithelium appears layered because its nuclei sit at different heights, but all cells touch the basement membrane, so it is truly simple.
- Transitional epithelium has cells that change shape as the tissue stretches, suiting organs that expand.
- Naming from structure lets you predict function, since thin simple epithelia exchange and thick stratified ones protect.

---

## Why This Classification Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

Classifying epithelia helps you understand the surfaces and linings relevant to healing and to cardiorespiratory and continence care. Each point below connects the system to practice.

- Stratified squamous epithelium forms the protective surface of the skin you help heal and protect.
- Simple squamous epithelium lines the air sacs of the lungs, where its thinness allows gas exchange central to respiratory care.
- Transitional epithelium lines the bladder, allowing it to stretch, which is relevant to continence and pelvic-health practice.
- Recognising that thin epithelia exchange and thick ones protect explains the vulnerability of different surfaces.
- The classification grounds your reading of tissue structure in healing and disease.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors cloud the system, and naming them keeps it reliable. When unsure, return to describing buildings by storeys and roof shape.

- It is a mistake to name a stratified epithelium by its deep cells, because the surface cell shape is used.
- It is a mistake to think pseudostratified epithelium is truly layered, because all its cells reach the basement membrane.
- It is a mistake to assume more layers always means better, because thinness is required for exchange.
- It is a mistake to overlook transitional epithelium, because its stretching role is distinctive.

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Naming epithelia from structure aloud is the strongest test.

- State the two structural features used to classify epithelia.
- Classify epithelia by number of layers and give the function of each family.
- Name the three cell shapes and their suited functions.
- Explain why pseudostratified epithelium is classed as simple.
- Name the epithelium lining the lung air sacs and explain why its structure suits its function.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have learned a two-part system for naming epithelia. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- Epithelia are classified by the number of cell layers and the shape of the surface cells.
- Simple epithelia have one layer for exchange; stratified epithelia have several for protection.
- Cell shapes are squamous, cuboidal, and columnar, each suited to particular functions.
- Combining the two descriptors names the tissue and predicts its function.
- Pseudostratified and transitional epithelia are special arrangements worth recognising.
