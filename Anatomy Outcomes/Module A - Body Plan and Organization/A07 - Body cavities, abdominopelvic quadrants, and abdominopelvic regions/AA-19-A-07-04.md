## Identify and describe the location of the nine abdominopelvic regions

# The Nine Abdominopelvic Regions

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Identify and describe the location of the nine abdominopelvic regions.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

The nine-region scheme is the finer-grained partner of the four-quadrant scheme, giving you a more precise map of the abdomen for detailed description. Being able to draw its four lines and name each of the nine regions lets you localise a finding with greater accuracy than quadrants allow. Today we build the grid, name the regions, and place the major structures within them.

- By the end of this session you will be able to define the four lines that create the nine regions.
- You will be able to name all nine regions in their correct positions.
- You will be able to locate the major structures found in each region.
- You will be able to choose between the nine-region and four-quadrant schemes appropriately.

---

## The Big Idea: Finer Division Gives Finer Precision

The organising idea is that adding more dividing lines produces smaller named areas, and smaller areas allow more precise localisation. The nine-region scheme is the same principle as the quadrants taken further, trading a little speed for considerably more anatomical precision. It is the scheme of choice when you need to describe a finding exactly rather than quickly.

- Four lines instead of two divide the abdomen into nine regions rather than four quadrants.
- Smaller regions allow more precise localisation of a structure or symptom.
- The scheme trades the speed of the quadrants for greater anatomical precision.
- It is preferred for detailed description, while quadrants suit rapid communication.

---

## Our Master Analogy: A Nine-Pane Window

To keep the layout concrete, picture a traditional nine-pane window, a three-by-three grid of glass panes held by two vertical and two horizontal bars. Each pane has a fixed position described by its row and column, such as top-left or centre, and anyone can name a pane instantly. The nine abdominopelvic regions are exactly this grid laid over the abdomen, with two vertical and two horizontal lines creating nine panes. Hold this window in mind as we name each pane.

- The window's two vertical and two horizontal bars mirror the four dividing lines of the scheme.
- Each of the nine panes has a fixed position, mirroring the nine named regions.
- Naming a pane by its row and column mirrors naming a region by its level and side.
- The analogy breaks down in that a window is flat and evenly spaced, whereas the abdominal lines follow bony and organ landmarks and the regions overlie layered organs of differing depth.

---

## Setting Up the Four Dividing Lines

Before naming the regions, define the grid, because the nine regions are simply the spaces it creates. Two vertical and two horizontal lines produce the three-by-three arrangement.

- Two vertical lines, the midclavicular lines, run downward from the midpoint of each clavicle.
- One horizontal line, the subcostal line, runs across the abdomen at the lower margin of the ribs.
- A second horizontal line, the transtubercular line, runs across at the level of the tubercles of the iliac crests.
- These four lines create a three-by-three grid of nine regions, three across in each of three rows.

---

## The Upper Row of Regions

Take the grid row by row, starting at the top, above the subcostal line. This upper row overlies the organs just beneath the ribs.

- The right hypochondriac region is the upper right, overlying much of the liver and the gallbladder.
- The epigastric region is the upper middle, overlying the stomach, the pancreas, and part of the liver.
- The left hypochondriac region is the upper left, overlying the spleen and part of the stomach.
- The word hypochondriac here means beneath the cartilage of the ribs, describing the position rather than any emotional state.

---

## The Middle Row of Regions

The middle row lies between the subcostal and transtubercular lines and is centred on the umbilicus. It overlies the mobile mid-gut and the kidneys behind it.

- The right lumbar region is the middle right, overlying the ascending colon and the right kidney.
- The umbilical region is the centre, overlying the small intestine and the transverse colon around the navel.
- The left lumbar region is the middle left, overlying the descending colon and the left kidney.
- The lumbar regions here refer to the flanks at the sides of the abdomen.

---

## The Lower Row of Regions

The lower row lies below the transtubercular line, overlying the pelvic organs and the lower intestine. These regions descend toward the groin.

- The right iliac, or inguinal, region is the lower right, overlying the appendix and the caecum.
- The hypogastric, or pubic, region is the lower middle, overlying the urinary bladder, the sigmoid colon, and, in the female, the uterus.
- The left iliac, or inguinal, region is the lower left, overlying the sigmoid colon.
- The iliac regions are named for the nearby iliac bones of the pelvis.

---

## Why the Nine Regions Matter in Physiotherapy Practice

The nine-region scheme is useful whenever precise description of an abdominal finding matters, and it sharpens both your reasoning and your communication. Each point below shows the finer grid at work.

- Describing pain as epigastric rather than simply upper abdominal points more specifically toward the stomach or pancreas.
- Localising a finding to the right or left lumbar region helps you consider the kidney lying behind that flank.
- Recognising hypogastric symptoms directs attention to the bladder and, in pelvic-health practice, the pelvic organs.
- The precise regional language matches how medical colleagues document, improving the quality of your referrals.
- Choosing the nine-region scheme when detail matters, and the quadrants when speed matters, makes your communication fit the situation.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors undermine correct use of the scheme, and naming them keeps it reliable. When unsure, redraw the nine-pane window using the four landmark lines.

- It is a mistake to think hypochondriac refers to anxiety, because here it means beneath the rib cartilage.
- It is a mistake to swap the region names between rows, because each name belongs to a specific level of the grid.
- It is a mistake to treat a region as naming a single organ, because each overlies several structures at differing depths.
- It is a mistake to use the nine-region and four-quadrant schemes interchangeably, because they use different lines and serve different purposes.

---

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Drawing the nine-pane window and naming its contents aloud is the strongest test.

- Define the four lines that create the nine regions from memory.
- Name all nine regions in their correct grid positions.
- List the major structures in the epigastric, umbilical, and hypogastric regions.
- State which regions overlie the kidneys and explain why.
- Explain when you would choose the nine-region scheme over the four-quadrant scheme.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have built a finer three-by-three grid and placed the major organs within it. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- Two midclavicular lines and the subcostal and transtubercular lines divide the abdomen into nine regions.
- The upper row is the right hypochondriac, epigastric, and left hypochondriac regions.
- The middle row is the right lumbar, umbilical, and left lumbar regions.
- The lower row is the right iliac, hypogastric, and left iliac regions.
- The nine-region scheme offers greater precision than the quadrants and is preferred when detailed description matters.
