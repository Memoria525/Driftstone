## Describe each body system’s major components

# The Major Components of Each Body System

A lecture for masters-level physiotherapy students

Learning objective: Describe each body system's major components.

---

## Where We Are Going Today

Knowing the names of the eleven systems is only the first step; the working knowledge comes from knowing what each system is actually built from. This objective asks you to open each department and describe the main organs and structures inside it, so that a system name immediately calls to mind its contents. That inventory is what lets you connect a specific organ to the larger function it serves.

- By the end of this session you will be able to describe the major components of each of the eleven systems.
- You will be able to name the principal organs and structures that make up each system.
- You will be able to explain how a system's components fit its overall role.
- You will be able to link the components of the movement-related systems to physiotherapy practice.

---

## The Big Idea: A System Is Defined by Its Component Parts

The organising idea is that each organ system is a specific assembly of organs and structures, and it is these components working together that produce the system's function. Naming the components turns an abstract label into a concrete piece of machinery you can reason about. Because the parts are chosen to serve the whole, the inventory of a system usually reveals the logic of what it does.

- Each system is a defined assembly of major organs and supporting structures.
- The components are what physically carry out the system's function.
- Listing the parts makes an abstract system name concrete and usable.
- The choice of components reflects the job the system performs, so the inventory is informative.

---

## Our Master Analogy: The Departments of an Airliner

To keep the inventories concrete, think of a large airliner, which is kept flying by distinct onboard systems, each made of specific components. The hydraulics have pumps, lines, and actuators; the electrical system has generators, wiring, and batteries; the fuel system has tanks, pumps, and pipes. You cannot understand a system without opening it and naming its parts, and the same is true of the body. Hold this airliner in mind, because describing a body system means listing the components inside its panel.

- Each aircraft system is a named assembly of specific components, mirroring an organ system.
- Understanding a system means opening it and naming its parts, just as with the body.
- The components are chosen to serve the system's job, as the body's organs are matched to their system's function.
- The analogy breaks down in that aircraft components are manufactured separately and bolted together, whereas the body's components grow together and often serve more than one system at once.

---

## Components of the Structural Systems

Begin with the systems that build and move the body, because their components are the ones you work with most directly. These are the frame, covering, and motors of the body.

- The integumentary system comprises the skin, along with the hair, nails, and cutaneous glands.
- The skeletal system comprises the bones, the cartilage, the ligaments, and the joints that connect them.
- The muscular system comprises the skeletal muscles, along with the tendons that attach them to bone.

---

## Components of the Control Systems

Next describe the components of the two coordinating systems, one wired and one chemical. Their parts explain how each achieves its style of control.

- The nervous system comprises the brain, the spinal cord, the peripheral nerves, and the sensory receptors.
- The endocrine system comprises the hormone-secreting glands, including the pituitary, thyroid, adrenal, and pancreas, among others.
- The endocrine glands are scattered rather than connected, which fits their use of the bloodstream to carry chemical messages.

---

## Components of the Transport and Defence Systems

These systems are built from vessels and the organs that service them, corresponding to the aircraft's plumbing and its protective circuits. Their components move and guard the body's internal fluids.

- The cardiovascular system comprises the heart, the arteries, the veins, the capillaries, and the blood they carry.
- The lymphatic system comprises the lymphatic vessels, the lymph nodes, the spleen, the thymus, and the tonsils.
- The lymphatic organs house the immune cells, which fits the system's role in defence as well as fluid drainage.

---

## Components of the Maintenance and Exchange Systems

The remaining systems are built from tubes, chambers, and glands that bring in, process, and expel materials. Their components reveal the pathway each substance follows through the body.

- The respiratory system comprises the nose, pharynx, larynx, trachea, bronchi, and lungs.
- The digestive system comprises the mouth, oesophagus, stomach, and intestines, with accessory organs including the liver, gallbladder, and pancreas.
- The urinary system comprises the kidneys, the ureters, the urinary bladder, and the urethra.
- The reproductive system comprises the gonads, the ovaries or testes, along with the ducts and associated glands and structures.

---

## Why Knowing the Components Matters in Physiotherapy Practice

Describing components is not an inventory for its own sake, because knowing which organ belongs to which system is what lets you localise problems and reason about them. Each point below shows the inventories at work.

- Knowing that tendons belong to the muscular system and ligaments to the skeletal system sharpens how you describe and treat soft-tissue injury.
- Recognising the components of the nervous system, from cord to peripheral nerve to receptor, lets you localise a neurological deficit along that chain.
- Knowing the respiratory components in sequence underpins airway clearance and breathing retraining.
- Understanding that the pancreas serves both digestive and endocrine roles reminds you that some components belong to more than one system.
- Being able to name the components lets you communicate precisely with medical colleagues about which structure is involved.

---

## Common Misconceptions to Discard

A few errors distort these inventories, and naming them keeps them accurate. When unsure, open the relevant department and list its parts one by one.

- It is a mistake to assign tendons and ligaments to the same system, because tendons are muscular components and ligaments are skeletal components.
- It is a mistake to think each organ belongs to only one system, because organs such as the pancreas serve two systems at once.
- It is a mistake to forget the supporting structures, because ligaments, tendons, and vessels are components just as much as the headline organs.
- It is a mistake to picture the endocrine glands as connected like the digestive tract, because they are separate glands linked only through the bloodstream.

---

## Consolidation and Self-Check

Because retrieval consolidates learning, close the slides and attempt these prompts before checking. Reciting a system and then naming its parts is the strongest test.

- For each of the eleven systems, name its major components from memory.
- State which system the tendons belong to and which system the ligaments belong to.
- List the components of the respiratory system in the order air passes through them.
- Name an organ that belongs to two systems and state which two.
- Trace the components of the urinary system from where urine is formed to where it leaves the body.

---

## Key Takeaways

We have opened each system and inventoried its parts, turning names into concrete machinery. If you keep only a few points, keep these.

- Each organ system is a defined assembly of major organs and supporting structures.
- The structural systems are built from skin, bones and joints, and muscles with their tendons.
- The control systems are built from the brain, cord, nerves, and receptors, and from the scattered endocrine glands.
- The transport, defence, and exchange systems are built from vessels, chambers, tubes, and glands matched to their functions.
- Some components, such as the pancreas, serve more than one system, so an organ's system membership is not always exclusive.
